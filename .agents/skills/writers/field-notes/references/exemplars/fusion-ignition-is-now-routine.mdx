The National Ignition Facility has now achieved fusion ignition ten times, per LLNL's own running tally, and nobody wrote a headline about the tenth. The first, in December 2022, was a global event — the moment a fusion reaction released more energy than the laser delivered to the fuel. The tenth, on 1 October 2025, was a Tuesday.

That silence is the actual milestone. Ignition was supposed to be the hard, maybe-impossible thing; three years on it's a scheduled experiment with a target-gain number attached. Read across the shots and the same quiet fact keeps surfacing: NIF isn't just repeating the 2022 result, it's climbing. December 2022 returned 3.15 MJ from 2.05 MJ in — a gain of about 1.5. The 7 April 2025 shot returned <Annotation client:load term="8.6 MJ from 2.08 MJ delivered" note="LLNL's record shot, 7 April 2025: target gain 4.13, versus ~1.5 for the first ignition in December 2022. The jump came from a redesigned high-density-carbon (diamond) capsule with a doped inner layer for better compression symmetry — not more laser energy." /> — a target gain of 4.13, roughly *triple* the first ignition's efficiency from essentially the same laser input.

That last clause is the whole point, and it's easy to miss. The gains aren't coming from a bigger machine. They're coming from the target — capsule geometry, dopants, compression symmetry — which means the frontier now is manufacturing and control, not raw power. That's the boring, industrializable kind of progress, and it's exactly what a lab needs before "ignition" becomes "power plant."

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="Watch for">
The metric that matters next isn't peak yield — it's *cadence*. NIF fires roughly a shot a day and takes hours to reset; a plant needs several per second. Watch whether the next record is a repetition-rate or target-fabrication result rather than another single-shot yield. The day the headline is "1 Hz," not "record gain," is the day inertial fusion energy stops being physics and starts being engineering.
</Callout>
