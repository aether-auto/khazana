It is a little after 07:00 on 19 November 1942, and along the frozen upper Don the fog is so thick that the Soviet gunners cannot see the Romanian trenches they are about to erase. General Nikolai Vatutin's Southwestern Front has spent the night moving
<Annotation client:load term="thousands of guns and Katyusha launchers" note="The Uranus artillery preparation on 19 November opened at 07:20 (Moscow time) with a mass bombardment; the day is still marked as Russia's Day of Rocket Forces and Artillery. — Operation Uranus, Wikipedia." />
into the snow opposite the sector the Wehrmacht has left to its weakest ally. The German Sixth Army — the finest army Germany has — is eighty kilometres to the east, bled white in the rubble of Stalingrad, and it is not the target. The target is the Third Romanian Army in front of Vatutin: overstretched, under-gunned, and holding a line it was never strong enough to hold.

At 07:20 the bombardment opens. For eighty minutes the shells fall on men who have no answer to them. And when the barrage lifts and the tanks come out of the fog, the trap that will swallow a quarter of a million Germans begins, not in the city everyone is watching, but on its forgotten flank.

The Germans have been looking the wrong way for three months.

To understand why the trap could close, you have to see what the summer had done to both sides. In June 1942, Hitler launched
<Annotation client:load term="Case Blue" note="Fall Blau (Case Blue), the 1942 summer offensive, split toward the Caucasus oilfields (Army Group A) and toward Stalingrad on the Volga (Army Group B). The overextension of a single-thrust offensive into two divergent axes is the structural error that left the flanks thin. — World History Encyclopedia; Battle of Stalingrad, Wikipedia." />,
the summer offensive that was meant to finish the Soviet Union — and instead split the German effort in two. One arm reached south for the Caucasus oil. The other, Army Group B, drove east for the industrial city on the Volga that carried Stalin's name. By September the Sixth Army under
<Annotation client:load term="General Friedrich Paulus" note="Friedrich Paulus commanded the German Sixth Army at Stalingrad. A capable staff officer, he had never before held independent field command of an army in a battle of this scale. — Battle of Stalingrad, Wikipedia; Britannica timeline." />
was inside Stalingrad, and the character of the war changed with the ground.

Stalingrad was not a battle the Sixth Army could win with the things that had made the Wehrmacht terrifying — the sweep of armour across open country, the encirclement in the field. It was a
<Definition client:visible term="Rattenkrieg" def="'War of the rats' — the German soldiers' name for the close-quarters urban fighting at Stalingrad, where the front ran through individual buildings, cellars, and sewers, and gains were measured in rooms rather than kilometres." />,
a war of rats, fought room by room and floor by floor for factories and grain elevators, against Vasily Chuikov's 62nd Army, which had orders to hold the west bank of the Volga at any cost and clung to a strip of the city sometimes only a few hundred metres deep. Every division Paulus fed into the city was a division that stopped manoeuvring and started grinding. By November the Sixth Army was
<Annotation client:load term="fixed in place" note="'Fixing' an enemy — committing his forces so completely to one point that they cannot redeploy to meet a threat elsewhere — is precisely what the city battle did to the Sixth Army. The best German formations were consumed street-fighting while the flanks were left to weaker allied armies. — World History Encyclopedia; Battle of Stalingrad, Wikipedia." />:
its best infantry and its assault engineers were committed to the rubble, and to hold the long flanks stretching back to the Don, the German command had nothing left but its allies.

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="The fatal geometry">
The city fighting did two things at once, and both were traps. It consumed the German army that could have manoeuvred — and it forced that army to hand the flanks to the Romanian, Italian, and Hungarian armies, who were never equipped to hold them. The Soviets did not have to beat the Sixth Army to destroy it. They had only to break what stood beside it.
</Callout>

North-west of the city, along the great bend of the Don, the line was held by the
<Annotation client:load term="Third Romanian Army" note="At the start of the Soviet November offensive the Third Romanian Army under Gen. Petre Dumitrescu held a front of roughly 138 km (86 mi) with a nominal strength of 152,492 Romanian troops and 11,211 attached German troops. — Third Army (Romania), Wikipedia." />
under General Petre Dumitrescu — a hundred and fifty thousand men strung across
<Annotation client:load term="a 138-kilometre front" note="The Third Romanian Army defended a front of about 138 km (86 mi) — a frontage far beyond what its strength and firepower could cover in depth. Wikipedia's Uranus article gives a comparable ~140 km figure. — Third Army (Romania), Wikipedia; Operation Uranus, Wikipedia." />,
a frontage that would strain a full-strength German army and was suicidal for an under-equipped one. South of the city, the Fourth Romanian Army held a line
<Annotation client:load term="no less than 270 kilometres long" note="The Fourth Romanian Army south of Stalingrad protected a line of some 270 km (170 mi) — even more dangerously overextended than the Third Army's sector. — Operation Uranus, Wikipedia." />.
Further up the Don sat the Italian Eighth and Hungarian Second Armies. All of them were thin, and all of them knew it.

While the Sixth Army starved for ammunition in the factories, the Soviet high command was doing exactly what the German command was not: looking at the map instead of the city.
<Annotation client:load term="Zhukov and Vasilevsky" note="Georgy Zhukov and Aleksandr Vasilevsky planned Operation Uranus for the Stavka, choosing not to reinforce the city fight directly but to mass reserves against the weak Axis flanks and encircle the Sixth Army. — Operation Uranus, Wikipedia; Britannica timeline." />
Georgy Zhukov and Aleksandr Vasilevsky had stopped feeding the meat-grinder in the city with anything more than the minimum required to keep Chuikov alive. Everything else — every fresh tank corps, every rebuilt rifle army — was going somewhere the Germans were not looking: into assembly areas behind the Don and behind the Volga, opposite the Romanians.

Read the two rosters against each other and the outcome is already visible. On the Soviet side, the striking power is
<Annotation client:load term="concentrated in mechanized and tank corps" note="The Soviet exploitation forces were the 4th Tank Corps and 4th Mechanized Corps (with the 13th Mechanized Corps), plus the 5th Tank Army under Romanenko — the mobile spearheads that turned a breakthrough into an encirclement. — Operation Uranus, Wikipedia." />
— the 5th Tank Army, the 4th Tank Corps, the 4th and 13th Mechanized Corps — formations built to break through a thin line and then run, deep and fast, into the enemy's rear. On the Axis side, the mass is the Sixth Army, and the Sixth Army is a hundred kilometres away with its hands full. What stands between the Soviet tank corps and the German rear is a Romanian infantry line with no reserve, no armour that matters, and no anti-tank gun that works.

<Callout kind="warning" title="What Paulus's mobile reserve could not do">
The German answer to a breakthrough was the panzer counterattack. But the 48th Panzer Corps, the reserve behind the Romanian sector, could field <strong>fewer than 100 serviceable modern tanks</strong>; its 22nd Panzer Division went into action with fewer than thirty running — many immobilized because mice had chewed through wiring in machines left dug-in and idle. The one force that might have sealed a breach could barely move.
</Callout>

## Uranus: The Trap Closes

Here is the operation itself, over the ground it was fought on: the Don sweeping down from the north-west through Kalach, the Volga on the right with Stalingrad on its western bank, and the two Romanian flank sectors — north and south — where the pincers went in. Step the scrubber phase by phase. Each phase is one move in a plan that took four days to encircle an army it never had to fight.

### Phase 1 — The army that could not turn

Look first at the map before the offensive, and the disaster is already latent in it. The Sixth Army sits jammed against the Volga on the right, its front bent around the city, every good division committed to the
<Annotation client:load term="street fighting" note="By November the German assault on Stalingrad had ground down to close urban combat for landmarks like the Barrikady and Krasny Oktyabr factories; the Sixth Army's mobility and reserves were consumed holding and attacking in the city. — World History Encyclopedia; Britannica timeline." />.
The long line running back to the Don — the army's lifeline and its blind side — is held not by Germans but by the two Romanian armies, drawn as thin lines across an enormous frontage. There is no operational reserve worth the name behind them. An army fixed to a river by its own offensive cannot pivot to defend its rear; that is what "fixed" means, and it is the precondition for everything that follows.

The German command knew the flanks were weak.
<Annotation client:load term="Romanian generals had warned of the danger" note="Romanian commanders, including Dumitrescu, repeatedly warned the German command that their sectors were overextended and under-equipped and requested reserves and better anti-tank weapons before the offensive; the warnings went substantially unheeded. — Third Army (Romania), Wikipedia; Operation Uranus, Wikipedia." />
Dumitrescu had asked for reserves and for anti-tank guns that could stop a T-34. What he got was a promise of a distant panzer corps and the instruction to hold. Intelligence of a Soviet build-up was reaching Army Group B through November; it was discounted, because the German command could not imagine the Red Army — supposedly at the end of its strength — massing a million men for a stroke of this scale. It was looking at the city, where the battle was, and not at the Don, where the battle was about to be.

### Phase 2 — The northern pincer

The bombardment on 19 November is the loudest sound of the war on that front to date, and when it lifts, the T-34s come down off the
<Annotation client:load term="Serafimovich and Kletskaya bridgeheads" note="The Southwestern Front attacked from bridgeheads over the Don at Serafimovich and Kletskaya that the Soviets had carefully held and reinforced through the autumn precisely for this purpose. — Operation Uranus, Wikipedia; Britannica timeline." />
— footholds over the Don the Soviets had held all autumn for exactly this hour. The Third Romanian Army, hit by
<Annotation client:load term="the 5th Tank Army and 21st Army" note="The Southwestern Front's breakthrough echelon included the 5th Tank Army (Prokofy Romanenko) and the 21st Army; the mechanized and tank corps then exploited into the depth toward Kalach. — Operation Uranus, Wikipedia." />
with tanks it cannot kill, does not so much retreat as dissolve. Whole divisions are overrun in the first hours; others fight and are surrounded where they stand. By afternoon the Soviet armour is through the crust and into open country, driving south-east — not to roll up the Romanian line, but past it, toward a bridge.

The German 48th Panzer Corps, the reserve that was supposed to seal exactly this kind of breach, moves to counterattack and cannot. With
<Annotation client:load term="fewer than 100 serviceable tanks" note="The 48th Panzer Corps had fewer than 100 serviceable modern tanks; the 22nd Panzer Division committed fewer than thirty running machines, some disabled by damage from being left dug in. — Operation Uranus, Wikipedia." />
between its two divisions, and its 22nd Panzer Division barely able to field a company's worth of runners, it is swept aside. The single mechanism that might have stopped Uranus on the first day never functions.

### Phase 3 — The southern pincer

The Stavka has designed the trap as two arms, and the second closes a day after the first. On 20 November, south of the city, Yeremenko's Stalingrad Front opens its own bombardment and sends the
<Annotation client:load term="51st and 57th Armies" note="The Stalingrad Front's southern attack on 20 November led with the 51st and 57th Armies, with the 4th Mechanized and 13th Mechanized Corps as the exploitation force driving toward the Kalach link-up. — Operation Uranus, Wikipedia." />
into the Fourth Romanian Army — the softest sector on the map, a single line stretched across two hundred and seventy kilometres. It gives at once.

Now watch the two arrows on the map. One drives south-east from the Don bend; the other, spearheaded by
<Annotation client:load term="Volsky's 4th Mechanized Corps" note="The 4th Mechanized Corps under Vasily Volsky was the southern pincer's armored spearhead, driving north-west from below Stalingrad to meet the northern arm near Kalach. — Operation Uranus, Wikipedia." />,
drives north-west from below the city. Both are aimed at the same point — the crossing of the Don at Kalach, the one place a bridge carries the road into the German rear. Still fighting in the rubble to the east, the Sixth Army is now inside a closing bracket and does not yet grasp it, and behind its back the two arrows are converging.

<Callout kind="note" title="Why Kalach">
Kalach-on-the-Don sits at the neck of the great Don bend, where the river swings closest to Stalingrad. Whoever holds the Kalach bridge holds the door of the pocket. The Soviets aimed both pincers at it not for the town but for the crossing — close the door there, and everything east of the Don is sealed against the river at its back.
</Callout>

### Phase 4 — The link-up at Kalach

The Kalach bridge is taken before the ring even closes, and taken by a trick.
<Annotation client:load term="a captured bridge, seized by ruse" note="Elements of the 26th Tank Corps (Southwestern Front) seized the vital Don bridge near Kalach on 22–23 November by a coup de main: Lt. Col. Filippov's 19th Tank Brigade advanced at high speed with headlights on using captured German tanks and a reconnaissance vehicle to lead the approach, causing the guards to mistake the column for their own vehicles — then opened fire before the deception was understood. — Operation Uranus, Wikipedia (two captured German tanks and a reconnaissance vehicle); 26th Tank Corps accounts; World History Encyclopedia." />
A detachment of the 26th Tank Corps rolls up to the crossing at night with headlights blazing and captured German vehicles leading the column, looking for all the world like a German force returning to base; by the time the guards understand their mistake, the bridge is Soviet and intact. With the Don crossing in hand, the two arms have their meeting place.

On
<Annotation client:load term="23 November, at about 16:00, near Sovetsky" note="The pincers linked up near the village of Sovetsky, close to Kalach, on 23 November 1942 (~16:00): the 4th Tank Corps (Kravchenko) of the Southwestern Front closing on the 4th Mechanized Corps (Volsky) of the Stalingrad Front. Some accounts date the effective closure to late on 22 November. — Operation Uranus, Wikipedia; Warfare History Network; Britannica timeline." />,
the tank crews of Kravchenko's 4th Tank Corps and Volsky's 4th Mechanized Corps see each other across the snow near the village of Sovetsky and fire recognition flares. The ring is closed. In four days, without fighting the Sixth Army at all, the Red Army has put an army group's worth of the enemy inside a bag. The map shows what the German command spends the next day refusing to believe: a continuous Soviet front now runs behind Paulus, between him and the Don, and everything east of it is trapped.

<Pullquote cite="Adolf Hitler's order to Paulus, late November 1942 (as recorded in the campaign histories)" href="https://www.worldhistory.org/article/2696/battle-of-stalingrad/" kind="telegram">
Sixth Army will hold its positions to the last man and the last round. Fortress Stalingrad will be held.
</Pullquote>

The bag has a name the Germans give it themselves:
<Annotation client:load term="the Kessel" note="Kessel — 'cauldron' — the German soldiers' word for the encirclement at Stalingrad. The pocket measured roughly 50 km east–west by 40 km north–south. — Operation Uranus, Wikipedia; Battle of Stalingrad, Wikipedia." />,
the cauldron. Inside it, roughly
<Annotation client:load term="250,000 to 290,000 men" note="Estimates of the number encircled range from about 250,000 to 300,000; ~290,000 Axis personnel is a commonly cited figure for the moment of closure, with ~250,000 German soldiers the more conservative count. — the exact number is contested and always will be — some twenty German divisions, two Romanian divisions, the

and tens of thousands of Soviet auxiliaries are cut off in a space

This is the moment the battle could still have ended differently, and the choice belongs to one man. Paulus, a careful staff officer who has never disobeyed an order in his life, asks for freedom to break out to the south-west while the Soviet ring is still thin — while it is still a cordon and not yet a wall. Hitler refuses.
<Annotation client:load term="Hitler forbids a breakout" note="Hitler, encouraged by Göring's promise of air supply and by the concept of holding 'fortress' positions, forbade Paulus to break out and ordered the Sixth Army to hold and await relief. The Sixth Army will hold "Fortress Stalingrad," he decrees, and it will be supplied from the air until relief arrives. Both halves of that promise are about to prove false.

## The Promise from the Sky

The plan to keep the Sixth Army alive rests on a number, and the number was a fantasy from the first day.
<Annotation client:load term="Göring promised to supply the army by air" note="Reichsmarschall Hermann Göring assured Hitler the Luftwaffe could supply the encircled Sixth Army by air, against the professional judgment of his own transport and Fliegerkorps commanders — Gen. Martin Fiebig (VIII Fliegerkorps) among those who warned it was impossible. — The Stalingrad Airlift, MilitaryHistoryNow.com; Turning Point at Stalingrad, Air & Space Forces Magazine." />
Reichsmarschall Göring told Hitler the Luftwaffe could feed and arm a quarter of a million men from the air. His own transport commanders told him it could not. He promised anyway.

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="The arithmetic of starvation">
Supply need ran in tiers: roughly <strong>300 tons a day</strong> for bare survival, near <strong>500 tons</strong> to keep the army operational — the figure Paulus's staff (Zeitzler) pressed on the high command — and <strong>700–800 tons</strong> to fight effectively. Across the whole airlift the Luftwaffe averaged around <strong>100 tons a day</strong> — roughly a fifth of the operational minimum. An army was being asked to live on a fifth of what it needed, in a Russian winter, and the difference was made up in frozen and starving men.
</Callout>

The figures are not close.
<Annotation client:load term="≈500 tons needed, ≈100 delivered" note="Requirement cited in tiers: ~300 t/day for survival, ~500 t operational, ~700–800 t to fight effectively. Actual average deliveries were on the order of ~116 tons/day (commonly rounded to 'roughly 100') — about a fifth of the operational minimum. Both the requirement tiers and the shortfall are corroborated across sources. — The Stalingrad Airlift, MilitaryHistoryNow.com; Battle of Stalingrad, Wikipedia; Turning Point at Stalingrad, Air & Space Forces Magazine." />
The transport fleet — Ju 52 trimotors and He 111 bombers pressed into the cargo role — flew into two airfields inside the pocket,
<Annotation client:load term="Pitomnik and Gumrak" note="Pitomnik was the main supply airfield inside the Kessel; Gumrak was the secondary. Their loss — Pitomnik on 16 January 1943, Gumrak on the night of 21/22 January — ended effective air supply and the evacuation of the wounded. — Pitomnik Airfield, Wikipedia; Battle of Stalingrad, Wikipedia." />,
through blizzards and a thickening Soviet fighter and flak screen, and they were shot out of the sky in numbers the Luftwaffe could not sustain.

<Detail summary="Go deeper: the cost of the airlift to the Luftwaffe">
Across the Stalingrad airlift the Luftwaffe lost on the order of <strong>~488 aircraft</strong> and roughly a thousand aircrew, the losses falling most heavily on the transport arm: by the commonly cited breakdown, some <strong>266 Ju 52s, 165 He 111s, 42 Ju 86s</strong>, and a handful of four-engined types (Fw 200s, He 177s, a Ju 290). The transport fleet's destruction over Stalingrad crippled the Luftwaffe's air-transport capacity for the rest of the war — a strategic loss that outlasted the battle. Figures vary by source and by whether combat and operational losses are combined; the ~488 total and the ~266 Ju 52 figure are the widely cited ones. — The Stalingrad Airlift, MilitaryHistoryNow.com; Battle of Stalingrad, Wikipedia.
</Detail>

The airlift did carry something out. In the weeks it functioned, transports flew perhaps
<Annotation client:load term="between 25,000 and 42,000 wounded and specialists out of the pocket" note="Estimates of those flown out of the Kessel — sick and wounded, plus irreplaceable specialists — range from about 24,900 to 42,000 before the airfields were lost. The figure is reported as a range. — Battle of Stalingrad, Wikipedia; World History Encyclopedia." />
— sick, wounded, and irreplaceable technicians — before the airfields were overrun. For the men left behind, the arithmetic only worsened: rations were cut, and cut again, and the winter did the rest.

## The Relief That Fell Short

Relief was the other half of Hitler's promise, and it came closer to working than the airlift ever did. To break the ring from outside, Hitler gave the job to his most capable field commander,
<Annotation client:load term="Field Marshal Erich von Manstein" note="Erich von Manstein commanded the newly formed Army Group Don, charged with relieving the Sixth Army. Manstein is widely regarded as the ablest German operational commander of the war. — Operation Winter Storm, Wikipedia; Warfare History Network." />,
at the head of the newly formed Army Group Don. His relief thrust,
<Annotation client:load term="Operation Winter Storm (Wintergewitter)" note="Operation Wintergewitter ('Winter Storm') opened on 12 December 1942, driving from the Kotelnikovo area south-west of the pocket toward the Sixth Army. — Operation Winter Storm, Wikipedia; Warfare History Network." />,
jumped off on 12 December from the area of Kotelnikovo, south-west of the pocket.

The spearhead was the
<Annotation client:load term="LVII Panzer Corps under Kirchner" note="The relief spearhead was the LVII Panzer Corps (Gen. Friedrich Kirchner), built around the 6th and 23rd Panzer Divisions — the 6th freshly arrived from France at full strength — with the 17th Panzer Division following. The corps fielded on the order of 170 tanks. — Operation Winter Storm, Wikipedia; Warfare History Network." />
— the 6th and 23rd Panzer Divisions, roughly a hundred and seventy tanks, the 6th newly arrived from refit in France and at full strength. Against thin Soviet screens it moved fast:
<Annotation client:load term="up to 60 kilometres on the first day" note="The relief force advanced up to about 60 km (37 mi) on the first day, achieving surprise, before Soviet resistance and reinforcement (notably the 2nd Guards Army) stiffened. — Operation Winter Storm, Wikipedia." />
in the opening drive, brushing aside the forward positions, closing on the pocket while the Sixth Army listened to the guns growing louder to the south-west.

The cascade above is the death of the Sixth Army in four steps, and the cruelest is the first. By 19–20 December, Manstein's tanks crossed the
<Annotation client:load term="Myshkova river, within 48 kilometres of the pocket" note="By 19–20 December the 6th Panzer Division crossed the Myshkova (Mishkova) river and reached a point roughly 48 km (30 mi) from the Sixth Army's southern perimeter — the closest any relief came. — Operation Winter Storm, Wikipedia; Warfare History Network." />
— thirty miles from the perimeter, close enough that the trapped men could believe rescue was coming. But the Sixth Army had fuel in its tanks for only about
<Annotation client:load term="30 kilometres of movement" note="Sixth Army's armor had fuel for roughly 30 km of movement; a breakout to meet a relief force 48 km away was therefore impossible without air-delivered fuel that never arrived in quantity. Col. Wilhelm Adam's account records the 30-km fuel state. — Operation Winter Storm, Wikipedia." />.
To break out and reach Manstein, it would have had to cross forty-eight kilometres on thirty kilometres of fuel — and Hitler, in any case, had forbidden it to try.

Then the flank gave way again, farther up the Don. On 16 December the Soviets opened
<Annotation client:load term="Operation Little Saturn" note="Operation Little Saturn (16 December 1942) struck the Italian 8th Army on the upper Don, collapsing it and threatening Army Group Don's rear and the airlift's airfields — forcing Manstein to divert forces from the relief and, ultimately, to abandon Winter Storm. — Operation Winter Storm, Wikipedia; Warfare History Network." />,
a second offensive that smashed the Italian Eighth Army and drove for Manstein's own rear — the very airfields the airlift flew from. Now the relief force was itself in danger of being cut off. On
<Annotation client:load term="23 December Manstein called off the attack" note="Manstein halted Winter Storm on 23 December and withdrew toward Kotelnikovo, as the relief force was threatened with encirclement and the wider front was collapsing after Little Saturn. — Operation Winter Storm, Wikipedia; Warfare History Network." />
and pulled back toward Kotelnikovo. The nearest relief the Sixth Army would ever have turned around forty-eight kilometres short and drove away, and the sound of its guns faded into the south-west. Inside the Kessel, the men who had heard them understood what the silence meant.

<Pullquote cite="Erich von Manstein, on the closing of the window for a breakout (Lost Victories)" href="https://warfarehistorynetwork.com/article/stunning-victories-ultimate-defeat/" kind="quote">
The tragedy of the Sixth Army was that it was condemned to remain passive at the very moment when only boldness could have saved it.
</Pullquote>

## What the Trap Decided

Stalingrad is remembered as the turning point of the war in the East, and the phrase is earned, but it is worth being precise about *what* turned. It was not that Germany lost a battle; Germany had lost battles before. It was that Germany lost an entire field army — a quarter of a million men, twenty-odd divisions, the equipment of an army group — and lost it in a way that could not be explained away, in the middle of the winter, in front of the whole world. The
<Annotation client:load term="myth of German invincibility" note="Stalingrad ended the perception of German invincibility on the Eastern Front and is widely treated as the strategic turning point of the war against Germany; after it, the strategic initiative in the East passed permanently to the Soviet Union. — World History Encyclopedia; Britannica timeline." />
did not survive the Kessel. After Stalingrad, the initiative in the East belonged to the Red Army and never came back to the Wehrmacht.

And the *manner* of the victory mattered as much as the fact of it. Uranus was not a frontal triumph of weight over weight. It was a design — the same design, at operational scale, that Hannibal had drawn at Cannae and Schlieffen had dreamed of: let the enemy's strength commit itself to one point, then strike where he is weak, and close the ring behind him. The Soviets did not have to defeat the best army in the world in the rubble of Stalingrad. They had only to recognize that the best army in the world had pinned itself in place, and to hit the Romanians beside it. Everything after — the sealed Kessel, the airlift that could not, the relief that fell short, the surrender — followed from four days in November on the flanks of a battle everyone else was watching in the city.

The Sixth Army spent the autumn taking a city and lost an army doing it. The Red Army spent the autumn ignoring the city and won a war. The trap did not close in Stalingrad. It closed a hundred kilometres away, on a frozen river, in a fog thick enough to hide a million men — and once it closed, it could not be opened again.
