It is a little after seven in the evening on 10 April 1815, and the Rajah of Sanggar is standing on the north shore of Sumbawa, watching his mountain come apart. For five days it has muttered — a sound English officers at Yogyakarta mistook for cannon fire, dispatching troops to relieve a post that was never under attack. Now the muttering stops being a sound and becomes a thing you can see. Three columns of fire rise from the summit of Tambora, separate at first, and then, high in the dark, their tops lean together and merge into one.

<Pullquote cite="Lt. Owen Phillips, on the account of the Rajah of Sanggar, collected for Sir Stamford Raffles, 1815" href="https://volcanicdegassing.wordpress.com/2015/04/03/the-great-eruption-of-tambora-april-1815/" kind="document">
About seven P.M. on the 10th of April, three distinct columns of flame burst forth near the top of Tomboro mountain… and after ascending separately to a very great height, their caps united in the air. In a short time the whole mountain next Sangar appeared like a body of liquid fire, extending itself in every direction.
</Pullquote>

The Rajah has no name for what he is seeing. It is the largest volcanic eruption in recorded history — a <Annotation client:load term="VEI 7" note="Volcanic Explosivity Index 7 — the highest confirmed magnitude of any eruption in the historical record. Tambora ejected an estimated 37-45 cubic kilometres of magma (dense-rock equivalent), roughly four to ten times the energy of Krakatoa in 1883. 1815 eruption of Mount Tambora, Wikipedia; Mount Tambora, Encyclopaedia Britannica." />, on a scale nothing in living human memory approached. That fact alone would make Tambora a chapter in a geology textbook. What makes it a chapter in the history of the entire world is a number nobody agrees on: how much sulfur the eruption threw into the stratosphere. Scientists using three different methods — melting-inclusion chemistry in the erupted rock, the sulfate frozen into polar ice cores, and the optical density of the atmosphere it left behind — have never converged on one figure. Their estimates span more than an order of magnitude. That argument, still running in the journals two centuries later, is the real spine of this story, because the size of the veil is the size of the catastrophe it caused, and getting it right — or admitting when we can't — is the difference between history and folklore.

## A mountain, and an argument about smoke

Before that evening, Tambora was a peak, not a story. It rose roughly <Annotation client:load term="four thousand three hundred metres" note="Pre-eruption height estimated at ~4,300 m (14,100 ft) — one of the tallest peaks in the Indonesian archipelago. Mount Tambora, Wikipedia; Encyclopaedia Britannica." /> above the Java Sea, green to its shoulders, home to a people whose language — recorded by a single passing traveller's word-list before the eruption erased them — linguists now classify as a probable isolate, related to nothing else documented in the region. When the eruption ended, the mountain stood <Annotation client:load term="two thousand eight hundred and fifty-one metres" note="Post-eruption summit: 2,851 m (9,354 ft). The eruption removed roughly 1,450 m — about a third of the mountain's height — and left a caldera some 6-7 km across and 600-700 m deep. Mount Tambora, Wikipedia." /> — a third of it, and the people who lived on it, simply gone.

The eruption column climbed past <Annotation client:load term="forty-three kilometres" note="The 10 April climax reached an eruption column of roughly 43-45 km, well above the ~12 km ceiling of ordinary weather. 1815 eruption of Mount Tambora, Wikipedia; Encyclopaedia Britannica gives 43.5 km." /> — the fact that will, a year later, kill crops in New Hampshire, because ash and gas thrown that high do not fall out of the sky in days. They cross into the stratosphere, above the rain that would otherwise wash them out, and there they spread. Detonations on 10 April were heard clearly at Trumon, Sumatra, more than two and a half thousand kilometres distant<Sidenote n={1} href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1815_eruption_of_Mount_Tambora" source="1815 eruption of Mount Tambora, Wikipedia">Some accounts push the outer bound further still — possible detonations reported as far as Thailand, over 3,000 km away. The garrisons that heard the closer blasts at Yogyakarta assumed they were under artillery attack and marched troops out to meet an enemy that did not exist.</Sidenote> — and officers along the coast mobilized for a battle that was, in fact, a mountain. Everyone who felt Tambora reached for the wrong explanation, because the right one was unthinkable. It is a small, exact preview of the entire next two years: an enormous, real cause, and a world of people experiencing only its disguised effects.

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="Tambora killed twice">
First it killed locally and by fire: pyroclastic flows, a displacement tsunami, and buried rice paddies on Sumbawa and Lombok in April-July 1815. Then, over the following two years, it killed at a planetary distance and by cold — a synchronized famine across three continents, caused not by lava but by an invisible sulfate veil the eruption hung in the stratosphere. The first toll is measured in the tens of thousands. The second is not really countable at all, because the deaths were too scattered, across too many countries, for any state at the time to add them into a single catastrophe.
</Callout>

On Sumbawa, the killing is immediate. The wind the Rajah felt was a pyroclastic surge — hot gas and rock outrunning any man — and it flattens Sanggar before anyone can understand what is happening. What the surge does not take, the sea does: the caldera's collapse displaces enough water to throw tsunamis across the Flores Sea, drowning villages the fire never reached. Then the ash falls, and the ash is the patient killer — deep enough on Sumbawa to crush roofs and bury the paddies that fed the island, and a paddy under ash grows nothing that season or the next. Weeks later, a British lieutenant named Owen Phillips, sent by Sir Stamford Raffles to carry relief rice, finds the survivors too weak to bury their own dead.

<Pullquote cite="Lt. Owen Phillips, relief mission to Sumbawa, 1815" href="https://volcanicdegassing.wordpress.com/2015/04/03/the-great-eruption-of-tambora-april-1815/" kind="document">
The extreme misery to which the inhabitants have been reduced is shocking to behold. There were still on the road side the remains of several corpses, and the marks of where many others had been interred: the villages almost entirely deserted and the houses fallen down, the surviving inhabitants having dispersed in search of food.
</Pullquote>

Nobody counted the local dead with precision, because no state on Sumbawa in 1815 kept that kind of ledger. The first real attempt came thirty-two years later, in 1847, when a Swiss botanist named Heinrich Zollinger became the first scientist to climb to the caldera rim and systematically collect survivor accounts. He put the direct dead — killed by the surges and ash — at roughly <Annotation client:load term="ten thousand" note="Zollinger's 1847 survey, the closest thing to a primary casualty count, estimated ~10,000 killed directly, plus a further ~37,825 dead of starvation on Sumbawa alone. Mount Tambora, Wikipedia." /> and the starvation dead on Sumbawa alone at roughly four times that. A 1998 revision by the volcanologists Tanguy and colleagues, working from the same fragmentary record, arrived at a similar shape — about 11,000 direct deaths and 49,000 indirect — and modern estimates for the eruption's total regional toll still span a wide range. The volcanologist Clive Oppenheimer puts the floor at <Annotation client:load term="at least seventy-one thousand" note="Clive Oppenheimer: 'there were at least 71,000 deaths in total' — the low end of the modern range. 1815 eruption of Mount Tambora, Wikipedia." /> deaths; the historian Reid, working independently, estimates <Annotation client:load term="around a hundred thousand" note="Reid: '100,000 people on Sumbawa, Bali and other locations died from the direct and indirect effects of the eruption' — a separate estimate from Oppenheimer's, and the actual figure behind Reid's name (not the 121,000 sometimes misattributed to him). 1815 eruption of Mount Tambora, Wikipedia." /> dead on Sumbawa, Bali, and other locations; and the page's own comparison table gives the widest tabulated range for the eruption as <Annotation client:load term="a hundred and twenty-one thousand" note="The article's comparison table lists Tambora's fatalities as '>71,000-121,000' — an unattributed range ceiling in that table, not a specific scholar's figure. 1815 eruption of Mount Tambora, Wikipedia." /> at its ceiling. The range is wide because the record is thin. That honesty — a range instead of a false decimal point — turns out to be the right posture for almost everything that follows.

## The number nobody agrees on

Here is the argument, and it matters more than any single casualty count, because it decides how big the *global* catastrophe was. How much sulfur did Tambora actually put into the stratosphere?

Notice that the rows above are not even all measuring the same thing — some report the mass of sulfur dioxide gas (SO₂), others the mass of elemental sulfur (S) it contains, roughly half the SO₂ figure by weight. That is not a trivial bookkeeping error; it is a real source of public confusion in how this eruption gets reported, and it is why a chronicle that wants to be trusted has to show the range instead of picking the biggest or roundest number. The 2023 reassessment by Manon Pouget and colleagues used the same basic method as the 2004 study — measuring how much sulfur a pocket of magma lost between being trapped in a crystal before the eruption and erupting into the open air — and, on updated instrumentation, found <Annotation client:load term="147 teragrams of sulfur dioxide, plus or minus 17" note="Pouget, Moussallam, Rose-Koga & Sigurdsson (2023), Bulletin of Volcanology. This figure is explicitly higher than earlier petrological and ice-core-derived estimates for Tambora, and the authors argue it is consistent with 1816 atmospheric optical-depth observations — meaning the sky itself, as recorded by contemporaries, may support the higher number." />, nearly three times the 2004 team's own figure. If their number holds, Tambora becomes the single largest volcanic sulfur release of the last two thousand years — larger even than the 1257 Samalas eruption on neighbouring Lombok, the previous record-holder.

Why can four serious research teams, using real data, land so far apart? A 2018 study answers that question directly, and it is the most important paper behind this whole piece precisely because it explains the *uncertainty itself* rather than adding one more point estimate to the pile. A team led by Lauren Marshall ran five state-of-the-art global aerosol models, forced each one with a simulated Tambora eruption, and checked what each model predicted should have been deposited in the Greenland and Antarctic ice cores — the same ice cores every other estimate ultimately calibrates against.

<Callout kind="note" title="Why the ice cores don't settle it">
Marshall and colleagues found that the ratio between how much sulfate aerosol sits in the atmosphere after an eruption and how much of it eventually gets deposited on the polar ice sheets <Annotation client:load term="varies by up to a factor of fifteen" note="Marshall, L. et al. (2018), Atmospheric Chemistry and Physics 18(3). Ice-core deposition ranged 19-264 kg/km² for Antarctica and 31-194 kg/km² for Greenland across five models, against a single ice-core-measured value of roughly 50 kg/km² for both. The gap between models is the paper's central, sobering finding." /> between different, individually credible atmospheric models. In other words: converting “sulfate found frozen in an ice core” into “sulfur mass that reached the stratosphere” is not a simple division problem. It depends on winds, chemistry, and deposition patterns that differ by up to 1,500% between equally serious simulations. The ice cores are real data. Turning them into a single global number is where the real uncertainty lives.
</Callout>

This is why the range in the table above is not a failure of research — it is the honest state of the science, and the version of the story this chronicle commits to. Whatever the true number, the mechanism nobody disputes is what happens next.

However much sulfur Tambora actually lofted, the mechanism is not in doubt: these droplets are small, bright, and long-lived, and they do one thing supremely well — reflect incoming sunlight back to space before it warms the ground.

    **The dimming.** Within months the veil spreads around the planet on the high winds — invisible from the ground except at dawn and dusk, when it turns the sky the colour of a bruise. Its measurable work: a global *annual mean* cooling of roughly <Annotation client:load term="0.4 to 0.7 °C" note="The commonly cited global annual mean cooling figure, averaging every latitude and season together. Year Without a Summer, Wikipedia; a 2012 Berkeley Earth land-temperature reanalysis cited on the same page put the drop nearer a full degree Celsius." />. A fraction of one degree — and it broke a hemisphere's harvest, because an annual global average hides everything that actually matters about a growing season.
  </ScrollyStep>

An average is a lie told about extremes, and this is the exact place to say so plainly. The 0.4-0.7 °C figure folds warm tropics, cool poles, and every season of the year into one smoothed number. Two independent 2023 studies — one using tree-ring growth records across the high-latitude Northern Hemisphere, the other reconstructing land temperatures specifically to study future catastrophic-eruption risk — arrive, by different methods, at the same much sharper number for the month that actually mattered: June 1816 land temperatures ran <Annotation client:load term="1.9 degrees Celsius below normal" note="Gao, Camarero, Babst & Liang (2023), Nature Communications: model simulations show global land surface temperature in June 1816 fell by −1.9 °C. Independently, Wilson et al. (2023), Scientific Reports, reconstruct −1.9 °C (±0.2 °C) global land cooling for 1816 by a different method — a rare case of two unrelated peer-reviewed methods converging on the same figure." />, nearly three times the size of the smoothed annual global mean. That is the number a Vermont cornfield actually experienced. A crop does not care what the planetary average was. It cares whether the temperature dropped below freezing while it was in the ground — and in 1816, in month after month, it did.

The strongest study in the whole ledger asked a harder question than “how cold was it” — it asked how much of that cold Tambora actually *caused*, versus ordinary bad luck in the weather. A 2019 event-attribution study, applying the same statistical methods now used to attribute individual modern heatwaves to climate change, found that normal atmospheric variability — the kind any year might bring — could explain <Annotation client:load term="only about a quarter" note="Schurer, Hegerl et al. (2019), Environmental Research Letters 14(9). Analogue summers matched on 1816's sea-level-pressure pattern, drawn from both observations and unforced climate-model runs, reproduced the precipitation anomaly with no external forcing, but explained only roughly a quarter of the anomalous cold." /> of 1816's cold anomaly in central Europe. Adding Tambora's volcanic forcing into the same climate models made a summer that cold <Annotation client:load term="up to a hundred times more likely" note="Schurer, Hegerl et al. (2019). Including Tambora's forcing in the climate models made the observed European cold anomaly up to 100 times more likely than in unforced runs, and the wet anomaly 1.5-3 times as likely — the strongest causal, not merely correlational, claim in the literature this chronicle draws on." /> to occur than it would otherwise have been, and the accompanying wet anomaly one-and-a-half to three times as likely. The mountain did not merely coincide with the disaster. The climate models say it manufactured it.

The cascade above is the whole scientific argument in five steps. What makes 1816 different from any ordinary bad year is the final node: the failure was *synchronized*. A June snowstorm in Vermont and a monsoon failure in Bengal and a rotting potato crop in Ireland were, for once, the same event — the same veil, thrown by the same disputed mass of sulfur, dimming the same sun over all of them at once.

It is 6 June 1816 in northern New England, and it is snowing — not a flurry, but <Annotation client:load term="six inches" note="On 6 June 1816, roughly six inches of snow fell across parts of New England; by 8 June the snow lay 18 inches deep at Cabot, Vermont. New England Historical Society; Year Without a Summer, Wikipedia." /> of it, drifting to eighteen at Cabot, Vermont, in the second week of June. Picture the wrongness of it from inside a Vermont farmhouse: the corn is in the ground, the sheep are shorn for summer, and the entire rhythm of the year assumes June is warm. Farmers who had already sheared their flocks try to tie the fleeces back onto shivering animals to keep them alive; some freeze anyway, half-dressed in their own wool. In Plymouth, Connecticut, a young clockmaker named Chauncey Jerome walks to work that week in an overcoat and mittens, and on the Fourth of July, watching men play quoits in their coats, he cannot feel patriotic.

The volatility is the part that made 1816 feel less like a bad spring and more like a broken year. Salem, Massachusetts, swung from 74°F to 21°F in a single thirty-hour stretch in April, then hit 101°F on 22 June — a wild, disorienting oscillation layered on top of the general cold, not a steady chill a farmer could plan around. The killing blow came in stages through August and September: a cold spell on 13-14 August froze the corn standing north of Concord, New Hampshire — the crop the whole region lived on — and by late September, hard frost and snow at Hanover finished whatever remained. Three-quarters of New England's corn crop was destroyed that year.<Sidenote n={2} href="https://newenglandhistoricalsociety.com/1816-year-without-a-summer/" source="New England Historical Society">Families foraged nettles, wild turnips, and hedgehogs — the diet of a famine, in a country that had not believed it could have one. Hay in New Hampshire reached $180 a ton, six times its normal price, because a starving crop meant starving livestock too.</Sidenote>

Down in Virginia, a retired president watches the same catastrophe from the other end of a ledger. Thomas Jefferson, home at Monticello and already sliding toward the debt that will shadow his last years, loses his crop to the cold summer, and the loss drives him <Annotation client:load term="further into debt" note="Crop failures during the 1816 cold summer deepened Thomas Jefferson's mounting debts at Monticello. Even a former president farming some of the best land in Virginia could not command a harvest the sun would not grant. Year Without a Summer, Wikipedia." /> — a small, telling detail, because it means the failure reached the man with the best land and every advantage the young republic could offer. Oat prices, the fuel that ran every horse in the country, went from twelve cents a bushel in 1815 to <Annotation client:load term="ninety-two cents" note="Oat prices rose from 12 cents per bushel (1815) to 92 cents (1816) — a near eight-fold increase. Year Without a Summer, Wikipedia." /> in 1816. That is not a market fluctuation. That is the arithmetic of hunger, written into a commodity ledger.

Congress did not help itself. In the middle of the crisis, sitting members voted themselves a salary increase, and voters — hungry, frozen, and furious — punished them for it. Roughly <Annotation client:load term="seventy percent of incumbent US representatives" note="In the 1816 elections, nearly 70 percent of incumbent US representatives lost re-election, including Daniel Webster — a political casualty of a crisis whose actual cause (a volcano on the far side of the planet) nobody in the electorate could have named. New England Historical Society." /> lost their seats that year, including Daniel Webster. No one on either side of that vote knew the summer's failure traced to a mountain in the Java Sea; they experienced it as pure, inexplicable weather, and they did what an angry, frozen electorate does. They threw the incumbents out.

Nobody in New England knew *why* the summer had died. The eruption was a year old and half a world away, unreported in any paper a Vermont farmer read; there was no theory of stratospheric aerosols, no notion that an unmapped mountain had reached across two oceans and salted their fields with frost. They experienced the disaster as pure weather — inexplicable, possibly deserved — and they did the rational thing a farmer does when the land betrays him twice running. They left.

The map traces a decision made a hundred thousand times over, in the same terrible winter of 1816-17: stay on land that had just proved it could freeze in July, or leave everything and walk west. The historian L. D. Stillwell put the Vermont exodus at <Annotation client:load term="ten to fifteen thousand people" note="Historian L. D. They caught what the period called "Ohio fever," loaded what they could onto wagons, and walked toward soil that might, unlike the frost-hammered granite hills of Vermont, actually yield a crop. Among the families who moved in exactly these years was a farmer named Joseph Smith, who relocated his household from Vermont to Palmyra, in western New York — where his son would, a few years later, found a new American faith. The migration did not *cause* that; claiming so would be exactly the kind of tidy over-reading a grounded chronicle has to resist. But the Smiths' move was, in part, the great reshuffling of New England the frost set in motion.

<Detail summary="Go deeper: the sky that announced itself twice a day">
The aerosol veil was invisible in ordinary daylight, but it announced itself at dawn and dusk. Sulfate droplets scatter the short-wavelength blue out of low-angle sunlight, leaving the reds and oranges behind — which is why the years after a great eruption are famous for lurid, blood-coloured sunsets. Observers across Europe recorded skies of unusual, dramatic red for up to three years after Tambora. Nobody connected them to a mountain. One London painter, watching the sky with more attention than anyone else alive, would preserve the evidence in his canvases for two centuries — a scientific record no one knew they were keeping. This chronicle returns to him below.
</Detail>

The cast above did not know they were living inside a climate disaster; they knew only that the summer had gone wrong. Start at the Villa Diodati, because it is the most famous rainy holiday in the history of literature. Lord Byron rented the villa on the shore of Lake Geneva from <Annotation client:load term="10 June to 1 November 1816" note="Byron's tenancy of the Villa Diodati at Cologny ran 10 June-1 November 1816. Villa Diodati, Wikipedia." />, and nearby, at a smaller house called the Maison Chapuis, the poet Percy Shelley settled in with an eighteen-year-old named Mary Godwin — not yet Mary Shelley — and her stepsister Claire Clairmont. Byron's physician, John Polidori, completed the party. They came for a summer on the lake. What they got was Tambora's summer.

It rains — the way it does when a hemisphere's weather has been knocked off its hinges, days on end, cold and grey, the Alps that should fill the windows erased into cloud. Confined indoors, the party reads German ghost stories aloud from a French anthology, and Byron makes a proposal that will outlast every poem he wrote.

<Pullquote cite="Mary Shelley, Introduction to the 1831 edition of Frankenstein" href="https://www.gutenberg.org/files/42324/42324-h/42324-h.htm" kind="quote">
“We will each write a ghost story,” said Lord Byron; and his proposition was acceded to… It proved a wet, ungenial summer, and incessant rain often confined us for days to the house.
</Pullquote>

Mary tries for days to think of a story and fails. Then, one night, half-asleep, she doesn't so much invent one as *see* it — and her own account, written fifteen years later, is the closest thing we have to a birth certificate for science fiction.

<Pullquote cite="Mary Shelley, Introduction to the 1831 edition of Frankenstein" href="https://www.gutenberg.org/files/42324/42324-h/42324-h.htm" kind="quote">
I saw — with shut eyes, but acute mental vision — I saw the pale student of unhallowed arts kneeling beside the thing he had put together. I saw the hideous phantasm of a man stretched out, and then, on the working of some powerful engine, show signs of life.
</Pullquote>

The novel that grew out of that rain-locked waking dream, <Annotation client:load term="Frankenstein; or, The Modern Prometheus" note="Mary Shelley began Frankenstein at Lake Geneva in the wet summer of 1816, aged 18; published 1818. Villa Diodati, Wikipedia; Haunted Summer of 1816, Britannica." />, put a new fear into the modern imagination: not a monster, but a creator's abandonment of what he brings to life. That this fable was dreamed up by an eighteen-year-old woman, in a cold house, under a sky dimmed by a volcano nobody in that room had heard of, while a hemisphere outside starved, is one of history's more exact ironies. Byron himself, that same Geneva summer, wrote something bleaker still — a poem about the sun going out, the world freezing in the dark, and, after the light fails, War having briefly had nothing left to consume, glutting "himself again." Byron explained the poem plainly enough afterward: he wrote it, he said, at Geneva, on <Annotation client:load term="a day so dark that the fowls went to roost at noon" note="Byron's own gloss on ‘Darkness’: written at Geneva during ‘a celebrated dark day, on which the fowls went to roost at noon, and the candles were lighted as at midnight.’ The poem's apocalyptic imagery was, in part, direct observation of Tambora's dimmed sky. ## The evidence hidden in the pigment

While Byron wrote the dark down, a painter in London was painting the *red* down — and, without meaning to, keeping a scientific record precise enough that instruments would confirm it two centuries later.

A team led by the atmospheric scientist Christos Zerefos went through 124 high-resolution images of paintings, most of them Turner's, held at London's Tate Gallery, and measured the <Annotation client:load term="ratio of red to green in the painted sunsets" note="Zerefos and colleagues measured red-to-green ratios in 124 paintings spanning 1500-2000 and compared them against the historical record of 54 major volcanic eruptions. The redder a painter made his sky, the more volcanic aerosol had actually been aloft in the year he painted it, and the correlation held across different painters and styles — which is the whole point. If it tracked the artist, it would be taste. Because it tracks the atmosphere, it is data.

Turner is the star witness. In exactly these post-Tambora years his skies turn to the famous, furious washes of orange and blood-red that critics have spent two centuries calling visionary — and they were visionary, but they were also *accurate*. His canvases turn out to be unwitting instruments: spectrographs of a poisoned sky, hung in a national gallery, holding a measurement no one knew they contained until someone thought to point a colorimeter at them.

Return to the oats. When a bushel went from twelve cents to ninety-two, feeding a horse became a luxury, and across Europe horses — the sole engine of overland transport — were slaughtered or simply starved for want of fodder. In the Grand Duchy of Baden, a forestry official named Karl Drais, watching the horses go, asked a question nobody had needed to ask before: what could carry a person if the horse were gone?

His answer rolled out of a Mannheim workshop in 1817 — a two-wheeled wooden contraption you straddled and pushed along the ground with your feet, no pedals yet, no chain, just balance and a shove. The bicycle, in other words, is very plausibly a fossil of the Year Without a Summer — a machine designed around the sudden, temporary absence of the horse. The horses came back when the harvests did. The idea of horseless personal transport did not.

## The wider dark: Asia, Ireland, and a contested epidemic

The story tends to be told, in the West, through Vermont and Lake Geneva, because those are the archives that survived and the names that carry. But the veil dimmed the whole planet, and where it fell on societies with thinner margins than Switzerland or New England, it killed on a scale that dwarfs the famous stories.

<Callout kind="caution" title="The cholera link — a contested hypothesis, not a settled fact">
Here the sources genuinely disagree, and a grounded chronicle has to show that rather than smooth it over. Wikipedia's own Year Without a Summer page states that “the delayed summer monsoon caused late torrential rains that aggravated the spread of cholera” from a region near the Ganges in Bengal as far as Moscow — implying Tambora's climate disruption helped trigger the first cholera pandemic, which did indeed begin in Jessore, Bengal, in 1817. But Wikipedia's own dedicated page on the 1817-1824 cholera pandemic makes **no mention** of Tambora, volcanic forcing, or unusual weather at all — it attributes the pandemic's origin and spread instead to trade routes and, by some historians' account, the Kumbh Mela pilgrimage on the upper Ganges. The two pages, drawing on different literatures, do not agree. The most defensible position is the cautious one: the 1817 Bengal cholera outbreak is a real, dated event that coincided with a documented monsoon disruption plausibly linked to Tambora's veil, and some historical accounts connect the two — but the epidemiological consensus on why the pandemic actually spread points elsewhere. Treat the volcano-cholera link as a documented, contested hypothesis worth naming, not a proven causal chain.
</Callout>

There is one more strand worth pulling, because it points toward the future rather than away from it. In Darmstadt, a boy of thirteen lived through the famine and never forgot it. His name was <Annotation client:load term="Justus von Liebig" note="Justus von Liebig, who experienced the 1816 famine as a child in Darmstadt, went on to pioneer the study of plant nutrition and mineral fertilizers — a scientific response to the fragility of the harvest the Year Without a Summer had exposed so brutally. The timeline above closes a loop that took two centuries to draw, and it is still not fully closed — the newest entry on it, from 2023, moved the central sulfur estimate *upward*, not toward consensus. The Rajah of Sanggar died without a word for what he saw. The New England farmers left their fields without knowing why the fields had died. Mary Shelley wrote her monster, Byron wrote his darkness, Turner painted his red skies, and Drais built his running machine, and not one of them connected their strange, cold, luminous year to a mountain in the Java Sea, because no one on Earth yet could. The connection was made slowly — Zollinger climbing the caldera in 1847 to count the dead, sulfur isotopes and ice cores in the twentieth and twenty-first centuries measuring a plume that four different research teams still can't agree on the size of, and, in 2019, a climate model finally doing the arithmetic that ties the whole chain together in one sentence: the eruption made that killing summer up to a hundred times more likely than it had any right to be.

That is worth carrying out of this story, more than any single number. The half-degree, or the 1.9 degrees, or the 147 teragrams, or the 10 — whichever figure survives the next reassessment — did not announce itself. It came dressed as ordinary bad weather: a late frost, a wet June, a red sunset, a Congress that raised its own pay at the wrong moment. By the time anyone could name the cause, the people it scattered and starved and inspired were long past being told. And the mountain is not finished being a live question. Eruptions on Tambora's scale — VEI 7 or larger — recur, by the best current estimate, roughly <Annotation client:load term="1.6 times per thousand years" note="Wilson et al. (2023), Scientific Reports: VEI 7+ eruptions occur at a base rate of approximately 1.6 per 1,000 years globally — equivalent to roughly a one-in-six chance in any given century." />, which works out to something close to a one-in-six chance in any given century — this one included. The 1815 argument over how much sulfur reached the stratosphere is not an academic curiosity. It is the calibration data for the next mountain, whichever one it turns out to be, and this time, at least, we will know its name before the snow falls in June.
