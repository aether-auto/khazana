On July 10, 2026, the Commerce Department moved the United Arab Emirates out of the export-control tier reserved for proliferation risks and into Country Group A:5 — the small club of allies who can receive advanced American chips without a license at all. Ten days later, the paperwork was still warm. The same government that had spent 2025 alone adding 95 Chinese companies to its Entity List across four separate rules had just handed a country with well-documented, publicly disputed ties to Chinese military-linked firms a license-free lane for Nvidia's Blackwell processors.

This is not a contradiction inside the policy. It is the policy, and it has been for four years: build a wall, and cut doors in it wherever a deal, an ally, or an unfinished investigation makes a door convenient.

Read the timeline as two policies running on top of each other, because that is what it is. One line adds names — dozens at a time, four times in 2025 alone, each rule published with a Federal Register citation you can go read yourself. The other line opens exceptions — to a chip that was banned eighteen months earlier, to a country whose flagship AI firm has been the subject of a standing congressional investigation into Chinese ties. Both lines are real. Both are official U.S. policy. And the gap between them is where this story lives.

Hold those four numbers, because everything below is an explanation of how they coexist. The <Definition client:visible term="Entity List" def="A Commerce Department roster (15 CFR Part 744, Supplement No. 4) of foreign parties the U.S. government has determined pose a national-security or foreign-policy risk. A listing generally requires exporters to obtain a license — often under a presumption of denial — before shipping controlled U.S.-origin items to that party." /> has grown almost every quarter since October 2022, when the Biden administration first cut off China's access to leading-edge chipmaking tools. Three administrations' worth of officials have defended the same theory of the case: name the firms, block the tools, and China's advanced-node ambitions stall. Judged narrowly, the theory has some support — the controls have measurably slowed China's access to the most advanced lithography and forced real cost and yield penalties onto its leading foundry. Judged as a strategy for actually stopping China's semiconductor advance, the record built since 2022 argues the opposite: the controls have not stopped anything, they have re-routed it, and every re-route has taught China's supply chain to need Washington less.

<Detail summary="Go deeper: what the H200 exception actually requires">
The January 2026 policy is not a blanket reopening. To qualify for case-by-case review, an H200 or MI325X shipment must fall under a Total Processing Performance ceiling of 21,000 and a DRAM bandwidth ceiling of 6,500 GB/s; the exporter must certify the sale won't delay U.S. customer orders or divert global foundry capacity; and the aggregate performance shipped to China cannot exceed 50% of what the same chip line shipped to U.S. customers. Nvidia separately agreed to turn over 25% of China H200 revenue to the U.S. government, layered with a 25% import tariff. Two independent analyses — the Institute for AI Policy and Strategy and the law firm ArentFox Schiff, reading the same rule from very different vantage points — reach the identical practical conclusion: the conditions are real on paper, and BIS has essentially no mechanism to verify a single one of them once the chips leave a U.S. port.
</Detail>

## Ninety-five names, four rules, one pattern

Start with the part of the story that is easiest to verify, because it is also the part most people get wrong. The popular shorthand is that Washington "blacklisted the Chinese chip industry" in a single dramatic sweep. It didn't. It has been adding names in installments — a rule every few months, each one smaller than the last three combined, each one covering a narrower and more specific slice of the supply chain than the one before it.

Sum the right-hand column and you get 95 Chinese entities added across four verified 2025 rules alone — before counting the 140-entity sweep that opened the window in December 2024, or whatever rules land after this piece publishes. That is a lot of paperwork, and it represents real investigative work: BIS's March 2025 rule, for instance, targeted chipmaking-tool distributors and chemical suppliers specifically implicated in re-supplying China's advanced-node fabs, not a scattershot list. The <Annotation client:load term="September 2025 rule" note="90 FR 44496, eff. Sep 12, 2025: 32 entities added worldwide, 23 under China, including Shanghai Fudan Microelectronics and its Singapore and Taiwan-based affiliates, cited for circumvention activity." /> is the clearest tell of all — it named Shanghai Fudan Microelectronics together with its own Singapore and Taiwan affiliates in the same rule, which is BIS explicitly acknowledging, in the text of a federal regulation, that the workaround for a listed Chinese firm is very often a shell one hop outside China.

That acknowledgment is the whole story in miniature. The Entity List's logic is jurisdictional: it lists a company, not a supply chain. A firm in Shenzhen loses access the moment it is named. A trading company in Singapore registered eight weeks later, run by the same people, sourcing the same parts for the same customer, does not lose anything — until BIS notices and adds it too, months or years after the fact. The <Annotation client:load term="CRS's own assessment" note="CRS R48642 (Karen M. Sutter, updated Sep 19, 2025): PRC firms have used design firms as third parties to circumvent the Foreign Direct Product Rule and fabricate Huawei's Ascend chips at TSMC, and have recalculated chip specifications to fall just below BIS's October 2022 thresholds." /> of the Congressional Research Service, the nonpartisan body that briefs Congress on exactly this policy, is blunt about it: Chinese firms have used third-party design houses to route Huawei's chip fabrication through TSMC, and have deliberately re-engineered chip specifications to fall a hair below whatever threshold the most recent BIS rule just drew. The list grows by dozens every quarter because the workarounds regenerate by dozens every quarter. Neither side is winning a war of attrition; they are running the same race at the same pace.<Sidenote>Some 2025 rules also added non-Chinese entities (Iran, Pakistan, South Africa, Turkey, the UAE, India) in the same document — the China figures in the DataTable above are the China-specific counts confirmed in each rule's Federal Register abstract, not the worldwide total.</Sidenote> Each new rule, on both sides, is just narrower than the last.

Trade press covering the controls' three-year anniversary in early 2026 reached for the same word CSIS and CRS use: paradox. The Cipher Brief's February 2026 retrospective on the October 2022 controls argued that, rather than paralyzing China's AI sector, the restrictions "promoted domestic self-reliance," pointing to Chinese AI startups building what it called a parallel innovation ecosystem increasingly decoupled from the very hardware Washington was trying to withhold. That framing can read as spin from people who want the controls to look worse than they are. The Federal Register record above says otherwise — it is BIS's own rulemaking, not a critic's talking point, that keeps naming the same shell-affiliate pattern rule after rule.

## Singapore, Malaysia, and the mansion that gave it away

If the Entity List is the wall, transshipment is the door — and in 2025 and 2026, investigators on three continents finally started documenting exactly how that door gets used.

Every one of those lines is a documented case, not a hypothetical. The most recent, and the one that made the transshipment problem impossible to wave away as theoretical, broke in Singapore in July 2026: police there charged four people and seized a bungalow worth roughly <Annotation client:load term="$42.4 million" note="The Diplomat, Jul 2026, citing Singapore Police Force: authorities seized a bungalow valued at approximately S$55 million (~US$42.4 million) plus roughly S$1 million in cash, tied to a fraud investigation into servers — possibly containing restricted Nvidia chips — shipped from Singapore to Malaysia between November 2023 and February 2025." /> — plus about a million Singapore dollars in cash — from a network involving firms called Luxuriate Your Life and the Aperia group. The servers those firms shipped from Singapore to Malaysia between November 2023 and February 2025 were purchased from Dell, Super Micro, and Asus under end-user declarations investigators now say misrepresented the buyer. Whether the servers' final stop was inside Malaysia or across the border in China remains part of an active investigation; what is not in dispute is that the paperwork lied about who was receiving them, and that the case is directly tied to the wider probe into whether Chinese AI labs — DeepSeek among them — obtained restricted Nvidia chips through third countries.

The U.S. side of the same pattern produced an indictment five months earlier. On November 20, 2025, the Justice Department <Annotation client:load term="charged four men" note="DOJ press release, Nov 20, 2025 (U.S. Citizens and Chinese Nationals Arrested for Exporting Artificial Intelligence Technology to China): defendants allegedly routed 400 Nvidia A100 GPUs to China via Malaysia and Thailand between 2024 and January 2025, and attempted a further shipment of Hewlett Packard Enterprise supercomputers containing Nvidia H100 GPUs and 50 separate H200 GPUs before it was interdicted." /> — two U.S. citizens and two Chinese nationals — with running exactly this circuit: falsified paperwork and fake contracts to route 400 Nvidia A100 GPUs through Malaysia and Thailand into China between October 2024 and January 2025, and a follow-on attempt to move Hewlett Packard Enterprise supercomputers containing Nvidia H100 and H200 GPUs along the same path, which law enforcement disrupted before it completed. The mechanism in both cases is not exotic. It is the oldest trick in export control: declare a plausible, unrestricted end-user in a country with looser enforcement, let the shipment clear that country's customs, and let the paperwork — not the cargo — cross the last border.

<Pullquote cite="Maxwell Roberts, Institute for AI Policy and Strategy, Jan 16, 2026" href="https://www.iaps.ai/research/bis-licensing-policy-for-h200s" kind="quote">
Once the chips are in China, it's up to Chinese companies to enforce the control: BIS has almost zero leverage.
</Pullquote>

That line is aimed at the January 2026 H200 licensing policy specifically, but it is the honest summary of the entire enforcement model. BIS is a few hundred people relying on shipping manifests, license applications, and — per the Institute for AI Policy and Strategy's own characterization of the agency's working tools — <Annotation client:load term="Google searches and Excel" note="IAPS, Jan 16, 2026: characterizes BIS's post-export monitoring capacity as effectively relying on manual research methods rather than systematic tracking, meaning end-user certifications and remote-access restrictions are extremely difficult to verify or enforce once a shipment has left the U.S." />. It cannot inspect a data center in Shenzhen. It cannot audit a server rack in Johor Bahru. Every rule it writes is enforced at the moment of export and essentially never again — which is exactly the seam the Singapore and Malaysia cases exploited, and exactly why the dollar figures behind this pattern keep climbing. One Southeast Asian freight-forwarding case alone is reported to have moved <Annotation client:load term="$510 million in high-end servers" note="The Economy, Jun 29, 2026: a single Southeast Asian freight-forwarder diversion case moved an estimated $510 million in high-performance US-designed servers in under a month during 2025, ahead of a March 2026 case involving falsified end-user declarations and dummy server shells." /> in under a month during 2025; a wider set of cases reviewed by the same analysis put the total value of high-performance servers "allegedly funneled to China" through 2024–2025 diversion schemes near $2.5 billion.

<Detail summary="Go deeper: how the region tried to patch itself">
Malaysia moved first among the transshipment hubs. On July 14, 2025, its Ministry of Investment, Trade and Industry announced — and TechCrunch and other outlets covered the same day — that all exports, transshipments, and transits of high-performance U.S.-origin AI chips would require a strategic trade permit under the Strategic Trade Act 2010, with 30 days' advance notice to authorities — effective immediately. The Malaysia Semiconductor Industry Association's president called it "a necessary action…to curb transshipment issues." The rule is real and the intent is genuine, but it is also instructive about the limits of a downstream patch: it depends on exporters self-declaring accurately, in exactly the jurisdiction where the Singapore and DOJ cases show declarations were already being falsified.
</Detail>

It is worth being precise about what Singapore and Malaysia actually are, because the "hub" framing can flatten a real distinction. Neither is a smuggling backwater dressed up as a tech center — Singapore has genuine world-class fabs, equipment, materials, and research capacity of its own, and Malaysia has forty years of experience in chip assembly, testing, and packaging and is actively trying to move upstream into design. That legitimate industry is precisely what makes the two countries useful as diversion points: enormous, entirely lawful chip and chip-equipment trade already flows through both, which means a diverted shipment has thousands of legitimate ones to hide inside.

Singapore and Malaysia alone accounted for roughly $9.1 billion of China's $51.1 billion in equipment imports in 2025 — a river of entirely legal trade next to which a diverted shipment of a few hundred GPUs, boxed as ordinary servers, is close to invisible. Vietnam shows the same dynamic from a different angle: one post-tariff trade analysis found Chinese components re-exported through Vietnamese supply chains at roughly 8.8%, against nearly 40% genuine Vietnamese value-added — real regional manufacturing integration that a diversion scheme can also exploit. None of this indicts Southeast Asia's chip industry, which is real and growing on its own merits. It does mean that "regulate transshipment" is not a narrow technical fix; it is asking three sovereign trade regimes to police the seams of an industry that is, for entirely legitimate reasons, exploding in size at exactly the moment Washington needs it to slow down.

## Two million chiplets nobody was supposed to make

The cleanest single case study in how a listed company keeps buying anyway does not involve a shady freight forwarder at all. It involves the two most sophisticated chip companies on earth, one of which was specifically blocked from supplying the other.

The Center for Security and Emerging Technology at Georgetown — a research center, not an advocacy shop, that spends its time quantifying exactly this kind of leakage — had already flagged the underlying dynamic in October 2024, a year before the shell-company scheme became public: Huawei's Ascend chip line kept testing the edges of what U.S. controls could actually enforce, precisely because <Annotation client:load term="the rules restrict companies, not the parts that move between them" note="CSET Georgetown, Oct 29, 2024 (citing Jacob Feldgoise & Hanna Dohmen): U.S. export-control officials 'face a difficult task: writing rules that restrict what private companies do' once components leave direct U.S. jurisdiction — the core enforcement gap the chiplet case later confirmed at scale." />. CSIS's own account of the episode — published separately, and independently corroborated by Tom's Hardware's reporting on the same TechInsights findings — puts the number at roughly two million chiplets moved through shell companies before TSMC caught on, a scale that dwarfs any single smuggling indictment discussed above and did not require crossing a single international border. The chips never left Taiwan's own supply chain until they were already inside Huawei's products. No transshipment hub, no freight forwarder, no falsified bill of lading — just a paperwork fiction TSMC's own compliance process failed to catch for the better part of a year.

## The firm nobody listed

Every Entity List rule invites the same question: who is missing? Since December 2024, the most consequential answer has had a name — ChangXin Memory Technologies, universally known in the industry as CXMT, and the closest thing China has to a DRAM and high-bandwidth-memory national champion.

<Pullquote cite="Reva Goujon & Ben Reynolds, Rhodium Group, Dec 9, 2024" href="https://rhg.com/research/slaying-self-reliance-us-chip-controls-in-bidens-final-stretch/" kind="document">
The list notably excludes China's DRAM champion, ChangXin Memory Technologies (CXMT), which is widely viewed as the country's leading HBM player.
</Pullquote>

Rhodium Group flagged the omission the same week the December 2024 rule — 140 entities, the largest single BIS action on record — was published, and called CXMT's exclusion "legitimate questions, particularly given its status as a major customer for Western SME producers." Ten months later, the CRS report that briefs Congress on this exact policy said the same thing in its own words, independently: <Annotation client:load term="CRS confirms the gap" note="CRS R48642, updated Sep 19, 2025: 'some key PRC national champion firms, such as Changxin Memory Technologies (CXMT)…remain unlisted' despite their strategic importance to China's memory and HBM ambitions." />. Two separate institutions — one a private research firm briefing investors, one the nonpartisan research arm of Congress — looked at the same list nine months apart and flagged the same missing name.

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="Approved, but not implemented">
As of Reuters' July 15, 2026 reporting, CXMT had reportedly already been approved by a U.S. interagency committee for addition to the Entity List — but that approval has "not yet been implemented." The company has been sitting in regulatory limbo for at least a year and a half while it becomes precisely the kind of national champion the controls were designed to prevent.
</Callout>

While Washington deliberated, CXMT grew. CSIS's own tally has the company going <Annotation client:load term="from virtually zero to 5 percent of the global memory chip market in five years" note="CSIS, 'The Limits of Chip Export Controls in Meeting the China Challenge' (Apr 14, 2025): ChangXin Memory Technologies grew from a standing start to roughly 5% of the global memory chip market within five years, a pace the report explicitly attributes in part to the controls' failure to reach it." />; by its own IPO prospectus, filed in mid-2026, that figure had reached a 7.7% share of the global DRAM market in 2025, making CXMT the world's fourth-largest DRAM manufacturer — a milestone Reuters reported the same week it confirmed the company's Entity List status was still pending. CXMT is now preparing to raise at least $8.6 billion on Shanghai's STAR Market, potentially the largest IPO in Asia in 2026, funding the exact production-capacity expansion and HBM push that would make it China's answer to SK Hynix and Micron. It is still, as of this writing, not on the Entity List.

CXMT is not an isolated gap; it is the sharpest example of a broader pattern the same CSIS analysis lays out in granular detail. Huawei's Pura 70 smartphone, torn down and component-mapped, now contains <Annotation client:load term="33 China-sourced components and only 5 from outside the country" note="CSIS, 'The Limits of Chip Export Controls in Meeting the China Challenge' (Apr 14, 2025), citing a teardown of the Huawei Pura 70." /> — a complete reversal from the phone Huawei was building before the first controls landed in 2019. China, per the same report, now produces roughly twice as many research papers as the United States on chip design specifically. None of that happened despite the controls existing; substantial parts of it happened because the controls existed, gave China's state apparatus an unambiguous target, and left the country's most important emerging champion free to chase it without the friction a timely listing would have imposed.

## So what

Put the four numbers from the top of this piece back together, because none of them makes sense as an isolated fact — only as a system.

**140** entities added in December 2024's single sweep, and **95** more Chinese firms added across four separate, verified 2025 rules — real, aggressive, and by any historical standard, extraordinary enforcement effort. **50%**, the volume cap that reopened a chip banned eighteen months earlier for case-by-case export to China, alongside a UAE reclassification that handed a country under active congressional scrutiny license-free access to the newest AI accelerators America makes. And **36%**, the share of one critical toolmaker's revenue China still commanded three years into a regime built explicitly to shrink it — a share ASML itself only expects to fall once a narrower, later rule finally targets the specific tool class China had been buying freely.

None of this means export controls accomplished nothing. They demonstrably slowed China's access to the most advanced lithography, and SMIC's yields as low as 20% at its 5nm-class node, at 40–50% higher cost than TSMC's equivalent, are the real, measurable price of not having EUV. The mistake is treating that partial success as the whole story. The controls' actual failure mode was never a single dramatic breach — it was structural, and it was built into the design from the start: list companies one at a time while their shell affiliates register in Singapore; restrict the newest chip while licensing the previous generation back in at volume; announce sweeping additions on a quarterly cadence while a documented national champion sits, fully identified and repeatedly flagged by name, in regulatory limbo for a year and a half. Every one of those gaps did the same thing on a longer clock: it gave a Chinese firm, a Chinese hub economy, or a Chinese tool industry more time to become the thing the control was supposed to prevent it from becoming.

The transshipment economy through Singapore, Malaysia, and now potentially the UAE is not a bug in an otherwise sound strategy. It is the predictable output of a policy that restricts named parties instead of physical capability, enforced by an agency that — in the words of the people who study it most closely — has almost zero leverage the moment a chip crosses a border it wasn't supposed to cross. CXMT's unfinished listing is not bureaucratic slowness; it is a year and a half in which the firm Rhodium Group and the CRS both named, independently, as China's DRAM champion turned that gap into a 7.7% share of the global market and an $8.6 billion war chest. And the July 2026 UAE reclassification is not an unrelated story about a different region — it is the same mechanism, applied prospectively, to the newest and most capable chips America makes, with the same senator who flagged the deal's technology-diversion risk to China calling it "corrupt" the same day it took effect.

CSIS's own read of this record is a policy reframing worth taking seriously rather than dismissing as the standard think-tank hedge. The authors of "The Limits of Chip Export Controls" argue Washington should shift its center of gravity from what they call "protect" measures — the Entity List, the licensing regimes, the enforcement chases across Southeast Asia — toward "promote" measures: subsidizing and accelerating the next generation of American and allied chip technology fast enough that China's catch-up, however real, never closes the gap. Their logic follows directly from everything above: <Annotation client:load term="export controls are relevant only so long as the United States and its allies possess chip technologies that China wants and needs" note="CSIS, 'The Limits of Chip Export Controls in Meeting the China Challenge' (Apr 14, 2025) — the report's closing argument for reallocating policy emphasis from restriction toward domestic and allied innovation investment." /> — and every quarter that China's own tool industry, memory champions, and packaging tricks close that gap is a quarter in which the same list of restricted names matters a little less. China's own state investment in the chase — per <Annotation client:load term="a separate CSIS analysis of China's localization drive" note="CSIS, 'China's Localization Drive in Semiconductors Gains Impetus from Allied Chip Export Controls' (Mar 24, 2026): an estimated $150 billion in Chinese public funding has been channeled into semiconductor self-sufficiency since 2014, versus the $52.7 billion the 2022 CHIPS and Science Act authorizes — a gap the report says is 'substantially smaller in scale' on the U.S. side." /> — runs to roughly $150 billion since 2014, nearly three times the $52.7 billion the U.S. CHIPS and Science Act authorized. A policy built entirely around subtraction, against an opponent outspending it three-to-one on addition, was never going to be a wall that holds by itself.

A wall with enough doors in it is not a wall. It is a toll booth — and the toll, three years and hundreds of entity listings into this policy, is being paid mostly by American chipmakers' market share, while the traffic keeps moving.
