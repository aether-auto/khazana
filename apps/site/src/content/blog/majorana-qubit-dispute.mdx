On June 24, 2026, a condensed-matter physicist at the University of St Andrews published seven paragraphs in Nature that quietly threaten to unwind a claim Microsoft had spent a decade and a public press conference building toward. Henry Legg's piece is not framed as an accusation of fraud. It does not allege that Microsoft's scientists lied. It argues something narrower and, in a way, more damaging: that the signal Microsoft published as evidence of the first topological qubit is <Annotation client:load term="statistically indistinguishable" note="Legg's Nature critique concludes that Microsoft's parity readout occurred in regions of phase space with considerable disorder that appear gapless — meaning the prerequisite for a topological interpretation is not established by the published data." href="https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10567-8" /> from an ordinary, disordered, entirely un-topological effect that has nothing to do with the exotic particle it was said to reveal.

That is the whole dispute, and it is worth sitting with how strange a sentence that is. Nobody is debating whether Microsoft's chip works as a computer — it doesn't yet, by anyone's account. The argument is about whether a specific squiggle of conductance data, filtered through a specific piece of analysis software, means what Microsoft says it means. A decade-long chase for a particle that would make quantum computers radically more scalable has arrived, again, at a fight over noise.

It is also, notably, not a fight conducted in blog posts and social media threads, though plenty of those exist too. Legg's critique ran through Nature's formal "Matters Arising" channel — a peer-reviewed mechanism reserved specifically for contesting a paper the journal has already published, which comes with an editorially guaranteed right of reply. Nature accepted it on April 20, 2026, and held it for two months so that Microsoft's rebuttal could be written, reviewed, and published in the same issue, on the same day. That is slow, deliberate, institutional machinery — the opposite of a hot take — and it is exactly the machinery a claim this consequential is supposed to go through.

## The promise: a qubit protected by its own shape

Every quantum computer built so far — IBM's superconducting transmons, Google's Sycamore, IonQ and Quantinuum's trapped ions — fights the same enemy. A qubit's state is a fragile superposition, and anything that touches it — a stray photon, a vibrating atom, a cosmic ray — collapses it. The industry's answer has been <Definition client:visible term="quantum error correction" def="spreading one logical qubit's information redundantly across many physical qubits, so a single physical error can be detected and corrected without collapsing the encoded state." />: throw enough noisy physical qubits at the problem, in a redundant enough code, that the logical qubit underneath survives. It works, but the overhead is brutal — often hundreds of noisy physical qubits per usable logical one.

Topological quantum computing proposes a different trade entirely: build a qubit whose error resistance is a fact of geometry rather than a fact of careful engineering. The building block is the <Definition client:visible term="Majorana zero mode" def="a quasiparticle predicted to appear, in pairs, at the ends of a topological superconducting wire — each half of one ordinary electron's worth of quantum information, split across two physically separated locations." /> (MZM), first theorized by Ettore Majorana in 1937 as a fermion that is its own antiparticle. Pair two of them at the ends of a wire and, according to theory, they jointly encode one bit of quantum information as a shared property — <Annotation client:load term="fermion parity" note="Whether the pair of Majorana zero modes together holds an even or odd number of electrons — a single binary degree of freedom split between two physically separated ends of a wire." href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.09549" /> — that cannot be read out, or disturbed, by a local perturbation at either end alone. As Scott Aaronson put it in his FAQ on the announcement, these are qubits built from <Annotation client:load term="nonabelian anyons" note="Excitations that can exist in a two-dimensional (or effectively 1D-at-the-ends) medium, whose exchange statistics depend on the order in which they are braided around each other — unlike ordinary particles, swapping two nonabelian anyons twice does not necessarily return the system to its original state." href="https://scottaaronson.blog/?p=8669" />, where an error, to matter, has to change the topology of how those anyons braid around each other — "robustness built in to the physics," in his phrasing, rather than bolted on by a code.

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="Why this would matter so much">
If it works, a topological qubit does not need the hundred-to-one physical-to-logical overhead of a superconducting or trapped-ion code, because the protection is structural rather than statistical. That is the entire commercial and scientific bet Microsoft has been making for more than a decade — and it is also exactly why an ambiguous signal is such a big deal. Extraordinary architectural claims need extraordinarily clean evidence.
</Callout>

Aaronson's caveats from the day of the announcement are worth keeping close, because they frame everything that follows. A topological qubit, if it exists, does not yet do anything useful on its own — "not yet," he wrote flatly, since a single qubit of any kind offers no computational advantage. And even a confirmed topological qubit would only bring the technology to roughly where conventional qubits stood <Annotation client:load term="20 to 30 years ago" note="Aaronson's framing: a real, working topological qubit would represent parity with where superconducting/trapped-ion qubits were two to three decades into their own development — meaning the architecture would still need to prove dramatically better reliability to justify its long, expensive gestation." href="https://scottaaronson.blog/?p=8669" /> — meaning the entire argument for building one at all rests on a reliability advantage that has not yet been demonstrated at scale.

Underneath the "braiding" language sits a specific mathematical object: a topological invariant computed from the wire's scattering matrix, whose *sign* — not its magnitude — is what is supposed to flip when the wire crosses from an ordinary superconductor into the topological phase. That sign-not-magnitude property is exactly why the physics community treats topological protection as qualitatively different from simply having low noise.

The invariant itself is well established theory; nobody in this dispute is arguing about equation (1). What is contested is a much more mundane, and much more consequential, question: given a noisy, finite, disordered real device, how do you actually *compute* det(r) reliably enough to trust its sign? That is the entire job of the Topological Gap Protocol, and it is the entire surface area of Legg's critique.

## Building one: the tetron and the topoconductor

Microsoft's announcement on February 19, 2025 introduced a new materials category it called a <Annotation client:load term="topoconductor" note="Microsoft's term for a hybrid material class — an indium arsenide (InAs) semiconductor layered with an aluminum (Al) superconductor — engineered, when cooled to millikelvin temperatures and tuned by magnetic fields and gate voltages, to host topological superconductivity at a nanowire's ends." href="https://azure.microsoft.com/en-us/blog/quantum/2025/02/19/microsoft-unveils-majorana-1-the-worlds-first-quantum-processor-powered-by-topological-qubits/" />: indium arsenide layered with aluminum, gate-defined into nanowires that, when cooled near absolute zero and tuned by magnetic field and voltage, are supposed to become topological superconductors with a Majorana zero mode pinned at each end. The basic qubit unit built from this material is called a <Annotation client:load term="tetron" note="Two parallel topological superconducting nanowires connected by a perpendicular trivial (non-topological) superconducting bridge — four Majorana zero modes total, one at each wire end — with quantum information encoded in the shared parity across the pair." href="https://azure.microsoft.com/en-us/blog/quantum/2025/02/19/microsoft-unveils-majorana-1-the-worlds-first-quantum-processor-powered-by-topological-qubits/" />: two parallel topological wires joined by a perpendicular trivial superconducting bridge, with four Majorana zero modes in total.<Sidenote source="Majorana 1, Wikipedia" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Majorana_1">Wikipedia's own summary of the architecture, cross-checked against Microsoft's announcement, corroborates the tetron's basic layout — useful because it is compiled independently of Microsoft's own framing.</Sidenote>

The readout mechanism is the genuinely new engineering in the 2025 paper. Rather than trying to directly measure a single Majorana mode — which would destroy the very superposition it is meant to protect — Microsoft's team coupled the proximitized nanowire to a <Annotation client:load term="triple-quantum-dot interferometer" note="An interferometer built from three tunnel-coupled quantum dots, used to convert the wire's fermion parity into a measurable shift in quantum capacitance without directly probing the Majorana modes themselves." href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.09549" />. The parity of the wire shifts the dots' <Annotation client:load term="quantum capacitance" note="An effective capacitance that depends on a quantum system's internal state — here, a shift of up to 1 femtofarad depending on whether the wire is in its even- or odd-parity state — read out dispersively through an RF resonator without collapsing the state." href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.09549" /> by up to one femtofarad, which a radio-frequency resonator can read dispersively — that is, without collapsing the state it's measuring. The published number for this step was genuinely strong: a signal-to-noise ratio of 1 in 3.7 microseconds at optimal flux values, and a measurement assignment error probability of just 1%, with the two parity states remaining stable for over a millisecond at a time.

<Sidenote source="Interferometric Single-Shot Parity Measurement in an InAs-Al Hybrid Device, arXiv:2401.09549" href="https://arxiv.org/abs/2401.09549">Microsoft's own 2025 paper does not overclaim this step. Its text states plainly that the measurement "does not distinguish between MZMs in the topological phase and fine-tuned low-energy Andreev bound states in the trivial phase" — the authors flagged the ambiguity themselves, before anyone else did.</Sidenote>

There is a second, easy-to-miss caveat worth surfacing here, because it explains a real gap between the paper and the press conference. Commenting directly on Scott Aaronson's blog after the announcement, Microsoft's Chetan Nayak clarified that the specific evidence Microsoft was calling proof of a topological qubit — as opposed to the peer-reviewed parity-measurement result — came from data first shown at an internal Station Q meeting, generated *after* the Nature paper had already been submitted for review roughly a year earlier. That evidence was, at the time of the press conference, unpublished and therefore unreviewed by anyone outside Microsoft.

<Annotation client:load term="Nature's own news desk" note="“Microsoft claims quantum-computing breakthrough — but some physicists are sceptical,” Nature news, February 2025." href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-00527-z" /> covered that gap at the time, and physicist <Annotation client:load term="Winfried Hensinger" note="Physics World, “Experts weigh in on Microsoft's topological qubit claim”: Hensinger called the disconnect between Microsoft's press release and its own peer-reviewed paper “a big no-no” in physics, warning it risked creating unrealistic public expectations that could ultimately hurt the field." href="https://physicsworld.com/a/experts-weigh-in-on-microsofts-topological-qubit-claim/" />'s "big no-no" complaint about the gap between the press release and the peer-reviewed paper was, in effect, a complaint about exactly this: the public claim and the refereed claim were not describing the same body of evidence. A second Nature news piece the same month reported the <Annotation client:load term="fresh challenge" note="“Microsoft quantum computing 'breakthrough' faces fresh challenge,” Nature news, February 2025." href="https://www.nature.com/articles/d41586-025-00683-2" /> that skepticism represented within days of the announcement — well before Legg's more formal critiques existed.

Microsoft's public roadmap, published alongside the announcement, was explicit about how far eight qubits is from a useful computer. The plan runs from today's single-tetron device, to a two-qubit device supporting measurement-based braiding, to a 4×2 array of eight qubits performing quantum error detection on two logical qubits, to a 27×13 tetron array for deeper error-correction development — on a chip Microsoft says is already designed to eventually house one million qubits. The company also claims its topological approach could cut quantum error-correction overhead roughly tenfold relative to the prior state of the art, and it has used the 2025 result to advance to the final phase of DARPA's US2QC quantum-benchmarking program as one of two finalist companies. None of that roadmap turns on whether the disputed parity signal is topological in origin — but the credibility of the roadmap's *foundational* claim, that Microsoft has already built the first working piece of protected hardware, turns on exactly that.

That caveat — buried in the methods, not the press release — is the seam the entire 2026 dispute grows out of. A parity-flip signal, on its own, cannot tell you whether you are looking at genuine topological protection or at an ordinary, disordered quantum dot that happens to flip its charge state in a similar way. Distinguishing the two requires a *second*, independent piece of evidence: proof that the wire actually hosts a robust superconducting gap in the region where the parity was read out. That second piece of evidence is where the Topological Gap Protocol comes in — and where the argument now lives.

## The ghost in the room: 2018, revisited

To understand why a statistical ambiguity provokes this much scrutiny, rewind eight years. In 2012, a Delft team led by Leo Kouwenhoven — including a young co-author named Sergey Frolov — published in Science the <Annotation client:load term="first experimental hints" note="Mourik, Zuo, Frolov, Plissard, Bakkers and Kouwenhoven, “Signatures of Majorana Fermions in Hybrid Superconductor-Semiconductor Nanowire Devices,” Science 336(6084):1003–1007, published online April 12, 2012 — bound, mid-gap zero-bias states in InSb nanowires under a magnetic field, consistent with (but not proof of) Majorana fermions." href="https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/22499805/" /> of Majorana zero modes in a superconductor-semiconductor nanowire. Six years later, the same lab, now working with Microsoft, went further: a March 2018 Nature paper titled "Quantized Majorana conductance" claimed to have observed a precisely quantized conductance plateau — a far stronger, more specific signature than the 2012 hints.

The 2018 retraction was not an isolated stumble, either. A related 2017 Nature paper from the same Delft group, on the nanowire epitaxy underlying these devices, was separately retracted in April 2022 after physicist Vincent Mourik — one of the two critics behind the 2018 retraction — identified data <Annotation client:load term="“inappropriately deleted or cropped”" note="Retraction Watch, April 24, 2022: the retraction notice for “Epitaxy of advanced nanowire quantum devices” (Nature, 2017) cites “unjustified data removal and cropping” in several figures, undermining the claimed agreement between theory and experiment." href="https://retractionwatch.com/2022/04/24/authors-retract-second-majorana-paper-from-nature/" /> from several figures, undermining the paper's claimed agreement between theory and experiment. Mourik's own characterization of the broader record was blunt: a "pattern of multiple unreliable papers," involving undisclosed data manipulation and selection issues across the group's output. That pattern is the backdrop against which any new Majorana claim from a Microsoft-affiliated group gets read.

IEEE Spectrum's headline for the episode — <Annotation client:load term="“Major(ana) Backpedaling”" note="IEEE Spectrum's coverage of the March 2021 retraction: “Controversial Microsoft-backed research on elusive theoretical particles that could have proved a major advance in quantum computing has now been retracted after other scientists pointed out critical flaws in the work.”" href="https://spectrum.ieee.org/majorana-microsoft-backed-quantum-computer-research-retracted" /> — became shorthand across the field for exactly the failure mode Legg's 2026 critique now revisits: a signal that looked, at first glance, like unambiguous evidence of exotic physics, undone not by fraud but by insufficiently rigorous handling of the raw data underneath it. Frolov, one of the two physicists who forced the 2018 retraction, had co-authored the original 2012 signatures paper. He was never opposed to the physics; he was opposed to sloppy evidence for it. That distinction — between doubting the theory and doubting the data — is precisely the distinction Legg draws in 2026, and it is why Microsoft's 2025 paper was built around a new, more rigorous-sounding validation step: a piece of software called the Topological Gap Protocol.

## Passing your own test: the Topological Gap Protocol

The <Annotation client:load term="Topological Gap Protocol" note="TGP — an automated analysis procedure, developed by Microsoft's own team and first published for an earlier device generation in Physical Review B 107, 245423 (2023), designed to classify regions of a device's parameter space as “topological” or “trivial” without a human eyeballing the plots and unconsciously picking the best-looking one." href="https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.19560" /> (TGP) exists to solve a real and well-known problem: humans staring at noisy conductance plots reliably talk themselves into seeing patterns that aren't robustly there. The TGP is meant to remove that judgment call by mechanically sweeping a device's tunable parameters and certifying, without a human's thumb on the scale, which regions of that parameter space look like a genuine topological superconductor. The 2023 paper describes it as a stringent, three-terminal transport test — current forced in one end of the wire and measured at both ends simultaneously, local and nonlocal, while gate voltage, magnetic field, and junction transparency are all varied — run on devices that were themselves designed and simulated in advance specifically to be robust against the disorder that plagued the 2012-to-2018 generation of experiments.

Microsoft's 2025 paper reported that a specific region of one device passed this protocol, and that the parity readout described above was performed inside that certified region — the logical chain being: TGP says gapped-and-topological, therefore the parity signal measured there reflects genuine Majorana physics rather than an accident of disorder. That chain is exactly what Legg's critique goes after, link by link.

## The argument about noise: what Legg actually found

Legg's public case built over roughly sixteen months and two written comments before the formal, peer-reviewed Nature critique appeared. In February 2025 he published a preprint dissecting the TGP as originally described for an earlier device generation, arguing the protocol's own definition of "topological" was unstable in four separate, compounding ways.

Legg's March 2025 comment on the actual 2025 Nature paper pushed further, into the code itself. He found that the TGP's conductance-antisymmetrization step reversed a data array by its <Annotation client:load term="index position rather than its physical value" note="Legg's technical finding: the code called the equivalent of a Python array reversal (x[::-1]) on the bias-voltage axis by list index, rather than antisymmetrizing around the true zero-bias-voltage value. <RunnableCode client:visible caption="The bug pattern in miniature — reversing by list position vs. by the physical value it represents. Illustrative reconstruction of the mechanism Legg describes; not Microsoft's literal source."
  code={`// Bias-voltage samples aren't symmetric around zero (a real device trait).
const biasUV = [-66.18, -40, -10, 5, 25, 50, 63.64];

// BUGGY: "antisymmetrize" by reversing the ARRAY INDEX order.
// This silently assumes the sampling is symmetric around zero — it isn't.
function antisymmetrizeByIndex(conductance) {
  const reversed = [...conductance].reverse();
  return conductance.map((g, i) => g - reversed[i]);
}

// CORRECT: antisymmetrize by matching each bias to its true negative VALUE.
function antisymmetrizeByValue(bias, conductance) {
  return conductance.map((g, i) => {
    const targetBias = -bias[i];
    // find the sample whose bias is closest to -bias[i]
    let bestJ = 0, bestDist = Infinity;
    bias.forEach((b, j) => {
      const d = Math.abs(b - targetBias);
      if (d < bestDist) { bestDist = d; bestJ = j; }
    });
    return g - conductance[bestJ];
  });
}

const conductance = [0.9, 0.5, 0.2, 0.05, 0.3, 0.6, 1.0];

The most direct part of the critique, though, is not about code at all — it is about what the raw conductance data show once plotted without the TGP's own antisymmetrization masking the result. Legg's reanalysis of Microsoft's own public transport data found "a substantial number of low energy states" across both local and nonlocal conductance channels in every device examined, large nonlocal conductance persisting close to zero bias — the textbook signature of a gapless system — and, in one device, "considerable particle-hole symmetry breaking" in a region where Microsoft's paper had stated such asymmetry was absent.

<Detail summary="Go deeper: why the antisymmetrization step can hide a gapless region" defaultOpen={false}>
The TGP's antisymmetrization step mathematically cancels the *symmetric* part of a conductance trace before thresholding it against the "gapped" criterion. That means the protocol can report a clean gap even when a large symmetric, near-zero-bias conductance signal is present in the raw data — because that signal gets subtracted out before the gap test ever sees it. Legg's argument is that this is precisely the situation in the devices he re-examined: substantial symmetric conductance near zero bias, which the antisymmetrized TGP output simply doesn't display. A superconducting gap is the prerequisite for the whole interpretive chain — without one, low-energy quasiparticles can switch parity freely, and an ordinary disordered quantum dot can produce a parity-flip signal that looks, from the outside, like a Majorana pair.
</Detail>

None of this reanalysis would have been possible from outside Microsoft at all if the underlying transport data had not been made public. Legg's critique is, in that specific sense, a case for open-data norms in experimental physics as much as it is a case against any one company: he did not run new measurements or gain privileged access — he downloaded the same public repository Microsoft published alongside its Nature paper and reran different code against it. That is also precisely why the dispute can be adjudicated by anyone with the raw files and the patience to re-derive the analysis, rather than resting on whichever lab happens to hold the only copy of the wire.

That sentence — from Nature's own editorial team, appended to the 2025 paper's review file well before Legg's critique existed — is the reason this dispute reads less like a bolt from nowhere and more like the field's own referees flagging a gap between what was measured and what was announced, the same gap Hensinger and Nature's news desk had already flagged in February 2025. Legg's 2026 critique is best read as a formal, technical elaboration of exactly that gap — not a new objection out of nowhere, but the field's long-standing unease finally worked through the data line by line. His own verdict, delivered in a metaphor that has circulated widely since: Microsoft, he said, claimed to have built the equivalent of a precision Swiss watch, but when he opened the case to examine the mechanism, he found what looked like a chaotic jumble of mismatched parts.

## Microsoft's reply: moving the goalposts, not the numbers

Nature published <Annotation client:load term="Microsoft's formal reply" note="“Reply to: On the robustness of topological gap detection via transport,” Nature (2026), with quantum hardware technical fellow Chetan Nayak as corresponding author — published in the same issue as Legg's critique, per Nature's editorial policy for Matters Arising exchanges." href="https://www.nature.com/articles/s41586-026-10568-7" /> the same day as Legg's critique — June 24, 2026. The reply does not, for the most part, contest Legg's specific numbers. It reframes what those numbers were ever supposed to prove.

<Pullquote cite="Chetan Nayak, quoted in Computerworld, June 2026" href="https://www.computerworld.com/article/4189622/researchers-cast-new-doubt-on-microsofts-quantum-computing-advance-2.html" kind="quote">
We stand by our results and our roadmap.
</Pullquote>

Microsoft's substantive argument is that the Topological Gap Protocol "was never intended as evidence" of topological superconductivity in the first place — only as a tuning procedure for choosing a promising operating point, a step upstream of the real evidence. That real evidence, Microsoft says, is the radio-frequency quantum-capacitance measurement itself: a stable, two-state signal whose oscillation with magnetic flux repeats with h/2e periodicity, a pattern the company argues is consistent with its theoretical model of a topological wire and hard to explain any other way. Microsoft further argues that some conductance inside the nominal gap doesn't necessarily disprove a gap, because junction-transparency effects can let current through without indicating gapless bulk physics, and that negative nonlocal conductance — one of Legg's disorder signatures — has more than one possible physical origin besides an accidental quantum dot. On the coding issues, Microsoft acknowledged what it called a single "off-by-one-pixel discrepancy" in the TGP's figure-plotting code, describing it as inconsequential to the paper's conclusions; separately, Microsoft physicist Roman Lutchyn has conceded that the TGP can produce false positives, but argues it does so with negligible likelihood. Microsoft's central counter to Legg personally is that he "proposes no alternative model capable of reproducing the capacitance signal" — in other words, that ruling out one interpretation is not the same as ruling in another.

## Why this is a statistical dispute, not a knockout

It is tempting to want a cleaner ending: someone is right, someone is wrong, and the field moves on. That is not how this kind of dispute resolves, and it is worth being honest about why. Neither Legg's critique nor Microsoft's reply, as of this piece, produces the one experiment that would settle it — a demonstrated braiding operation, an independently reproduced device showing the same parity signal under a truly fixed and pre-registered analysis protocol, or a published, unambiguous phase diagram that both sides agree on. What exists instead is a disagreement about how to weigh several pieces of circumstantial, partially-automated evidence against each other: a capacitance signal Microsoft says is hard to fake, a TGP certification Legg says is parameter-dependent to the point of near-meaninglessness, and raw conductance data both sides have now looked at and reached opposite conclusions from.

That is precisely the shape of a replication-crisis dispute in physics rather than in a softer science: the underlying phenomenon, if real, obeys strict quantum-mechanical law, but *detecting* it from noisy transport measurements is a statistical inference problem, and statistical inference problems are exactly where hardcoded filters, index-versus-value bugs, and an under-specified "gapped" threshold can quietly decide the answer before anyone runs a single new measurement. Condensed-matter physics is especially exposed to this failure mode because the object under study is a mesoscopic device — a few microns of disordered semiconductor and superconductor, unique down to its individual dopant atoms — rather than a fundamental particle behaving identically everywhere. Two "identical" nanowires, fabricated side by side on the same chip, can show meaningfully different disorder landscapes; an ordinary quantum dot trapped in one region of one wire can, in the right circumstances, mimic several of the electronic fingerprints theory predicts for a Majorana pair. That is exactly the ambiguity Microsoft's own 2025 paper flagged in its methods section, and it is why a clean resolution needs more than one device, one lab, or one piece of certifying software agreeing with itself.

An unambiguous resolution would look something like this: an independent laboratory, with no stake in Microsoft's roadmap, obtaining or fabricating an equivalent device, publishing its raw transport data alongside a pre-registered analysis protocol agreed in advance by both camps, and reporting whether a topological gap survives that protocol before anyone knows what the parity readout shows. That has not happened, and there is a structural reason it is hard: unlike a tabletop optics experiment, these are proprietary, purpose-built heterostructure devices requiring a fabrication capability that exists in only a handful of places on Earth, Microsoft's own lab foremost among them. ## The takeaway

If you build systems that infer a hidden true state from noisy measurements — which is most of experimental physics, and no small amount of software engineering — the Majorana dispute is a case study in where that inference quietly breaks. It rarely breaks at the headline number. It breaks in the unglamorous middle: which window of the data you chose to analyze, whether an array got reversed by position or by the value it was supposed to represent, whether a plotting parameter that "just happened" to be hardcoded to show the best region was ever revisited once the paper needed writing. None of that requires bad faith. It requires, as the 2018 retraction's own authors put it, "insufficient scientific rigour" — and a field's job, when a claim is extraordinary enough, is to keep re-deriving the mundane parts until the extraordinary part either survives every reasonable re-analysis or doesn't. As of this piece, the topological qubit has not yet done either.
