At 11:07 UTC on October 21, 2016, name resolution for Twitter, GitHub, Netflix, PayPal, Reddit, and Amazon began failing for a large slice of the internet — not because any of those companies had been breached, but because their shared DNS provider, Dyn, was drowning in forged traffic from hundreds of thousands of hijacked security cameras and home routers <Annotation client:load term="a series of DDoS attacks" note="Antonakakis et al., §6.3: 'we observed 23 attack commands that targeted Dyn infrastructure, from 11:07–16:55 UTC,' citing Dyn's own postmortem for the list of affected high-traffic sites." href="https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity17/technical-sessions/presentation/antonakakis" />. A month earlier, the same botnet had thrown 623 Gbps of junk traffic at a single security blogger's website — the largest attack its host, Akamai, had ever mitigated <Annotation client:load term="623 Gbps" note="Antonakakis et al., §6.3: 'an unprecedented 623 Gbps DDoS attack.' Independently confirmed by Akamai's own on-record figure of 623 Gbps via Krebs on Security." href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2016/11/akamai-on-the-record-krebsonsecurity-attack/" />. The weapon behind both attacks contained no novel exploit, no buffer overflow, no zero-day. It contained a list of 61 usernames and passwords — the kind printed on a sticker on the back of a DVR — and a scanner fast enough to try them against the entire IPv4 internet.

This is the teardown of that machine: Mirai. Not the folklore version — the one measured. A team of nine researchers from Georgia Tech, Michigan, Illinois, Akamai, Cloudflare, Google, and Merit Network spent seven months with a 4.7-million-IP network telescope, an Internet-wide scanner, live honeypots, and logs handed over by the victims themselves, and published the result as the peer-reviewed forensic record this piece is built on <Annotation client:load term="Understanding the Mirai Botnet" note="Antonakakis, M. et al., 26th USENIX Security Symposium (2017), pp. 1093–1110 — the primary measurement study this teardown is grounded against throughout." href="https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity17/technical-sessions/presentation/antonakakis" />. We're going to build the mechanism from that record — scan, brute-force, report, load, command — and then use it to make an uncomfortable argument: nothing about Mirai was a bug. Every piece of it was a rational exploitation of decisions the IoT supply chain made on purpose.

Hold onto that last pair of numbers, because they are the whole argument in miniature: 24,000 of some of the internet's cheapest, weakest, most disposable devices — no botmaster had to buy server time or rent a data center — pushed more traffic than the internet had ever recorded from a single DDoS attack. Nobody had to be clever. The devices did the work; the humans just had to know 61 passwords.

## Why brute force was even worth trying

Start with the naive objection: shouldn't 61 passwords be a joke of an attack surface? A modern server rejects a brute-force attempt after five tries. Rate-limiting, account lockouts, fail2ban — this is solved. If you assume the IoT device on the other end behaves like a hardened Linux server, guessing `root:xc3511` looks like a stunt, not a strategy.

That assumption is exactly what Mirai's designers rejected, and the data proves them right. The devices Mirai targeted were purpose-built consumer electronics — DVRs, IP cameras, home routers — running Telnet servers nobody expected to be internet-facing, with no rate limiting, no lockout policy, and in a meaningful number of cases, credentials that were <Definition client:visible term="hardcoded" def="baked into the device firmware at the factory rather than stored in user-editable configuration — changing the password in the admin panel does nothing, because the login the Telnet daemon actually checks lives somewhere the panel can't reach." /> rather than merely defaulted. Researchers at the threat-intelligence firm Flashpoint traced one of Mirai's most productive credential pairs — `root:xc3511` — to a Telnet daemon burned into white-labeled DVR and camera circuit boards manufactured by a single Chinese company, XiongMai Technologies, and resold under dozens of consumer brand names <Annotation client:load term="root:xc3511" note="Krebs on Security, ‘IoT Device Maker Vows Product Recall, Legal Action Against Western Accusers’ (Oct 2016): Flashpoint researchers traced the credential to XiongMai Technologies' white-labeled DVR/IP-camera boards; ‘the password is hardcoded into the device firmware and the tools needed to disable it aren't present’ in the device's own web panel." href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2016/10/iot-device-maker-vows-product-recall-legal-action-against-western-accusers/" />. XiongMai itself later admitted as much in a statement to the press.

<Pullquote cite="Xiongmai Technology, statement to the press, October 2016" href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2016/10/iot-device-maker-vows-product-recall-legal-action-against-western-accusers/" kind="quote">
Mirai is a huge disaster for the Internet of Things. XM have to admit that our products also suffered from hacker's break-in.
</Pullquote>

Owners could not simply change the password and move on, because in many of these devices there was no password to change — the web-admin panel controlled a cosmetic login layer, while the Telnet daemon underneath answered to a credential compiled into the firmware. That's the crux of this teardown's argument, and we'll come back to it once the mechanism is on the table: the "vulnerability" here was never a coding mistake anyone could patch. It was a supply-chain decision, repeated across dozens of downstream brands, to ship remote management open by default and unfixable by the customer.

First, though, the machine itself.

## The five-part architecture

Mirai is not one program running on infected devices — it's five cooperating roles, most of which the malware author kept deliberately dumb so that any one part could run on hardware as weak as a $30 IP camera. The researchers reconstructed this architecture directly from the source code Mirai's author released in October 2016 <Annotation client:load term="reconstructed this architecture" note="Antonakakis et al., §2, Fig. 2 — ‘Mirai Operation,’ derived from the publicly released source code (hackforums.net, Sept 30 2016)." href="https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity17/technical-sessions/presentation/antonakakis" />.

Read the loop clockwise. A bot doesn't infect anything itself — it only *finds* things. It fires stateless <Definition client:visible term="TCP SYN" def="the first packet of a TCP connection attempt; sending one without completing the handshake is enough to learn whether a port is open, at a fraction of the cost of a full connection." /> probes at pseudorandom IPv4 addresses on Telnet ports 23 and 2323, and when one answers, it runs a brute-force login using ten username/password pairs pulled at random from the credential dictionary. The moment one pair works, the bot's job is done: it reports the victim's IP address and the winning credentials to a hardcoded report server and goes back to scanning. A completely separate program — the loader — picks up that report, connects to the new victim, works out what CPU architecture it's running (MIPS, ARM, x86, and half a dozen others), and pushes the right compiled binary. Only after that binary executes does the device become a bot itself, dialing home to a command-and-control server to wait for orders while it starts scanning for the next victim.

That split matters. A weak IoT device has no spare compute for the fingerprinting, cross-compilation logic, and multi-threaded connection handling the loader needs — so Mirai never asks it to do that work. The bots are appliances that only ever do two cheap things: scan, and brute-force ten passwords. Everything expensive lives on rented infrastructure the attacker controls <Annotation client:load term="everything expensive lives on rented infrastructure" note="F. Lauria, IIT-CNR Technical Report TR-06/2023: the loader 'demonstrates a robust and efficient approach through its multi-threaded architecture' — a main listener thread plus a dedicated worker thread per victim, separate from the lightweight scanning bots." href="https://iris.cnr.it/retrieve/c8af9fa9-4172-4fa2-9a86-18d019eb7f8b/prod_482082-doc_198344.pdf" />. This is the first of several places where Mirai's design looks less like malware written by an amateur and more like an engineer optimizing for the actual constraints of the hardware it was exploiting.

Follow one device through that loop and its state machine is short — five hops from "never seen" to "attacking on command."

That single fact will matter a great deal later, when we get to what actually stopped the botnet from simply growing forever.

## The dictionary — and why ten-of-sixty-one was already overkill

Here's the part worth running yourself. Mirai's released source shipped a dictionary of 62 credential lines; because one line repeats, that's 61 genuinely unique username/password pairs <Annotation client:load term="61 genuinely unique" note="Antonakakis et al., §2: ‘a pre-configured list of 62 credentials.’ CSO Online's independent parse of the released scanner.c: ‘scanner.c has 62 password lines to check, [but] only 61 of them are unique combinations’ — the two accounts corroborate each other on the dictionary's size." href="https://www.csoonline.com/article/558215/here-are-the-61-passwords-that-powered-the-mirai-iot-botnet.html" />. Each scanning bot doesn't even try all 61 against a given target — it randomly samples just 10 of them per attempt. That sounds like it should make the attack weaker. In practice, it barely matters, because the dictionary wasn't built to be exhaustive against one device — it was built to be broad against the *population*.

function sampleTen(dict) {
  const pool = [...dict];
  const picked = [];
  for (let i = 0; i < 10 && pool.length; i++) {
    const idx = Math.floor(Math.random() * pool.length);
    picked.push(pool.splice(idx, 1)[0]);

  return picked;

// One simulated DVR still running its factory-default login:
const victimCreds = ["root", "xc3511"];

function bruteForce(dict, real) {
  const attempts = sampleTen(dict);
  for (let i = 0; i < attempts.length; i++) {
    const [u, p] = attempts[i];
    if (u === real[0] && p === real[1]) {
      return { success: true, triesUsed: i + 1 };
    }
  }
  return { success: false, triesUsed: attempts.length };
}

const result = bruteForce(DICTIONARY, victimCreds);
console.log("10 random guesses from a 15-pair sample:", result);

Run it a few times: sometimes it lands on try one, sometimes it misses all ten. That miss doesn't matter, because scanning is continuous and the population of scanning bots is huge — if this bot misses, another bot scanning the same IP block an hour later tries a different random 10, and the device is still running the same factory login it shipped with. The dictionary didn't need to be clever. It needed to be broad enough that across hundreds of thousands of vulnerable devices and a botnet that never stops scanning, misses simply don't compound.

## How researchers proved this traffic was Mirai, not noise

The mechanism above is what the source code says Mirai does. Proving that hundreds of thousands of real devices on the real internet were actually doing it — as opposed to ordinary background scanning, which happens constantly — is a harder, more interesting problem, and it's the moment in the paper where the measurement itself becomes the hard part of the teardown.

Mirai's scanner is stateless: to keep up its scan rate, it doesn't track which IPs it has already probed, and by design it discards no memory of a probe once it's sent. But that statelessness left an accidental fingerprint. Every outbound SYN packet reused the destination IP address as its 32-bit TCP sequence number — a field that's supposed to be random. The odds of an ordinary scanner doing that by chance are one in 2^32.

<RunnableCode client:visible caption="The exact artifact Antonakakis et al. used to separate Mirai from background internet noise. Edit the ‘ordinary scanner’ sequence number and watch the fingerprint fail to match."
  code={`function ipToInt(ip) {
  const [a, b, c, d] = ip.split(".").map(Number);
  return ((a << 24) | (b << 16) | (c << 8) | d) >>> 0;
}

// Mirai's stateless scanner sets the outbound SYN's sequence number
// equal to the destination IP — an artifact of skipping per-connection
// state to scan faster.
function isMiraiFingerprint(destIp, seqNum) {
  return ipToInt(destIp) === (seqNum >>> 0);
}

const dest = "192.0.2.44";
const miraiStyleSeq = ipToInt(dest);   // Mirai: seq === dest IP, always
const ordinarySeq = 0x9f2a71c3;        // a normal scanner: a random seq

console.log("Mirai-style probe flagged:", isMiraiFingerprint(dest, miraiStyleSeq));
console.log("Ordinary-scanner probe flagged:", isMiraiFingerprint(dest, ordinarySeq));
console.log("Chance of an accidental match: 1 in", 2 ** 32);
return "over 7 months, researchers expected ~86 accidental matches on a 4.7M-IP telescope; they logged 116.2 billion.";`} />

That gap — 86 expected, 116.2 billion observed — is not a rounding error, it's a different phenomenon entirely, and it's why the researchers could say with confidence that they weren't measuring internet background radiation <Annotation client:load term="116.2 billion" note="Antonakakis et al., §3.1: expected ‘roughly 86 packets demonstrating this pattern’ by chance; observed ‘116.2 billion Mirai probes from 55.4 million IP addresses’ over the 7-month telescope window (July 18, 2016–Feb 28, 2017)." href="https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity17/technical-sessions/presentation/antonakakis" /> — they were watching an actual botnet, hour by hour, IP by IP.

## From one IP to a quarter million, then a peak of 600,000

Mirai's very first scan came from a single IP address at a U.S. bulletproof hosting provider on August 1, 2016 <Annotation client:load term="a single IP address" note="Antonakakis et al., §4.1: the bootstrap scan ran from an IP belonging to DataWagon, ‘a U.S.-based bulletproof hosting provider,’ 01:42–03:59 UTC on Aug 1, 2016; the botnet itself emerged about 40 minutes later, 04:37 UTC." href="https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity17/technical-sessions/presentation/antonakakis" />. Within one minute of the botnet emerging, 834 devices were scanning. Within ten minutes, 11,000 hosts were infected. Within twenty hours, the count stood at 64,500 — a doubling time of roughly 76 minutes. That's slower than classic desktop worms like Code Red (37 minutes) or Blaster (9 minutes), a gap the researchers attribute to the low compute and bandwidth of the devices themselves, and to the accuracy cost of a brute-force login versus a straight exploit.

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="600,000 was never the number behind the famous attacks">
The botnet's all-time peak of 600,000 devices came at the END of November 2016 — driven not by the original 61-password Telnet dictionary, but by a second, unrelated exploit that Mirai variants added after the source code went public: a remote-code-execution bug in routers' CPE WAN Management Protocol (CWMP), a remote-configuration service. The Krebs (Sept 21), OVH (mid-Sept), and Dyn (Oct 21) attacks — the ones that made global headlines — were launched from the earlier, smaller steady-state population of roughly 200,000–300,000 devices, essentially all recruited through the original Telnet credential dictionary. It's a stronger version of this piece's thesis, not a weaker one: even the SECOND spike came from the same root cause — a management protocol left open and exploitable by default — not a new class of mistake.
</Callout>

## The supply-chain argument, stated plainly

Here's the case this teardown has been building toward. Every mechanism above — the stateless scanner, the loader's clean separation of concerns, the 61-entry dictionary, even the botnet's failure modes — is *good engineering* aimed at a target that shouldn't have existed: hundreds of thousands of internet-facing devices with a remote-management service turned on by default and, for a meaningful fraction of them, no way for the owner to turn it off.

XiongMai's own recall after the attacks makes the scale of that mismatch concrete — in the opposite direction from what you'd expect. The company said it would recall millions of devices, mainly network cameras, sold in the U.S., and separately promised to strengthen password functions and patch older models <Annotation client:load term="recall millions of devices" note="Krebs on Security, ‘IoT Device Maker Vows Product Recall, Legal Action Against Western Accusers’ (Oct 2016): ‘XiongMai...said it would be issuing a recall on millions of devices — mainly network cameras.’ The Register, ‘Chinese electronics biz recalls webcams at heart of botnet DDoS woes’ (Oct 24, 2016), separately reported Xiongmai's promise to strengthen password functions and issue a patch for earlier products, without giving a device count of its own." href="https://krebsonsecurity.com/2016/10/iot-device-maker-vows-product-recall-legal-action-against-western-accusers/" />. Millions of devices sounds like it should dwarf a botnet that peaked at 600,000 — but the mismatch is structural, not numerical: XiongMai never sold those millions as XiongMai-branded product. It sold circuit boards, wholesale, to dozens of downstream brands who stamped their own logos on the housing and never touched the firmware underneath. A recall aimed at "XiongMai-branded product" cannot reach a vulnerability sold as a component and relabeled a hundred times over, no matter how large the number attached to the recall announcement is.

That's the structural point the researchers themselves land on in their own discussion: patchable-code defenses assume a maintained update channel and a single accountable vendor, and IoT's white-label economics routinely has neither.

<Pullquote cite="Antonakakis et al., §7 (Discussion)" href="https://www.usenix.org/conference/usenixsecurity17/technical-sessions/presentation/antonakakis" kind="document">
The Mirai botnet demonstrated that even an unsophisticated dictionary attack could compromise hundreds of thousands of Internet-connected devices... IoT devices are much more heterogeneous and, from a security perspective, mostly neglected.
</Pullquote>

Contrast that with how desktop security actually improved. Windows, iOS, and Android each concentrate accountability in one vendor who can push a patch to every unit overnight — the entire premise of automatic updates. A white-labeled DVR chipset has no equivalent: no one company owns the fix, no one channel reaches every relabeled unit, and in the worst cases, no interface exists for the owner to intervene even if they wanted to. Mirai's 61 passwords weren't a coding flaw hiding in plain sight, waiting for a diligent engineer to find and fix. They were the visible tip of a manufacturing model that had no mechanism for "fix" to mean anything at all.

## The takeaway

If you build or evaluate connected hardware, the practical lesson isn't "use better passwords" — Mirai's targets often couldn't. It's these three, in order of leverage.

**Default-closed beats default-open, structurally.** A device that ships with remote management off, and requires deliberate action to turn it on, cannot be swept by a dictionary scanner no matter how good the dictionary is. Mirai's entire mechanism depended on Telnet already being reachable; take that assumption away and the 61 passwords are useless.

**"The owner can fix it" is not a security control if the owner has no path to fix it.** Any credential, key, or config value your product depends on for safety needs a real, working, discoverable way for the end user (or an automatic update mechanism) to change it — not a web panel that changes a cosmetic setting while the actual daemon underneath ignores it.

**Accountability has to survive white-labeling.** If your component ships inside other companies' branded products, a "recall" that only reaches your own logo reaches almost nobody. The chipset-level fix — not the downstream brand's fix — is the one that actually shrinks the vulnerable population, and it has to be built into how the component is licensed and updated from day one, not bolted on after 600,000 devices have already joined someone else's botnet.

Mirai is Japanese for "the future." The researchers who studied it chose to end their paper on that name deliberately — not as a boast about the malware, but as a warning about what happens next time a device category ships a hundred million units before anyone asks whether the factory password can be changed.
