A drug used by tens of millions of people gets a new safety flag: a peer-reviewed meta-analysis reports a statistically significant excess of a specific cancer. What do you actually know at that moment? Not as much as the headline implies, and not as little as a skeptic wants to believe — the honest answer sits in an uncomfortable middle, and getting to it requires reading the paper the way an epidemiologist does rather than the way a headline writer does. This is a skill, not a hot take, and it transfers to every drug-safety story you will ever read, not just the one below.

The case in front of us is real and current: GLP-1 receptor agonists — semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy), tirzepatide (Mounjaro, Zepbound), liraglutide (Victoza, Saxenda) and their relatives — are now used by roughly <Annotation client:load term="11% of US adults" note="Gallup poll, May 28–June 5 2026: 11% of US adults are current GLP-1 users, up from 3% in 2024; 15% report having used one for weight loss at some point." /> as of mid-2026, a scale that turns any cancer signal, real or spurious, into a public-health question that matters to tens of millions of households. This primer is not about whether to take a GLP-1 drug. It is about the machinery you need to read the *evidence* about GLP-1 drugs and cancer — and the same machinery reads every other drug-safety story you will meet afterward.

## The misconception that ruins every one of these headlines

Almost every reader treats a reported relative risk the same way: *the number is the danger.* "62% higher risk of thyroid cancer" reads like a near-doubling of your personal odds, especially when it comes with a "statistically significant" stamp and a 95% confidence interval that (barely) excludes 1.0. That reading is the single most common error in lay epidemiology, and it survives contact with peer review because relative risk and absolute risk are genuinely different quantities that happen to share the word "risk."

Run the numbers before you decide how scared to be. Thyroid cancer is <Annotation client:load term="rare" note="13.7 new thyroid cancer cases per 100,000 people per year, 2020–2024 US population data. SEER Cancer Stat Facts, National Cancer Institute." /> in the general population — about 13.7 new cases per 100,000 people per year. A relative risk of 1.62 sounds dramatic; converted into the same units as the base rate, it is a much smaller number than the headline implies, and the gap between those two framings is exactly where a scary-sounding but clinically tiny signal hides.

<RunnableCode client:visible caption="Predict the absolute gap first, then run it. Change baseRate and rr to match any other cancer/study pair in this primer."
  code={`// Thyroid cancer background rate: 13.7 cases per 100,000 people per year (SEER, 2020-2024).
const baseRatePer100k = 13.7;
const rr = 1.62;              // Duchemin et al. 2025 meta-analysis, 95% CI 1.08-2.44
const population = 100000;    // one year, one 100,000-person cohort

const untreatedCases = baseRatePer100k;
const treatedCases = baseRatePer100k * rr;
const excessCases = treatedCases - untreatedCases;

console.log("Untreated cohort: " + untreatedCases.toFixed(1) + " cases per 100,000 per year");
console.log("GLP-1 cohort (RR " + rr + "): " + treatedCases.toFixed(1) + " cases per 100,000 per year");
console.log("Absolute excess: " + excessCases.toFixed(1) + " extra cases per 100,000 per year");
console.log("Framed as relative risk: +" + Math.round((rr - 1) * 100) + "%");
console.log("Framed as absolute risk: +" + (excessCases / 1000).toFixed(4) + " percentage points");

return "same data, two framings, very different gut reactions";`} />

Both framings are true at once. "A 62% higher relative risk" and "8.5 extra cases per 100,000 people per year" describe the identical result, and neither one is more "correct" — but only the second tells you anything about how worried an individual patient should be. This primer's companion piece on <a href="/blog/hazard-ratios-not-risks">what a hazard ratio actually measures</a> goes deeper on the rate-versus-risk distinction generally; here the point is narrower and just as important: **a small absolute gap can still be a real, replicated, worth-investigating signal** — smallness in absolute terms does not mean the finding is fake, and "it's a small number" is not a rebuttal on its own. The right question is never "is this scary." It is "is this real, and if so, why."

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="The move this primer teaches">
Convert every relative risk to an absolute one before reacting to it — then, separately and just as carefully, ask whether the number is even real. Those are two different jobs, and skipping either one is how headlines mislead honest readers.
</Callout>

## Foundation: what a GLP-1 drug actually touches, and why cancer is even a plausible question

<Definition client:visible term="GLP-1" def="glucagon-like peptide-1, a gut hormone released after eating that triggers insulin secretion, slows stomach emptying, and signals fullness to the brain. GLP-1 drugs are synthetic analogs engineered to activate the same receptor for hours instead of minutes." /> is not, on its own, a cancer drug or a cancer risk — it is a hormone your body already makes. The reason cancer is even a reasonable question to ask is that the receptor it binds, GLP-1R, sits on far more tissue than the pancreas.

Follow the diagram left to right and the whole debate becomes legible. GLP-1 receptors are expressed not just in the pancreas but in the <Annotation client:load term="lung, stomach, intestine, liver, kidney, heart, blood vessels, and CNS regions including the hypothalamus and brainstem" note="Valencia-Rincón et al., J Clin Invest 2025 — GLP-1R tissue distribution reviewed from clinical and preclinical literature." />, which is exactly why a single hormone receptor can plausibly touch a dozen different cancer types through completely different mechanisms. Two of those mechanisms point toward *lower* cancer risk. Weight loss and reduced insulin resistance cut the mitogenic (growth-promoting) effect of chronically elevated insulin — a real, independently documented driver of endometrial, pancreatic, and breast cancer risk. Separately, and weight-loss-independent, GLP-1 receptor agonists appear to shift immune cells toward an antitumor posture — more M1-polarized macrophages, more tumor-infiltrating CD8+ T cells, fewer regulatory T cells — in preclinical models, even in lean mice that never had weight to lose. A third path, direct receptor stimulation of specific tissues, is where the *original* alarm came from: first-generation agonists drove parafollicular C-cell proliferation in rodent thyroid tissue, which is why GLP-1 drugs still carry a thyroid-tumor warning on the label. Whether that rodent finding translates to human thyroid cancer risk is precisely the contested question this primer is built around.

<Callout kind="note" title="The drug matters, not just the class">
These three paths do not weigh the same for every molecule. Liraglutide and exenatide (first-generation) are the agents tied to the original rodent C-cell and 2011 pancreatic-cancer alarms. Semaglutide showed tumor-*suppressive* effects in murine thyroid cancer models via the immune-modulation path. Tirzepatide (a dual GLP-1/GIP agonist) and retatrutide (an investigational triple GLP-1/GIP/glucagon agonist) show the strongest preclinical tumor suppression yet, in endometrial, breast, and pancreatic models — via Valencia-Rincón et al., J Clin Invest 2025.
</Callout>

That table is the first hard evidence that "GLP-1 drugs and cancer" is not one question. It is at minimum a four-by-many grid — drug, mechanism, cancer type, and (as the next section shows) study design — and collapsing that grid into a single headline number necessarily throws away the information that would let you interpret it correctly.

## The design of the study moves the number as much as the drug does

Here is the part almost no headline mentions: the *same* drug class, studied for the *same* cancer, in overlapping time periods, can produce opposite verdicts depending on how the comparison is built. This is not a metaphor — it happened inside a single published paper.

A 2025 meta-analysis pooling seven retrospective cohort studies (5,066,681 patients) on GLP-1 receptor agonists and colorectal cancer reported a pooled cohort-level relative risk of <Annotation client:load term="RR 2.31 (95% CI 1.82–2.93)" note="Zhong, Wu & Khan, BMC Gastroenterology 2025 — pooled analysis across 7 retrospective cohort studies, I² = 36%." /> — a harmful, statistically significant signal. The very same paper, pooling the *incidence* comparisons from the same underlying studies against other drugs, reported an odds ratio of <Annotation client:load term="OR 1.73 (95% CI 0.21–14.18, p = 0.61)" note="Zhong, Wu & Khan, BMC Gastroenterology 2025 — incidence-based pooling, I² = 100%, statistically null." /> — completely null, with a confidence interval so wide (0.21 to 14.18) it is nearly uninformative, and heterogeneity (I² = 100%) meaning the individual studies did not even roughly agree with each other. One paper. Two ways of aggregating overlapping data. A "significant, harmful" headline and a "no effect" footnote, both defensible readings of the identical literature.

That is not a fluke of one bad paper — it is what happens whenever retrospective, claims-database studies compare a drug against a *choice* of comparator, because the comparator's own patients are not a neutral background. A patient started on insulin is, on average, sicker and further into diabetes progression than one started on a GLP-1 drug; a patient on metformin might be closer in health status. If the comparator arm's *true* background cancer rate differs from the treatment arm's for reasons that have nothing to do with the drug — a phenomenon called confounding by indication — the apparent relative risk shifts even when the drug does nothing at all. Watch it happen in code, holding the GLP-1 arm's true rate completely fixed and changing only which comparator the study happened to choose:

<RunnableCode client:visible caption="The GLP-1 arm's true cancer rate never changes below. Only the comparator arm's background health does. Watch the computed RR swing anyway."
  code={`// The GLP-1 arm's TRUE colorectal cancer rate is fixed the whole time.
const glp1TrueRate = 40; // cases per 100,000 per year — never changes

// Three plausible comparator populations, each with a DIFFERENT background
// rate for reasons that have nothing to do with GLP-1 drugs at all:
// insulin users are typically sicker/longer-diabetic (higher background risk),
// SGLT2-inhibitor users are often healthier at initiation,
// and a "healthy user" comparator (e.g. lifestyle-only) skews healthier still.
const comparators = {
  "insulin (sicker population)":        70,
  "SGLT2 inhibitor (healthier population)": 48,
  "healthy-user baseline":               35,
};

for (const [name, comparatorRate] of Object.entries(comparators)) {
  const rr = glp1TrueRate / comparatorRate;
  console.log(name + ": comparator rate = " + comparatorRate +
              "/100k -> computed RR = " + rr.toFixed(2) +
              (rr < 1 ? "  (looks PROTECTIVE)" : "  (looks HARMFUL)"));
}

return "same drug, same true rate, three different verdicts";`} />

Run it and the GLP-1 arm never moved — only the comparator did — and the study's verdict flipped from "harmful" to "protective" purely as an artifact of which other drug's users happened to populate the reference group. This is exactly why the colorectal-cancer literature looks protective against insulin (RR 0.57), still protective but less dramatically against SGLT2 inhibitors (RR 0.77) and thiazolidinediones (RR 0.82), and genuinely muddled when a different paper's pooling strategy collapses those distinct comparisons into one number. Randomized trials sidestep this specific problem by assignment, not by belief — which is exactly why the next section separates every effect size in this primer by study design, not just by cancer type.

<Detail summary="Go deeper: why retrospective cohorts can't just adjust the confounding away">
Statistical adjustment (propensity matching, multivariable regression) only controls for confounders the researchers measured and included. Confounding by indication is dangerous specifically because the reasons a physician chooses one diabetes drug over another — perceived frailty, other comorbidities, insurance formulary, how advanced the diabetes is — are often not fully captured in claims data, so residual confounding survives even careful adjustment. The target-trial-emulation design used in some of the pancreatic-cancer and ASCO 2025 studies below is a partial fix: it defines eligibility, treatment assignment, and follow-up as if it were a randomized trial, then applies the same propensity-matching logic — better than naive cohort comparison, but still not randomization.
</Detail>

Five things jump out, and each is a distinct lesson.

**Colorectal cancer looks protective, but the protection is comparator-dependent.** Every clean comparison against a specific drug class — insulin, SGLT2 inhibitors, thiazolidinediones — lands below 1.0 with a confidence interval that does not cross it. The moment a paper pools across mismatched comparators and switches metrics mid-analysis, the picture dissolves into the contradiction shown above. A 2025 target-trial-emulation study presented at the ASCO Annual Meeting (170,030 adults with diabetes and obesity, propensity-matched against DPP-4 inhibitors) adds an independent, differently-designed data point pointing the same protective direction: <Annotation client:load term="16% fewer colon cancer cases and 28% fewer rectal cancer cases" note="Mavromatis et al., presented at the 2025 ASCO Annual Meeting, Abstract 10507 — reported in ecancer's meeting coverage: 'There were 16% fewer colon cancer cases and 28% fewer rectal cancer cases in the group prescribed GLP-1 receptor agonists.' Presented data, not yet peer-reviewed." /> than the comparator arm, alongside an overall obesity-related-cancer hazard ratio of <Annotation client:load term="HR 0.93 (95% CI 0.88–0.98, p = .005)" note="Mavromatis et al., presented at the 2025 ASCO Annual Meeting, Abstract 10507 — not yet peer-reviewed. Target-trial emulation, 170,030 adults, 43 US health systems, 2013-2023, GLP-1RA vs DPP-4 inhibitor, mean follow-up 3.8-3.9 years." /> and an all-cause mortality hazard ratio of 0.92 (95% CI 0.87–0.97, p = .001) — with the benefit concentrated in women (8% lower cancer risk, 20% lower mortality) and no significant effect detected in men. <Sidenote>That data is presented, not yet peer-reviewed — real, but earlier in the evidence pipeline than a published meta-analysis, and worth re-checking once it clears review.</Sidenote>

**Pancreatic and breast cancer show no reliable signal in either direction.** The largest single meta-analysis of randomized trials — 48 RCTs, 94,245 participants, led by Albert Ko's group at Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health — found both squarely straddling 1.0, and its authors rated the certainty of "little or no effect" as *moderate*, not high, given how few actual cancer events even a dataset that size accumulates over a median 70-week follow-up. A separate real-world target-trial-emulation study (1,636,056 patients with type 2 diabetes, the TriNetX federated health-record network) found pancreatic cancer incidence *lower* with GLP-1 drugs across six different comparator classes (hazard ratios from 0.42 to 0.82) — consistent in direction with "not harmful," even as its own authors caution that "causal inferences cannot be made" from an observational design.

**Kidney cancer is the boundary case that tests whether you actually learned the checklist.** Ko's RCT meta-analysis puts it flat at null, OR 1.12 (95% CI 0.78–1.60). But a separate real-world cohort — the OneFlorida+ electronic-health-record network, 43,317 GLP-1RA users matched against 43,315 nonusers with obesity — reported <Annotation client:load term="HR 1.38 (95% CI 0.99–1.93, p = .04)" note="Bracha et al., JAMA Oncology, October 2025 — OneFlorida+ multicenter EHR network, n=86,632. The paper's own text calls this finding “marginally nonsignificant” despite p = .04, because the 95% CI's lower bound sits at 0.99." /> for kidney cancer — a p-value under the conventional 0.05 threshold, sitting right next to a confidence interval whose lower bound is 0.99, a hair below the null line. The paper's own authors call this "marginally nonsignificant," which is the correct call: when the p-value and the confidence-interval boundary nearly disagree like this, trust the interval, not the p-value alone, because the interval tells you the plausible range of true effects while the p-value collapses that whole range into a single yes/no threshold. The same OneFlorida+ study, on the *overall* obesity-related-cancer question, found a real, unambiguous benefit — HR 0.83 (95% CI 0.76–0.91, p = .002) — an independent real-world corroboration, using a different comparator (nonusers rather than DPP-4 inhibitors), of the same protective direction the ASCO 2025 data found for the drug class as a whole.

**Thyroid cancer is the one place three independent teams — different data, different countries, different authors, no shared funding — landed on the same statistically significant direction.** Duchemin and colleagues (84,156 trial participants, pooled RCTs) found RR 1.62 (95% CI 1.08–2.44); Silverii and colleagues (87,465 participants with thyroid-cancer events across 28 trials) found OR 1.55 (95% CI 1.05–2.27). Neither is a trial-based RCT re-analysis of the other — but a third team, working from an entirely different kind of evidence, reaches the same direction from real-world claims data rather than pooled trials: <Annotation client:load term="Bezin and colleagues (French SNDS national health-insurance database, 2,562 thyroid-cancer cases matched to 45,184 controls)" note="Bezin et al., Diabetes Care 2023;46(2):384-390 — nested case-control analysis, French national health insurance system (SNDS), type 2 diabetes patients on second-line antidiabetic drugs, 2006-2018." /> found <Annotation client:load term="adjusted HR 1.58 (95% CI 1.27–1.95)" note="Bezin et al. 2023 — GLP-1 receptor agonist use for 1-3 years vs. no use, adjusted for available confounders; medullary thyroid cancer specifically: HR 1.78, 95% CI 1.04-3.05. Neither confidence interval comes close to the alarm-bell language a "62% increase" implies on its own, and none of the three teams claims to have proven causation — but three independently conducted studies, using three different designs (trial meta-analysis, trial meta-analysis, national claims cohort), landing in the same direction, on the same rare outcome, is a real corroborated signal, not noise.

**And the largest single analysis, plus the one written by the drug's own manufacturer, both found the thyroid signal statistically indistinguishable from nothing.** Ko's 48-trial meta-analysis put thyroid cancer at OR 1.37 (95% CI 0.82–2.31) — crossing 1.0. Vilsbøll and colleagues, whose 2026 paper pooled 93 trials (101,732 participants) plus real-world claims data, reported HR 1.70 (95% CI 0.99–3.03) versus placebo and HR 1.83 (95% CI 0.70–6.71) versus active comparators — both crossing 1.0 — and concluded "the totality of data analysed did not suggest an association."

That conclusion deserves one more layer of scrutiny before you accept or dismiss it, and it is the layer a headline never gives you: six of the paper's nine authors are Novo Nordisk employees and shareholders — the manufacturer of liraglutide (Victoza, Saxenda) and semaglutide (Ozempic, Wegovy). <Annotation client:load term="Financial conflict, disclosed" note="Vilsbøll et al. 2026 conflict-of-interest statement: “Michael Stellfeld, Sune Dandanell, Jens-Peter David, Ceyda T. Symmetric skepticism, not selective skepticism, is the actual discipline here.

Vilsbøll and colleagues offer their own explanation for why a numeric excess shows up even in their data without being real: detection bias. Patients newly started on any closely-monitored drug get more medical attention, including more thyroid ultrasonography, in the months right after starting it — and 58% of the thyroid-cancer cases identified in their cardiovascular-outcome trials were diagnosed within the first year of treatment, exactly the window where "look harder, find more" would produce a spurious early spike with no true change in tumor biology.

## Where this leaves you

Collapse the whole grid into a mental model rather than a list. GLP-1 receptors sit on tissue across the entire body, so a single hormone-mimicking drug plausibly touches a dozen cancer types through at least three distinct mechanisms — metabolic (less mitogenic insulin signaling), immune (independent of weight loss), and direct receptor stimulation (the one rodent-model finding still unresolved in humans). Layer study design on top of biology and the picture sharpens rather than blurs: colorectal cancer looks genuinely protective in every clean, single-comparator analysis, muddies only when a paper pools mismatched comparators together; pancreatic and breast cancer sit flat at null in the largest, best-designed evidence; kidney cancer sits right on the boundary between null and real, depending on which design you ask; and thyroid cancer carries a real, three-times-independently-replicated excess signal whose own finders — on both the "it's real" and the "it's not" sides — refuse to call it proven, because a plausible detection-bias mechanism sits right next to a plausible biological one, and only more data resolves which is doing the work.

That is not an unsatisfying answer. It is the correct one, and it is available to anyone willing to read past the headline into the comparator, the design, the interval, and the funding line — the same six questions, asked in order, on every drug-safety story you will meet next. The next time a relative risk shows up in your feed, do not ask whether to be scared. Ask which of those six questions the article answered, and which ones it quietly skipped.
