A combine throws a fault code three days before rain, mid-harvest, and the part that's actually broken is a forty-dollar sensor. Swapping it takes twenty minutes with a wrench. Getting the machine to *recognize* the new sensor — to write its calibration into the engine control unit and clear the code — takes something else entirely: a piece of software called Service ADVISOR, and as of this writing, the fully-functional version of that software still exists on exactly one class of laptop in America, pending a federal judge's signature on an order that would change that. Not the farmer's. Not the independent mechanic's down the road. The dealer's.

That is the whole shape of a modern repair monopoly, and it is not a metaphor. For a decade, John Deere's <Annotation client:load term="diagnostic and calibration tool" note="Full-function Service ADVISOR is the tool the FTC's Jan. 15, 2025 complaint alleges is 'capable of performing all electronic repairs' on Deere equipment — restricted to authorized dealers. A separate, limited version, Customer Service ADVISOR, is what farmers and independent shops could get." href="https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2025/01/ftc-states-sue-deere-company-protect-farmers-unfair-corporate-tactics-high-repair-costs" /> was the only thing standing between a broken tractor and a working one, for any repair that touched the machine's electronics — and Deere controlled who could run it. On July 8, 2026, the Federal Trade Commission and five state attorneys general filed a proposed stipulated order in federal court to resolve an antitrust case built on exactly that fact. The Commission's own vote to issue it was 2-0, and the order now sits in the U.S. District Court for the Northern District of Illinois awaiting a judge's signature — stipulated orders, the FTC notes of its own filing, "have the force of law when approved and signed by the District Court judge," not before. The remedy it proposes is unusually specific, though: not a fine, not an apology, but a ten-year mandate to force Deere to open the firmware layer itself, once signed. This is a teardown of that layer — the actual embedded-systems mechanism a repair monopoly runs on — and of the five-year legal campaign it took to crack it open.

## A tractor is a distributed system wearing a hood

Here's the intuition that makes the rest of this legible: a modern Deere large tractor or combine isn't a mechanical machine with some electronics bolted on. It's a small distributed computer network that happens to also plow a field. A dedicated controller handles the engine, another the transmission, another the axle, another the diesel-particulate aftertreatment, another the operator's armrest console — an independent reverse-engineering project spent months <Annotation client:load term="cataloguing more than two dozen distinct controller message IDs" note="Tractorhacking.github.io's documentation project reverse-engineered the John Deere CAN bus protocol from Sept. 2017–Mar. 2018 and catalogued 28+ distinct proprietary CAN bus IDs, spanning engine, transmission, axle, and aftertreatment controllers." href="https://tractorhacking.github.io/documentation/" /> on the machine's shared <Annotation client:load term="CAN bus running the J1939 protocol" note="The controller-area-network standard the trucking and off-road-equipment industry uses for inter-controller messaging." href="https://tractorhacking.github.io/documentation/" />, the same kind of network a modern car uses, just with a plow instead of a steering wheel at the other end of some of those messages. Swap a sensor, and you haven't just replaced a mechanical part — you've introduced a new node onto that network, and every other node has to agree to talk to it.

That agreement is where the lock lives, and it's worth drawing the actual shape of it before naming the fault codes it produces — the diagram below is an illustrative synthesis of the documented pieces (the CAN-bus controllers, the shared VIN check, the dealer-only diagnostic client, and the calibration store), not a leaked schematic.

Deere's own equipment throws specific, documented fault codes when the VIN check at the center of that diagram doesn't resolve automatically — codes like <Annotation client:load term="ECU 002006.14" note="A VIN Information Mismatch fault: the Engine Interface Controller's stored VIN and the Engine Control Unit's stored VIN disagree, on machines with an immobilizer system that uses VIN matching as a security check." href="https://www.news-jd.com/fault-codes/ecu-002006-14-vin-information-mismatch-john-deere-system-advisory/" /> and <Annotation client:load term="ACU 000237.02" note="A VIN Security Data Conflict fault on the armrest/operator console controller — the same VIN-matching immobilizer check, tripped on a different controller." href="https://www.news-jd.com/fault-codes/acu-000237-02-acu-237-02-vin-security-data-conflict/" /> — both variations on the same idea: a Vehicle Identification Number is stored redundantly across controllers as a security check, and when a swapped or reflashed part doesn't match, the machine doesn't just log an error, it degrades. Function gets limited "to ensure security" until the mismatch is resolved with dealer-level programming tools. That's not a bug. It's the immobilizer doing exactly its documented job — the same VIN-matching architecture a modern car uses to stop a stolen ignition from starting a stranger's engine, repurposed to decide who's allowed to finish a repair.

Let's model that check directly, because it's small enough to run and it explains the entire rest of this piece in eleven lines.

<RunnableCode client:visible caption="A simplified model of the VIN-pairing check documented in Deere's own fault codes — not Deere's actual source, but the same logic: same mismatch, different outcome depending on who holds the credential."
  code={`// Two controllers on the CAN bus each store a copy of the machine's VIN.
// A part swap without re-pairing produces a mismatch (per ECU 002006.14 /
// ACU 000237.02). What happens next depends on ONE thing: who's asking.
function checkPairing(engineVIN, otherControllerVIN, hasDealerCredential) {
  const paired = engineVIN === otherControllerVIN;

  if (paired) return "VIN MATCH -> normal operation";

  if (hasDealerCredential) {
    return "MISMATCH -> Service ADVISOR (dealer) re-pairs the VIN -> function restored";
  }
  return "MISMATCH -> no dealer credential -> fault code stands, function DEGRADED";
}

console.log(checkPairing("1M0S1234ABC", "1M0S1234ABC", false));
console.log(checkPairing("1M0S1234ABC", "1M0S9999XYZ", false));
console.log(checkPairing("1M0S1234ABC", "1M0S9999XYZ", true));
return "Identical mismatch. Two outcomes. The credential is the fix — not a tool, not a skill, not a part.";`} />

Run it and notice what *doesn't* change between the second and third line: the mismatch itself. The sensor is the same sensor, the wiring is identical, the mechanical work is done either way. The only variable that flips the outcome from "degraded" to "restored" is whether the machine recognizes the credential of whoever's holding the diagnostic laptop. That's the entire monopoly, compressed into one boolean.

## The four layers a "restricted repair" has to pass through

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="Why “restricted repair” is the FTC's term of art">
The complaint doesn't allege Deere blocks *all* repair — it alleges a specific, narrower monopoly over what it calls "restricted repairs": the subset that requires reading, clearing, or writing something inside the equipment's electronic control units. A farmer has always been free to replace a tire. The fight is entirely about the layer where a physical fix has to be confirmed in software before the machine will act on it.
</Callout>

Stack those layers up and the architecture of the lock becomes obvious — each one is a real, separate step, and Deere historically controlled the bottom two outright.

Notice the inversion: the layer that's hardest to fake — cutting metal, swapping a physical sensor — is the one layer nobody needed permission for. The layer that's *easiest* to fake, cryptographically speaking, was the one Deere fenced off entirely. That's not an engineering necessity. It's a business decision wearing an engineering costume, and the clearest proof of that claim is what happened when someone actually tried to fake it.

## The "limited" tool wasn't a lesser version — it stopped at the fence deliberately

Deere didn't leave farmers with nothing. Since 2022 it has sold a scaled-down tool, Customer Service ADVISOR, and it is worth being precise about exactly where its limits sit, because the precision is the evidence. Deere's own documentation describes what the customer-facing tool does: <Annotation client:load term="“connect to machines...to clear and refresh all codes, take diagnostic readings and perform limited machine calibrations”" note="John Deere's own product description of Customer Service ADVISOR's capabilities, as reported in a Jul. 2022 trade-press writeup of the tool's launch." href="https://www.farm-equipment.com/articles/20561-john-deere-customer-service-advisor" /> — note the qualifier sitting inside Deere's own phrase: *limited* calibrations. The FTC's complaint is more direct about what "limited" excludes: it alleges Customer Service ADVISOR is an inferior tool that farmers and independent shops can buy, but that it "cannot diagnose, test, or calibrate for" the class of repairs the complaint calls <Annotation client:load term="“restricted” repairs" note="Per a law-firm summary of the complaint's own language: restricted repairs 'require access to the underlying software and/or coding' that the limited customer tool doesn't expose." href="https://www.regulatoryoversight.com/2025/03/ftc-states-sue-john-deere-in-right-to-repair-lawsuit/" /> — precisely the electronic-calibration and VIN-security layers from the stack above. You could buy a version of Service ADVISOR. It just stopped exactly at the fence, by design, every time.

That distinction is what turns "Deere sells a repair tool to everyone" from a defense into evidence for the other side. A company that wanted to prevent unsafe repairs would gate *unsafe actions*. A company that gates an entire *class of actions* — including the safe, ordinary ones, like calibrating a replacement sensor — regardless of whether the specific action is risky, is gating something else: who gets paid to do the work.

## What the fence is actually made of

In August 2022, a security researcher who goes by Sick Codes took a John Deere <Annotation client:load term="2630 and 4240 touchscreen display" note="Sick Codes' DEF CON 30 talk demonstrated the jailbreak on these two widely-used Deere touchscreen controller models." href="https://www.thedrive.com/news/hacker-gains-access-to-tractor-diagnostics-with-new-john-deere-hack" /> apart on stage at DEF CON. He didn't need Deere's cooperation, a leaked credential, or months of cryptanalysis. He wired a microcontroller directly into the display's circuit board and used it to make the touchscreen believe it was talking to a certified Deere dealer — full stop. Once that belief was established, the device handed over root-level control: he could read, write, and change anything on it. He proved the point the way security researchers always prove a point, by running Doom on it.

What made the forgery possible wasn't a subtle cryptographic weakness — it was the near-total absence of cryptography at that checkpoint. In his own words describing the flaw: <Annotation client:load term="“nothing's encrypted or checksummed properly”" note="Sick Codes, quoted describing the root cause of the jailbreak he demonstrated at DEF CON 30 (Aug. 2022)." href="https://www.theregister.com/2022/08/16/john_deere_doom/" />. Once physical access to the board was available, unsigned code executed without any integrity check catching it. That's a genuinely different failure than "the encryption was crackable" — it's closer to a lock that isn't attached to the door. The security researcher didn't defeat the dealer-authentication gate. He proved the gate was a suggestion.

## What the July 2026 proposed order would require — and where it can still be gamed

<Pullquote cite="Terms of the stipulated order, per the FTC's Jul. 8, 2026 release and Minnesota AG's parallel release" kind="document">
Deere must make available to farmers and independent repair providers the same equipment repair resources — including applicable software capabilities — that it currently provides to authorized Deere dealers: reading, clearing, and resetting electronic fault codes; reprogramming of electronic components, including part "pairing"; restarting a machine following an emissions-related shutdown; and viewing technical manuals and troubleshooting solutions.
</Pullquote>

Read that clause against the four-layer stack from earlier and the settlement's precision becomes obvious: it doesn't just say "give farmers a tool." It names the *layers*. "Reprogramming... including part pairing" is the electronic-calibration and VIN-security layers, explicitly. "Restarting a machine following an emissions-related shutdown" is the exact "limp mode" behavior a modern diesel engine's <Annotation client:load term="selective catalytic reduction and Diesel Exhaust Fluid aftertreatment system" note="The EPA's Feb. 2026 guidance specifically covers 'all nonroad diesel engines equipped with advanced emission control technologies including selective catalytic reduction and inducement systems as well as Diesel Exhaust Fluid (DEF) system repairs.'" href="https://www.epa.gov/newsreleases/epa-advances-farmers-right-repair-their-own-equipment-saving-repair-costs-and" /> triggers when it detects tampering or a fault — deliberately throttling the machine, not just logging an error. For years, that emissions-compliance shutdown was also Deere's strongest engineering-sounding argument for keeping the reset tool dealer-only: touch the wrong setting and you've defeated a federal emissions control. The EPA's own February 2026 guidance took that argument off the table months before the FTC's proposed order was even filed in July, clarifying that the Clean Air Act permits <Annotation client:load term="“temporary overrides of emission control systems”" note="EPA guidance (Feb. 2, 2026): 'temporary overrides of emission control systems are allowed when it is for the purpose of repair.'" href="https://www.epa.gov/newsreleases/epa-advances-farmers-right-repair-their-own-equipment-saving-repair-costs-and" /> specifically "for the purpose of repair" — meaning resetting a shutdown to finish a fix was never actually the compliance risk it had been treated as. This proposed order was written by people who understood the mechanism this piece just walked through, not just the symptom.

As proposed, it would run for ten years under FTC and state supervision, with compliance reports due <Annotation client:load term="every 60 days during rollout, then annually" note="Per legal-alert summaries of the stipulated order from Axinn LLP and Freshfields." href="https://www.axinn.com/en/insights/axinn-viewpoints/right-to-repair-alert-deere-company-reaches-settlement-with-ftc-states" />, and it would survive Deere's own future product roadmap: any new repair tool Deere builds would have to be extended to farmers and independent shops once it reaches <Annotation client:load term="50% of authorized dealer locations" note="Per the Minnesota AG's release and the Freshfields/Axinn legal summaries of the order's terms." href="https://www.ag.state.mn.us/Office/Communications/2026/07/08_John-Deere.asp" /> — all of it contingent on the district judge's signature. (Minnesota's own AG release describes the deal in more conclusive terms than the FTC's own filing does; this piece follows the FTC's own characterization of its filing status.)

<Callout kind="caution" title="The clause worth watching">
That 50%-dealer-rollout trigger is also the settlement's most obvious pressure point. It answers "when must Deere share a new tool" with a number tied to *Deere's own distribution decisions* — which means the pace at which Deere chooses to roll a new capability out to its own dealer network directly controls how long farmers and independent shops wait for the same capability. It's not a loophole in the sense of letting Deere refuse forever; it's a lever that lets Deere set the clock.
</Callout>

Worth being precise about what this proposed settlement is not, too. Once signed, it would resolve the FTC's federal antitrust case — the one built entirely on the monopolization theory this piece has been tracing. It is a different instrument from the <Annotation client:load term="$99 million multidistrict class-action settlement" note="Agreed Apr. 6, 2026, covering farmers who paid Deere or its dealers for large-equipment repairs from Jan. 10, 2018 forward — a damages fund, explicitly stated to have no effect on the FTC's separate case." href="https://topclassactions.com/lawsuit-settlements/lawsuit-news/john-deere-class-action-settlement-includes-99m-right-to-repair-changes/" />, independently confirmed at <Annotation client:load term="$99 million plus 3.95% annual interest" note="A separate legal summary of the multidistrict class settlement confirms the $99M-plus-interest figure and the Jan. 10, 2018 class-period start date." href="https://nationalaglawcenter.org/john-deere-agrees-to-settle-antitrust-lawsuit/" />, which pays farmers back for repairs they already overpaid for. One case buys the future open; the other pays for the past. Neither one required Congress or a single state legislature to pass anything — which is precisely the gap Colorado's lone ag-specific statute, and every stalled bill next to it in that table, left for an antitrust complaint to fill.

## The takeaway: a repair lock is a business decision, not an engineering one

Every claim in Deere's defense of its old system — safety, emissions compliance, warranty integrity, data security — describes a real engineering concern. None of them requires that the *authentication credential itself* exist only on a dealer's laptop. You can check a VIN match without deciding who's allowed to hold the key that resolves a mismatch. You can protect a calibration file's integrity without deciding that integrity can only be certified by the seller. The four-layer stack from earlier makes the tell visible: mechanical work needed no permission at all, and the layer that needed the *most* trust — writing to the control unit that runs safety and emissions logic — was gated not by a stronger cryptographic proof, but by Sick Codes' account, by almost none.

If you're building anything with an update mechanism, a pairing check, or a "restart after safety shutdown" flow — a drone, a home battery, a piece of medical equipment — the lesson from a decade of tractor litigation isn't "add authentication." It's "ask who authentication actually serves before you ship it." A VIN-matching immobilizer that only a manufacturer's own credential can clear isn't a safer design than one an owner can clear with proof of ownership — it's the same design, with one extra business rule bolted onto the security layer. The fault codes this piece opened with, ECU 002006.14 and ACU 000237.02, will still fire the same way regardless of how the case ends. What would change, once a judge signs the FTC's proposed order, is who's allowed to hold the tool that clears them.

Deere's proposed settlement would answer that question by court order — once signed — for ten years, on this one product line, under FTC and state supervision, with a 50%-dealer-rollout clause worth watching for the next decade of new tools. Nothing about the mechanism it constrains is unique to farm equipment — the four layers in the stack above exist, in some form, in every device that pairs a replaceable part to a controller and checks a credential before trusting it. The tractor just happened to be the machine expensive enough, essential enough, and litigated long enough for regulators to finally take the stack apart in public.
