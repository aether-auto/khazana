Here is a number that should not survive contact with a single cable news segment about kids today: the American teen birth rate has fallen 81% since 1991. Not stagnated. Not wobbled. Fell, for thirty-four straight years, through the crack epidemic's hangover, through 9/11, through the 2008 recession, through a pandemic, through the entire rise of the smartphone — a slope so consistent that a demographer looking only at the line would guess it was manufactured. It was not. It is the Youth Risk Behavior Survey and the National Vital Statistics System, run by the same federal government every two years since 1991, and it says the generation everyone worries about is, on the metrics that most worried their parents, the safest one on record.

Read the shape before the number. All four lines start at the same point by construction and end somewhere between a fifth and three-fifths of where they started — but they don't fall at the same rate, and the gaps between them are the whole argument of this piece. Cigarettes and pregnancy collapsed hardest. Sex fell the least. That asymmetry is not noise; it is a fingerprint, and it points at a specific mechanism rather than a generic "kids got more responsible" story.

Hold those four next to each other, because the entire rest of this dispatch is an argument about why they moved together — and why nobody has fully proven the reason.

## Thirty years, the same direction

The fall did not happen in one clean decade. It happened in three distinct chapters, each with its own candidate explanation, and the order they arrive in matters more than any single chapter read alone.

That is the single fact every theory below has to survive.

## The gap the naive story misses

The easy, popular explanation for a falling teen-pregnancy rate is that teenagers are simply having less sex — abstinence, not technology. The data says otherwise, and the size of the gap between "how much less sex" and "how much less pregnancy" is the tell.

<Math client:visible tex="\text{pregnancy risk} \;\approx\; \text{sexual activity} \times (1 - \text{contraceptive effectiveness})"

Run the numbers against that equation and the gap between the sex line and the pregnancy line in the opening chart stops looking like a coincidence.

If declining sexual activity were doing most of the work, the pregnancy line and the sex line would fall at roughly the same rate — a teen having sex is a teen at risk of pregnancy, in a simple model. They don't. Sex fell 41% from its 1991 level; pregnancy fell 81%. Something other than "less sex" is absorbing the other forty points of decline, and the researchers who have actually decomposed this arithmetic have an answer.

This is the single best-corroborated causal claim in this entire piece, because it shows up twice, in two non-overlapping time windows studied by different research teams using different survey waves — 1995–2002 and 2007–2014 — and both times the answer is the same: better contraception, not less sex, is what actually moved the teen pregnancy rate. It is a satisfying, well-evidenced answer to one specific question. It is also, on its own, an incomplete answer to the bigger one, because contraceptive technology has nothing to do with why teens are also drinking less, smoking less, fighting less, and — per the YRBS's own numbers — reporting far fewer lifetime sexual partners even when they do have sex: the share reporting four or more lifetime partners fell from <Annotation client:load term="15% in 2013 to 6% in 2021" note="CDC YRBS Data Summary & Trends Report, 2013-2023: percentage of high school students reporting four or more lifetime sexual partners fell from 15% (2013) to 11% (2015), 10% (2017), 9% (2019), and 6% (2021), holding flat at 6% in 2023." />. Contraception explains the pregnancy arithmetic. It does not explain the mood.

## So contraception explains the pregnancy math. What explains everything else?

Two broader hypotheses try to answer that harder question — a whole generation getting more cautious across categories that have nothing to do with birth control. Both are real research programs with real evidence behind them. Both are also more contested than their popular retellings admit, and the honest version of this dispatch has to show the contest, not resolve it.

## Hypothesis two: a generation with less lead in its blood

The lead-abatement hypothesis starts from a genuinely striking fact: leaded gasoline was phased out under the Clean Air Act starting in the late 1970s, childhood blood-lead levels fell sharply in its wake, and lead is a documented neurotoxin linked to impulse-control problems and impaired executive function. If a whole cohort of children absorbed measurably less lead than the cohort before them, and lead damages exactly the brain systems that govern risk-taking, you would expect that cohort to show up in the data fifteen to twenty years later as measurably less impulsive — including, plausibly, less unprotected sex and less youth crime.

<Pullquote cite="Rick Nevin, Environmental Research 83(1), 2000" href="https://mpra.ub.uni-muenchen.de/35324/" kind="document">
Long-term trends in population exposure to gasoline lead were found to be remarkably consistent with subsequent changes in violent crime and unwed pregnancy.
</Pullquote>

Nevin's 2000 paper is the specific study behind this hypothesis as it applies to teen pregnancy, not just crime, and it is genuinely the origin of the idea that a whole generation's brains were less lead-damaged than their parents'. The more rigorous econometric treatment of the parallel crime claim came seven years later from economist Jessica Wolpaw Reyes, who used state-by-state variation in how fast each state phased out leaded gasoline to isolate the effect.

<Detail summary="Go deeper: how Reyes isolated the lead effect from everything else that changed in the 1990s">
Reyes's identification strategy exploits the fact that states adopted unleaded gasoline at different speeds for reasons unrelated to their crime trends — mostly driven by vehicle-fleet turnover and refinery logistics — so a state that cut childhood lead exposure faster in, say, 1975 should show a bigger crime drop specifically around 1995-2000, twenty years later, when that less-exposed cohort reaches peak crime-committing age. Joining lead-exposure measures from 1955-1990 to crime rates twenty years on, Reyes found a 10% change in gasoline-lead exposure corresponded to an 8% change in violent crime two decades later — and concluded the phase-out was responsible for approximately a 56% decline in US violent crime between 1992 and 2002.
</Detail>

Fifty-six percent is a large, specific, headline-ready number, and it is also the number that a subsequent peer-reviewed reckoning has substantially cut down to size.

Higney, Hanley, and Moro's 2022 meta-analysis in *Regional Science and Urban Economics* pooled twenty-four separate lead-crime studies and found the underlying literature had been <Annotation client:load term="overstated by publication bias" note="Higney, Hanley & Moro (2022): pooled partial correlation of 0.16 and elasticity of 0.09 across 24 lead-crime studies; bias-corrected estimates imply lead abatement explains roughly 7-28% of the US homicide decline, versus Reyes's single-study 56% figure for violent crime overall." /> — the pattern where studies finding a large, dramatic lead-crime link were more likely to get published and cited than studies finding a small or null one. Their corrected estimate: lead abatement plausibly explains somewhere between 7% and 28% of the US homicide decline, not 56% of the broader violent-crime decline. That is still a real, nonzero effect — it is just roughly a quarter to a half the size of the number that made the hypothesis famous.

<Callout kind="caution" title="The pregnancy-specific version has an even bigger problem: timing">
Reyes's rigorous work is about crime, not pregnancy — the extension to unwed teen pregnancy is Nevin's weaker, more correlational claim. Critics examining that specific extension have flagged a real mismatch: gasoline-lead levels fell relatively slowly after 1990, while the teen unwed-conception rate kept plunging sharply through the 1990s and 2000s. If lead exposure were the dominant driver of the pregnancy decline specifically, the two curves should track each other's *rate of change*, not just their direction — and on the pregnancy side, they visibly don't line up as cleanly as the crime-side correlation does.
</Callout>

None of this means the lead-abatement story is wrong. Childhood lead exposure almost certainly did something to the cohorts who grew up with far less of it in their blood than their parents did, and a 7-to-28% contribution to one major social outcome is not a small effect at a population scale. What it means is that this hypothesis, as it's usually retold — "we banned leaded gas and a generation got less impulsive, full stop" — is doing more work in the popular version than the peer-reviewed version can currently support, especially for the specific outcome this dispatch is about.

## Hypothesis three: the kids stayed home

The third candidate is the one dominating the current conversation about teenagers, and it has the opposite evidentiary problem from lead: strong, recent, well-measured correlational data, and a timing mismatch that is, if anything, more obvious than the lead hypothesis's.

Psychologist Jean Twenge and coauthors Brian Spitzberg and W. Keith Campbell analyzed nationally representative surveys of 8.2 million American adolescents and college-bound students from 1976 to 2017, tracking time spent on in-person socializing — getting together with friends, going to parties, dating, driving around for fun — against time spent on digital media. Their finding: face-to-face socializing among teens fell steadily across the whole period, but the fall accelerated sharply after roughly 2010, and by 2016, college-bound high school seniors were spending about <Annotation client:load term="an hour less per day" note="Twenge, Spitzberg & Campbell (2019), Journal of Social and Personal Relationships: college-bound high school seniors in 2016 reported roughly one hour less per day of face-to-face peer interaction than Gen X seniors did in the late 1980s. N=8.2 million, 1976-2017." /> on face-to-face interaction with peers than Gen X seniors had in the late 1980s. Twenge's own later popular account put a sharper number on the acceleration: the share of teens who said they got together with friends nearly every day <Annotation client:load term="dropped by more than 40%" note="Twenge, 'Have Smartphones Destroyed a Generation?' The Atlantic, September 2017 — reporting the share of teens getting together with friends nearly every day fell by more than 40% from 2000 to 2015, with the decline steepening after roughly 2010." /> between 2000 and 2015, with most of that steepening concentrated in the smartphone-adoption years.

The timeline lines up with the technology in one specific sense: American adult smartphone ownership was still a minority behavior — 35% — as late as May 2011, and did not cross the halfway mark until June 2013. That is precisely the window in which several of this piece's steepest recent declines — alcohol use falling from 29.2% to 22.7% between 2019 and 2021 alone, in-person socializing falling by Twenge's account — show up most sharply.

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="A screen-mediated cohort, but a cohort-level pattern only">
Twenge, Spitzberg, and Campbell are explicit about a critical limitation in their own data: the displacement between digital media and face-to-face interaction holds at the <em>cohort</em> level — across the whole population, in-person interaction fell as digital-media use rose — but NOT at the <em>individual</em> level, where heavier social-media users are not the teens with the least face-to-face time. That asymmetry is exactly what you'd expect if something about the whole social environment shifted, rather than digital media directly cannibalizing any one teen's hours with friends.
</Callout>

And yet the seed observation that opened this whole inquiry cuts hard against reading screens as the *origin* of the decline, even as an accelerant. The underlying declines in teen pregnancy, sexual activity, and substance use are visible in the data starting in the early-to-mid 1990s — <Annotation client:load term="long before broadband, let alone Instagram" note="Vox Future Perfect, 'The forgotten success story of America's teenagers': the underlying teen risk-behavior declines are visible in CDC data beginning in the 1990s, well before broadband internet access was common and roughly two decades before Instagram (launched 2010) or widespread teen smartphone ownership." />. A mechanism that only becomes physically possible around 2010-2013 cannot be the reason a decline that started around 1991-1995 began. It can plausibly be one of the reasons the decline kept going, or even accelerated, after 2010 — the timing for that narrower claim fits reasonably well — but "smartphones caused the decline" and "smartphones may have deepened an already-fifteen-year-old decline" are very different claims, and the popular version of this hypothesis rarely distinguishes between them.

<Detail summary="Go deeper: what a 'slow life strategy' theory adds, and what it doesn't resolve">
Twenge and independent writer Adam Mastroianni have separately advanced a broader framing sometimes called a "slow life strategy": as teen lives got objectively longer, safer, and materially richer across the late twentieth century, the expected cost of any given risky act rose too, so the same cost-benefit calculation that once tolerated more risk now tolerates less. It's an appealing unifying frame — it can explain a decline that predates smartphones (rising material security started well before 2010) while still leaving room for screens to matter later. But it is closer to an economic intuition than a directly tested, quantified hypothesis with its own independent dataset the way the contraception and lead stories have, and it should be read as a candidate synthesis rather than a fourth confirmed mechanism.
</Detail>

## Is some of this just a worse survey?

There is a fourth possibility this dispatch would be dishonest to skip, and it is not about teenagers at all — it is about the survey measuring them. The Youth Risk Behavior Survey is the backbone of almost every number in this piece, and the survey itself has been quietly getting harder to trust in its most recent waves.

This is a real, quantified problem with the most recent leg of the trend — plausibly explaining why "ever had sex" dipped to 30% in the 2021 wave and then ticked back up to 32% in 2023, rather than continuing to fall in a straight line. It is much harder to use this to explain away the entire thirty-year decline, which shows up consistently across a dozen-plus survey waves, two independent MMWR report series, a separate federal indicators compilation (childstats.gov), and — for pregnancy specifically — an entirely different data system (birth certificates via the National Vital Statistics System, which has nothing to do with survey response rates at all). The methodology problem is real. It is a caveat on the shape of the last five years, not a refutation of the last thirty.
</Callout>

## What got worse while everything else got better

A piece this optimistic about teenagers needs one honest boundary drawn around its own claim, because not every youth-safety indicator moved in the same direction, and pretending otherwise would be its own kind of dishonesty.

Overall teen mortality fell from <Annotation client:load term="68.6 to 44.8 deaths per 100,000" note="CDC MMWR QuickStats, National Vital Statistics System: death rate for teens aged 15-19 fell from 68.6 per 100,000 in 1999 to 44.8 per 100,000 in 2013, a decline of 34.7%." /> teens aged 15-19 between 1999 and 2013 — a decline of roughly 35%, tracking much of the same window as everything else in this piece. But that curve is U-shaped, not monotonic: CDC's own mortality tracking shows the broader 15-24 all-cause death rate jumping <Annotation client:load term="20.8% in a single year — from 69.7 to 84.2 per 100,000, 2019 to 2020" note="CDC NCHS Data Brief No. 427, 'Mortality in the United States, 2020': all-cause death rate for ages 15-24 rose 20.8%, from 69.7 per 100,000 in 2019 to 84.2 in 2020. CDC NCHS Data Brief No. 471 separately shows the homicide rate for ages 15-19 rose 91% from 2014 through 2021 (to 12.8 per 100,000), and the 15-19 suicide rate rose 57% from 7.5 (2009) to 11.8 (2017)." />, driven by a mix of COVID-era disruption, a sharp rise in homicide and overdose deaths, and an already-rising suicide rate. Self-reported persistent sadness or hopelessness among high schoolers, similarly, rose from <Annotation client:load term="26.1% in 2009 to a peak of 42% in 2021, easing only slightly to 40% in 2023" note="CDC YRBS: percentage of high school students reporting persistent feelings of sadness or hopelessness in the past year rose from 26.1% (2009) to 36.7% (2019) to 42% (2021), before easing to 40% (2023), per the MMWR Supplement on Mental Health and Suicide Risk (su7304a9) and CDC's 2023 YRBS results summary." /> — the one major indicator in this dataset that moved in the opposite direction from everything else.

This dispatch's claim is specifically about the syndrome documented in the opening chart — teen pregnancy, sexual activity, drinking, smoking, and hard-drug use — where the government's own decades-long data series are unambiguous and mutually corroborating. It is not a claim that every dimension of adolescent well-being improved without reversal. Physical risk-taking cratered. Mental health, on the same government's own numbers, moved the other way for at least part of the same period. Both things are true at once, and a dispatch that only told you the first one would be cherry-picking as badly as the panic narrative it's trying to correct.

## So what

Line up what the data actually supports, because the honest answer here is less satisfying than either the "kids are ruined" narrative or a single tidy explanation for why they aren't.

The decline itself is not in serious dispute. It shows up in survey data and in birth-certificate data, across federal agencies that don't coordinate their numbers, over three decades and multiple presidential administrations, indifferent to recessions and to a pandemic. Whatever caused it, "the decline is fake" is the one hypothesis this evidence rules out cleanly.

The best-evidenced *mechanism* — not root cause, mechanism — is that better contraceptive technology, adopted more consistently, explains the overwhelming majority of the drop specifically in teen pregnancy: 86% of it, by the most careful decomposition available, with essentially no change in how much sex teens were having. That is a genuinely rigorous, twice-replicated finding, and it should get more credit in the popular conversation than it does.

But contraception cannot explain why drinking, smoking, fighting, and hard-drug use fell in the same window, by similar or larger margins, among the same generation. For that broader pattern, this dispatch found two real research programs and no proof. The lead-abatement hypothesis has a rigorous econometric backbone for crime specifically, but its own peer-reviewed meta-analysis cut the headline effect size by half to three-quarters, and the extension to teen pregnancy specifically runs into a timing mismatch its advocates haven't resolved. The screen-mediated-socializing hypothesis has strong, recent, well-measured correlational data — but the underlying decline began roughly fifteen to twenty years before the technology it's blamed on existed in any teen's pocket, which makes it a plausible accelerant of a trend already in motion, not a plausible first cause.

The honest state of the field, three decades in, is this: researchers can tell you with high confidence *that* American teenagers got dramatically safer, and can tell you with genuine rigor *how* the pregnancy piece specifically happened. Nobody has closed the case on *why* the whole syndrome — sex, drinking, drugs, fighting — moved together, starting before any of the leading suspects were in a position to cause it. That is not a failure of the research. It is what an honest, still-open scientific question looks like from the inside, and it is a more interesting place to end than either a triumphant just-so story or a moral panic that the data does not support.
