The metronome never stops. From a single loudspeaker bolted to a lamppost on
Nevsky Prospekt, it ticks through the dark — sixty beats a minute when the city
is calm, faster when the bombers are coming. Tonight it is slow. Tonight, that
is the only mercy.

Anna pulls the blanket tighter and listens past the metronome for the other
sound: the groan of truck engines far to the east, out on the ice of Lake
Ladoga, where the road runs that should not exist. Thirty centimeters of black
ice carrying a column of one-and-a-half-ton trucks, headlights off, drivers
leaning out of open doors so they can jump when the ice gives. It gives often.

> The ration today was 125 grams of bread. Half of it is sawdust and cellulose.
> She weighs it in her palm before she eats it, the way you would weigh
> something precious, because it is.

By the river the anti-aircraft crews stamp their feet against a cold that has
its own arithmetic: at minus thirty, a wound stops bleeding before it can be
dressed, because the blood freezes first. The crews know this. They have stopped
talking about it.

*The metronome ticks. The trucks crawl west across the ice. The city, impossibly,
holds.*
