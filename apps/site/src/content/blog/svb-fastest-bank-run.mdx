On the morning of March 9, 2023, Silicon Valley Bank was a going concern with $173 billion in deposits, a 40-year history, and <Annotation client:load term="nearly half of America's venture-backed technology and life-science companies" note="Federal Reserve OIG, Material Loss Review of Silicon Valley Bank (2023-SR-B-013), p.11, citing SVBFG's own October 2022 corporate overview: the bank 'served nearly half of the venture-backed technology and life sciences companies in the United States.'" /> on its client roster. By the close of business that day it had a negative cash balance, and by the next morning it did not exist. Nobody stormed a lobby. Nobody stood in line. The money left through phones, in an amount and at a pace that had never been recorded before — not by wire transfer volume, not by teller-line length, but by the sheer number of taps on a screen. The Federal Reserve's own Vice Chair for Supervision later told the U.S. Senate that SVB "suffered a devastating and unexpected run by its uninsured depositors in a period of <Annotation client:load term="less than 24 hours" note="Testimony by Vice Chair for Supervision Michael S. Barr, U.S. Senate Committee on Banking, Housing, and Urban Affairs (Mar 28, 2023): 'the bank then suffered a devastating and unexpected run by its uninsured depositors in a period of less than 24 hours.'" />." The more precise regulatory record narrows that further still, to the fastest bank run in the history of American banking.

Do the arithmetic on that first number and the second, because the pace is the entire story before you even reach the cause. Forty-two billion dollars over eight hours is <Annotation client:load term="roughly $5.25 billion an hour" note="$42B ÷ 8 hours = $5.25B/hour, computed from two primary-sourced figures: DFPI's $42 billion withdrawal-request total (Order Taking Possession, Mar 10 2023) and the DFPI's own 'within the span of approximately eight hours' finding (Review of DFPI's Oversight and Regulation of SVB, May 8 2023, p.45)." /> — call it <Annotation client:load term="about $1.46 million a second" note="$42,000,000,000 ÷ (8 × 3,600 seconds) ≈ $1,458,333/second. The closest historical comparison, Washington Mutual's 2008 collapse, took <Annotation client:load term="sixteen days" note="Johnathan D. This dispatch is about why the clock sped up that much — and it is not, mainly, a story about rumor or panic. It is a story about a balance sheet that was already broken, and a depositor base wired, literally, to notice.

## The mechanism, not the mob

Start with the part almost every headline from that week skipped: SVB did not fail because people panicked over nothing. It failed because it had spent two years converting deposits into a bet on interest rates staying low — and lost that bet with unusual precision. To see why, first look at how fast the bank itself grew, because the growth rate is what forced the bet in the first place.

SVB's deposit base did not grow — it detonated. Forty-five billion dollars was the total in 2017; by the end of 2020, that had climbed to $103.2 billion; by the end of 2021, $191.4 billion; and by the first quarter of 2022, deposits had crossed $200 billion — a more-than-fourfold expansion in a little over four years. <Annotation client:load term="Every one of those figures traces to the Federal Reserve's own inspector general" note="Federal Reserve OIG, Material Loss Review of Silicon Valley Bank (2023-SR-B-013), p.9 and p.11: 'In 2017, SVB had total deposits of approximately $45 billion... SVB's total deposits increased from $103.2 billion in 2020 to $191.4 billion in 2021... SVB's deposits exceeded $200 billion as of March 31, 2022.' The OIG separately notes SVB's total assets, on a parallel trajectory, 'doubled twice in a 5-year period, from just over $50 billion at the start of 2018 to over $100 billion in 2020 and then to more than $200 billion in 2021.'" />, which used its own review of SVB's growth to explain why the bank's funding base became so dangerous — almost entirely because a zero-rate world was pumping cash into venture capital, and venture capital was pumping cash straight into SVB's operating accounts. The bank's own fourth-quarter 2021 investor presentation, cited in that same OIG review, boasted that <Annotation client:load term="average client funds increased by $36 billion" note="Federal Reserve OIG, Material Loss Review of Silicon Valley Bank (2023-SR-B-013), p.11: SVBFG's Q4 2021 Financial Highlights presentation 'noted that average client funds increased by $36 billion as venture capital investment activity fueled its clients' liquidity.'" /> in that single year "as venture capital investment activity fueled its clients' liquidity" — the same sentence that, in hindsight, describes the fuel for the fire. A bank cannot leave $200 billion sitting in cash; it has to put deposits to work, and SVB's clients were not, for the most part, borrowing much — tech startups burn cash, they do not usually take out mortgages. So the bank bought bonds with the deposits it could not lend out, at the exact moment when bond yields were at generational lows. By year-end 2022 deposits had fallen back to <Annotation client:load term="$173.1 billion" note="SVB Financial Group 2022 Form 10-K, p.1: 'total deposits of $173.1 billion' as of Dec 31, 2022, down from $189.2 billion at Dec 31, 2021." /> as venture funding dried up and clients burned through cash — but the bond portfolio bought at the top of the market was already underwater, and getting more so every month rates kept climbing. Watch what happened to the price of money itself over the same window.

For twenty-four straight months the effective federal funds rate sat at essentially zero, and Silicon Valley's venture-funded clients were flush — SVB's deposits <Annotation client:load term="exceeded $200 billion by March 2022" note="Federal Reserve OIG, Material Loss Review of Silicon Valley Bank (2023-SR-B-013, Sep 2023), p.9: 'SVB's deposits exceeded $200 billion as of March 31, 2022, and its total assets increased to $217 billion,' up from ~$45 billion in deposits / ~$50 billion in assets in 2017." /> from about $45 billion just five years earlier. The bank did what a bank with too much cheap money and not enough loan demand does: it bought bonds. Specifically, it built a <Definition client:visible term="held-to-maturity" def="an accounting classification for securities a bank intends to hold until they mature. HTM securities are booked at amortized cost, not market price — so their losses stay invisible on the balance sheet and income statement unless the bank is forced to sell." /> portfolio of long-dated agency mortgage-backed securities — <Annotation client:load term="$91.3 billion of them at amortized cost by year-end 2022" note="SVB Financial Group 2022 Form 10-K, Note 3 (Investment Securities), consolidated HTM securities table: total amortized cost $91,327 million as of Dec 31, 2022." /> — with a <Annotation client:load term="weighted-average duration of 6.2 years" note="SVB 2022 10-K, cited independently in the Federal Reserve's Barr Report (Apr 28, 2023, p.22): 'As of December 31, 2022, SVBFG's total HTM securities portfolio had a weighted-average duration of 6.2 years, and the majority of SVBFG's HTM portfolio consisted of agency MBS with a maturity of 10 years or more.'" />. Then the Fed, fighting the worst inflation in forty years, moved the funds rate from 0.08% to above 4.6% in twelve months — the line above going almost vertical. Every bond SVB had bought at 2021 yields was now worth dramatically less than face value, and because the bank had classified those bonds as "held to maturity," the loss was real but did not have to be marked on the books — until, that is, the bank actually needed the cash.

<Math client:visible tex="\frac{\Delta P}{P} \;\approx\; -D \times \Delta y"
  caption="The duration approximation: for a small move in yields, a bond portfolio's percentage price change is roughly minus its duration times the change in yield. A 6.2-year-duration book meeting a multi-point rate shock does not lose a little value — it loses a lot, and fast." />

That is not a made-up formula for the piece — it is the standard first-order bond-pricing identity, and it explains the *shape* of what happened to SVB's book without needing to reproduce its exact number: a portfolio with several years of duration is mechanically far more sensitive to a rate move than a portfolio of short-term bills, which is exactly the trade-off SVB made when it reached for MBS yield in 2021. The real, reported number is worse than a back-of-envelope estimate would even suggest, because it was not one clean parallel shift — it was two years of purchases at falling-then-rising yields, plus SVB's own decision, examined below, to strip out the interest-rate hedges that would have cushioned the blow.

Eleven times worse in twelve months. By SVB's own audited 10-K, the held-to-maturity book had swung from a $1.34 billion paper loss at the end of 2021 to a <Annotation client:load term="$15.16 billion paper loss" note="SVB Financial Group 2022 Form 10-K, Note 3: HTM securities, amortized cost $91,327M, unrealized losses $(15,160)M, fair value $76,169M, as of Dec 31, 2022." /> at the end of 2022 — against total stockholders' equity of <Annotation client:load term="just $16.0 billion" note="SVB Financial Group 2022 Form 10-K, p.1: 'total SVB Financial stockholders' equity of $16.0 billion' as of Dec 31, 2022." />. Do that division and the bank's entire capital cushion was, on paper, almost exactly canceled out by one line item on one side of its balance sheet — <Annotation client:load term="94.75%" note="$15.16B ÷ $16.0B = 94.75%, computed from the two SVB 10-K figures cited above." /> of it, before counting a dollar of loan losses, fraud, or anything resembling the classic bank-failure story. SVB was not brought down by a bad loan book. It was brought down by U.S. government-backed mortgage bonds — the single safest asset class a bank can hold from a credit-risk standpoint — bought at the wrong moment and held too long against deposits that could leave in an instant.

<Callout kind="aside" title="Accountants call it “hide-‘til-maturity”">
Held-to-maturity accounting is not an SVB invention — it is a standard, legal classification every bank uses. But it has a critic's nickname for a reason. CFA Institute's Sandy Peters argued in the week of the collapse that the treatment let SVB's $15.1 billion hole stay "hidden in plain sight," disclosed only in a footnote rather than reflected on the balance sheet investors and depositors actually read, writing that <Annotation client:load term="“the purpose of financial reporting is to convey the results of the company. It is not to assure the company stays around.”" note="Sandy Peters, CPA, CFA, 'The SVB Collapse: FASB Should Eliminate “Hide-‘Til-Maturity” Accounting,' CFA Institute Market Integrity Insights (Mar 13, 2023)." /> The loss did not appear because SVB hid it maliciously — it appeared because the rules let a real, market-priced loss sit outside the numbers everyone actually looks at, right up until a forced sale made it real.
</Callout>

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="Insolvent on paper, weeks before the run">
This is the detail that separates SVB from a classic panic: the Federal Reserve's own staff had already flagged the danger, internally, before a single depositor pulled a dollar. The mob did not create the insolvency. It discovered it.
</Callout>

## The Fed knew, and moved anyway too slowly

Here is the part of the record that turns this from a story about markets into a story about supervision — and the record starts far earlier than March 2023. A joint examination by the San Francisco Fed and the California DFPI, back in <Annotation client:load term="March 2019" note="Federal Reserve OIG, Material Loss Review of Silicon Valley Bank (2023-SR-B-013), p.11, citing a March 2019 joint FRB San Francisco / CDFPI examination report: private equity and venture capital firms' deposits 'typically experience irregular, larger-volume cash flows' and 'the bank's funding sources were concentrated and potentially volatile on short notice.'" />, had already put the exact mechanism of this piece into writing: SVB's private equity and venture capital deposit base "typically experience[s] irregular, larger-volume cash flows," and the bank's funding was "concentrated and potentially volatile on short notice." That is a four-years-early, word-for-word description of what happened on March 9. Examiners named the risk. Nobody made the bank change its behavior. Then, in February 2023 — three weeks before the run — Federal Reserve System staff briefed the Board of Governors on interest-rate risk across the banking system, and used SVB as a named example.

The Fed's own inspector general put a number on how exposed SVB already looked to its regulator months before it failed: <Annotation client:load term="total unrealized losses equal to 110 percent of capital, as of Q3 2022" note="Federal Reserve OIG, Material Loss Review of Silicon Valley Bank (2023-SR-B-013), p.28: a February 2023 System supervisory presentation to the Board of Governors 'illustrat[ed] that (1) as of the third quarter of 2022, total unrealized losses amounted to 110 percent of capital' at SVB." />. That is not a subtle warning sign. It is a bank whose theoretical losses, unrealized only because accounting rules let them stay unrealized, already exceeded its entire equity cushion — flagged to the Federal Reserve Board of Governors by name, five weeks before the bank collapsed. The Fed's own self-review is unusually blunt about what happened next: supervisors did not move with the urgency the number demanded.

<Pullquote cite="Michael S. Barr, Vice Chair for Supervision, Federal Reserve — Review of the Federal Reserve's Supervision and Regulation of Silicon Valley Bank (April 28, 2023)" href="https://www.federalreserve.gov/publications/files/svb-review-20230428.pdf" kind="document">
Silicon Valley Bank (SVB) failed because of a textbook case of mismanagement by the bank. Its senior leadership failed to manage basic interest rate and liquidity risk. Its board of directors failed to oversee senior leadership and hold them accountable. And Federal Reserve supervisors failed to take forceful enough action, as detailed in the report.
</Pullquote>

<Detail summary="Go deeper: why SVB's own risk models missed it too">
The Barr Report documents that SVB's board had breached its own long-term interest-rate-risk limits "on and off since 2017" — not a new problem. In April 2022, rather than reducing the underlying exposure, management changed a modeling assumption about how long deposits would stay at the bank, which had the effect of making the risk limit breach disappear on paper without changing the actual balance sheet. Over the same period, SVBFG also removed interest-rate hedges that would have offset some of the losses from rising rates — a bet that rates would not rise much further, made by a bank whose entire deposit base was concentrated in the sector most sensitive to a rate-driven slowdown in venture funding. The Fed's report is explicit that this was a management choice to protect short-term reported profit, not an oversight. Supervisors, for their part, spotted interest-rate deficiencies in SVB's 2020, 2021, *and* 2022 CAMELS exams — three straight annual exams — yet never converted the finding into a formal supervisory action (an MRA) until November 2022, by which point the bank had four months left to live. When examiners finally moved to downgrade SVB's ratings in August 2022, the Barr Report notes it was "the first downgrade of SVBFG or SVB in the period since 2017" — five years of essentially unbroken "satisfactory" ratings on a bank that was, by then, growing at a pace its supervisory team had never before had to track.
</Detail>

## The rule that bought SVB three extra years

Slow supervision was not only a matter of individual judgment calls — it was also structural, and traces back to a 2018 act of Congress. The Economic Growth, Regulatory Relief, and Consumer Protection Act (EGRRCPA) raised the asset threshold for a bank to face the Fed's most stringent "enhanced prudential standards" from <Annotation client:load term="$50 billion to $250 billion" note="Congressional Research Service, Silicon Valley Bank and Signature Bank Failures (IN12125, Mar 21 2023): 'Originally, all BHCs with over $50 billion in assets were subject to EPR to address too big to fail concerns. In 2018, P.L. 115-174 raised this asset threshold to $250 billion.'" />, and the Fed's 2019 implementing rule sorted large banks into four tiers of shrinking scrutiny. SVBFG crossed <Annotation client:load term="the $100 billion threshold in average total consolidated assets in June 2021" note="Federal Reserve Barr Report (Apr 28, 2023), p.24: 'In June 2021, SVBFG crossed the $100 billion threshold in average total consolidated assets and therefore met the criteria for a Category IV firm under the 2019 tailoring rule.'" />, making it a "Category IV" firm — the tier with the lightest capital, liquidity, and stress-testing requirements of the four. Worse, transition periods in the rule meant that even Category IV's modest requirements — including the liquidity coverage ratio and the net stable funding ratio, the two standards specifically designed to catch a duration/liquidity mismatch like SVB's — <Annotation client:load term="had not yet been applied to SVBFG at the time of its failure" note="Federal Reserve Barr Report (Apr 28, 2023), p.24: 'at the time of its failure, an important subset of Category IV capital and liquidity requirements, including supervisory stress testing, the stress capital buffer, the liquidity coverage ratio (LCR), and the net stable funding ratio (NSFR), were not yet applied to SVBFG because of applicable transition periods in the rules.'" />. A bank had grown four-fold into a $200-billion institution faster than the regulatory machinery built to classify $200-billion institutions could catch up to it. The Fed's own report calls this out as its fourth and final headline finding: the tailoring framework itself "impeded effective supervision by reducing standards, increasing complexity, and promoting a less assertive supervisory approach."

## The complication: a fast run needs a fast trigger, too

A duration mismatch explains why SVB was vulnerable. It does not, by itself, explain why the run took eight hours instead of eight weeks. For that you need the trigger and the depositor base — and both were unusual.

It helps to remember what an "old-fashioned" bank run actually required, because the friction that used to slow one down was not incidental — it was load-bearing for the whole system of bank supervision. A depositor in 1930, or even 1980, had to physically travel to a branch, stand in a line long enough to be visible to other depositors (which itself spread the panic, but slowly, one visible queue at a time), and receive cash or a check that then had to clear. Even the electronic-era version of that friction — a phone call to a broker, a wire initiated during business hours, a transfer that settles next-day — gave supervisors a window measured in days to intervene, negotiate an emergency sale, or arrange a lender-of-last-resort facility before the bank ran out of cash. Washington Mutual's regulators, watching a <Annotation client:load term="$2.8 billion single-day peak" note="Two independent sources: California DFPI, Review of DFPI's Oversight and Regulation of SVB (May 8, 2023, citing Johnathan D. Rose's Fed working paper), and FDIC Vice Chairman Travis Hill, remarks 'Recent Bank Failures and the Path Ahead' (Bipartisan Policy Center, Apr 12, 2023, fdic.gov): 'At its peak, WaMu lost $2.8 billion in deposits in a single day... yet 15 times smaller than the $42 billion pulled from SVB in one day.' SVB's regulators, watching a bank bleed at $5.25 billion an hour, did not.

Two forces made this specific book of depositors capable of moving that fast, and the Fed's postmortem names both explicitly.<Sidenote>SVB's clients were disproportionately businesses, not individuals — payroll accounts, operating cash, venture-fund escrow. **Concentration.** SVB's depositor base was not diversified across the economy the way a retail bank's is — it was startups, venture funds, and the people who run both, almost all in one industry, frequently sharing the same board members, the same law firms, and the same group chats. <Annotation client:load term="Over 94 percent of SVB's deposits were uninsured" note="Federal Reserve OIG, Material Loss Review of Silicon Valley Bank (2023-SR-B-013), p.9: 'As of year-end 2022, over 94 percent of SVB's total deposits were uninsured.'" /> at year-end 2022 — <Annotation client:load term="$151.6 billion of uninsured deposits" note="Congressional Research Service, Silicon Valley Bank and Signature Bank Failures (IN12125, Mar 21 2023): 'SVB ($151.6 billion) ... reported large estimated uninsured deposits on their last call reports.'" /> on its last call report, by far the largest single number in this entire piece — because operating accounts routinely run above the FDIC's $250,000 insurance limit. Every one of those depositors had the maximum possible incentive to be first out the door the moment solvency was in doubt, because nothing above $250,000 was guaranteed to come back.

**Technology.** The withdrawal itself required no phone call, no branch visit, no fax. A founder could move a company's entire operating cash from a phone in the time it took to read a Slack message.

<Callout kind="warning" title="This is not the Flash Crash — a different mechanism entirely">
It is worth being precise about what kind of "speed" this is, because it is easy to conflate this with a different, previously-covered kind of financial-market speed run. The 2010 Flash Crash was an <em>algorithmic</em> liquidity vacuum: a single automated sell program drained the order book of E-Mini futures contracts in minutes, and machines reacting to machines did the damage — a market-microstructure failure resolved by a five-second circuit breaker, with the underlying assets fundamentally unchanged in value before and after. SVB was the opposite kind of event: a slower-motion (hours, not minutes), fully <em>human</em>-decision-driven run, triggered by a real and already-existing insolvency on a duration-mismatched balance sheet, executed one deliberate tap at a time by thousands of individually rational depositors. Nothing glitched. Nothing malfunctioned. The technology did not cause a false signal — it correctly, instantly executed a true one.
</Callout>

## What the Fed itself concluded about the physics of this

This is the sentence this entire dispatch has been building toward, and it comes not from a journalist or an academic but from the Federal Reserve's own Vice Chair for Supervision, in the cover letter of the central bank's self-investigation of its own failure to prevent this.

<Pullquote cite="Michael S. Barr, Vice Chair for Supervision, Federal Reserve — Review of the Federal Reserve's Supervision and Regulation of Silicon Valley Bank (April 28, 2023)" href="https://www.federalreserve.gov/publications/files/svb-review-20230428.pdf" kind="quote">
The combination of social media, a highly networked and concentrated depositor base, and technology may have fundamentally changed the speed of bank runs. Social media enabled depositors to instantly spread concerns about a bank run, and technology enabled immediate withdrawals of funding.
</Pullquote>

Read that claim precisely, because it is doing more careful work than the popular "Twitter caused a bank run" shorthand that followed the collapse. Barr is not arguing that social media invented a panic out of nothing — his own report spends 118 pages establishing that SVB's insolvency exposure was real, board-known, and Fed-known well before March 9. He is arguing something narrower and more structurally important: that the *conduction speed* of a true signal through a banking system has permanently changed, because two things that used to be slow — spreading concern, and acting on it — are now instant. A 2008-era depositor heard rumors from a newspaper or a phone call, then had to visit a branch or wait for a wire to clear; a 2023-era one saw a venture partner's Slack message and moved the money before finishing the sentence. The Fed's own historical comparison set (Wachovia's 8 days, WaMu's 16) is not a set of examples where regulators had unlimited time to react — it is a demonstration that even *those* runs, by the standards of the era, counted as fast. SVB reset the baseline by roughly two orders of magnitude.

<Callout kind="note" title="Contagion, not just one bank">
The Barr Report's second stated lesson generalizes the first: "a firm's distress may have systemic consequences through contagion — where concerns about one firm spread to other firms — even if the firm is not extremely large." SVB was not, by pre-crisis standards, systemically important; regulators had exempted it from the Fed's most stringent oversight tier precisely because of its size. The speed of its run is part of why regulators concluded, days later, that letting it fail on ordinary terms risked spreading the same networked panic to other regional banks.
</Callout>

The contagion mechanism is worth naming precisely, because it is the same network effect that emptied SVB in eight hours, now pointed at every other regional bank simultaneously. The moment SVB failed, a depositor at any other bank with a similar profile — concentrated in one industry, heavy on uninsured balances, sitting on unrealized bond losses — had exactly the same rational incentive SVB's own depositors had: move first, ask questions later. Regulators were not just resolving one failed bank on March 12; they were trying to stop the same eight-hour mechanism from re-triggering at a dozen banks at once, which is the actual justification for a systemic risk exception that, on its face, looks like it is bailing out a single mid-sized regional lender.

## Why one bank's bad bet mattered to the whole system

SVB was not the only bank sitting on paper losses from the 2022 rate shock — it was simply the one whose depositor base was capable of noticing and acting in unison. The scale of the underlying problem, industry-wide, puts SVB's specific failure in context.

## So what — the payoff

Three things fall out of the arithmetic in this piece, and they matter more than the SVB story itself, because the conditions that produced it were not unique to one bank.

First: the eight-hour, $42 billion run was not evidence that mobile banking is dangerous in the abstract. It was evidence that mobile banking is dangerous specifically *combined with* an uninsured, concentrated, professionally networked depositor base — the exact profile of a bank serving one industry's operating cash. A retail bank whose depositors are individually insured up to $250,000 and largely unconnected to one another has neither the incentive nor the coordination mechanism to reproduce this. The Fed's own language is careful on this point: "a highly networked and concentrated depositor base, *and* technology" — the conjunction is load-bearing. Regulators reading this correctly would stress-test concentration and speed together, not technology risk alone.

Second: the balance-sheet mechanism predates and outlasts any single bank's story. Every bank in America that bought long-duration bonds in 2020–2021 and did not hedge the rate risk was carrying some version of SVB's exposure — the $620.4 billion industry-wide unrealized loss figure proves it was not a one-bank phenomenon. What made SVB unique was not the size of its bet but the speed at which its specific depositors could force it to realize the loss. The lesson for supervision is not "watch for bad bond portfolios" — regulators already watch for that, and had, per the Fed's own account, flagged SVB's specifically five weeks before it failed. The lesson is that the *response time* available to fix a flagged problem has collapsed from months to hours, and a supervisory process built for the slower era — an MOU planned for "Q1 2023," proposed at a bank that was already at 110% of capital in unrealized losses — was not fast enough to matter.

Third, and this is the reframing worth carrying forward: distinguish this precisely from a market-microstructure failure like a flash crash, because the fix is entirely different. A flash crash is solved by better circuit breakers and slower matching engines — friction is the cure, because the problem is a false or overreacting signal moving too fast through automated systems. SVB's problem was a *true* signal moving, for the first time, as fast as the automated systems around it — friction cannot cure that, because the signal was correct. The only real fixes are upstream of the speed: better capital and duration-risk requirements so insolvency does not develop silently for a year, and faster, more forceful supervisory escalation so that a flagged 110%-of-capital exposure gets a binding order in weeks, not a memorandum planned for next quarter. The clock speed of a bank run is not going back to 2008. The only lever left is closing the gap before the clock starts.

Fourth, and this is the practical takeaway for anyone who banks the way SVB's clients did: the $250,000 insurance limit is not a rounding error, it is the entire game. Every one of the depositors racing for the exit on March 9 was racing specifically because the money above that line had zero government backstop until regulators chose, after the fact, to extend one. SVB's own uninsured share — <Annotation client:load term="over 94 percent of deposits" note="Federal Reserve OIG, Material Loss Review of Silicon Valley Bank (2023-SR-B-013), p.9." /> — was not an eccentricity of one bank's client base; it is close to the norm for a business operating account holding payroll and runway. The Fed and FDIC's decision to invoke the systemic risk exception protected SVB's and Signature's depositors after the fact, but the joint statement is explicit that this was a discretionary, case-by-case call, not a change to the standing insurance limit — a founder banking the same way today is still, by the letter of the law, uninsured above $250,000, betting on a repeat discretionary rescue that regulators have not promised to make automatic. The lesson the market actually learned from SVB was not "diversify your deposits." It was "diversify your deposits, and hope the next bank that mismanages its duration risk is judged systemically important enough to bail out."
