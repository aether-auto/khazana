Give two gamblers the same loaded coin — heads pays even money, and it lands heads 60% of the time. Same edge, same bankroll, same thousand flips. One of them turns \$100 into a small fortune. The other goes broke. The only thing that differs between them is *how much of the bankroll they stake on each flip*, and that single number decides everything. Bet too little and you leave almost all the growth on the table. Bet too much and a favorable game still grinds you to zero. Somewhere in between sits one fraction that compounds money faster than any other strategy — and there is a formula for it.

The rest of this piece is about why that hill has exactly this shape, and how a physicist at Bell Labs found its summit.

## The bet that feels right and ends in ruin

The intuitive answer to "how much should I bet?" is to maximize how much money you expect to make. It is also catastrophically wrong, and seeing *why* is the first real step.

The mistake is choosing the wrong thing to maximize. Expected *dollars* is dominated by a vanishingly rare branch where you win every flip and own the world; that one fantasy outcome drags the average up even as almost every real path leads to zero. What you actually live through is not the average of the outcomes — it is the *product* of them, flip after flip. And products are ruled by a different quantity entirely.

## The deeper question: what were you supposed to maximize?

The failure of expected-dollar maximization is not a surprise to anyone who has read Daniel Bernoulli. In 1738 — more than two centuries before Kelly — Bernoulli posed what became known as the St. Petersburg paradox: a coin-flipping game with *infinite* expected value that nobody would pay more than a few dollars to play. His diagnosis was precise. The problem is not that the game is unattractive; the problem is that *expected money is the wrong thing to maximize*.

Bernoulli proposed instead that rational agents should maximize the *expected utility* of wealth, not its expected amount — and that utility grows as the *logarithm* of wealth. A dollar gained when you are poor is worth far more than a dollar gained when you are rich. Formally, if your current wealth is $w$ and you win $\delta$, the utility you gain is $\ln(w + \delta) - \ln(w) = \ln(1 + \delta/w)$, which shrinks as $w$ grows. This is why no sensible person bankrupts themselves chasing infinite expected value: the logarithm of the winning outcome is finite, and the logarithm of zero is negative infinity.

The equivalence is not coincidental. Maximizing the geometric mean of outcomes is the same thing as maximizing the expected logarithm of wealth — which is exactly what the Kelly criterion does. Bernoulli arrived at log utility through moral philosophy; Kelly arrived at the same answer through information theory. Both are saying: the quantity that governs long-run survival is multiplicative, and multiplicative processes are ruled by their *geometric* mean, not their arithmetic one.<Sidenote n={10} href="https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/paradox-stpetersburg/" source="Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy">The Wikipedia article on the Kelly criterion states this directly: "In a 1738 article, Daniel Bernoulli suggested that, when one has a choice of bets or investments, one should choose that with the highest geometric mean of outcomes. This is mathematically equivalent to the Kelly criterion, although the motivation is different." The English translation of Bernoulli's paper was not published until 1954 — two years before Kelly's paper.</Sidenote>

The distinction between geometric and arithmetic means is where most intuitions about investing and betting quietly break down. An arithmetic mean adds and divides. A geometric mean multiplies and takes the root. They agree when all terms are equal; they diverge whenever there is variance. For a sequence of independent returns, it is the *geometric* mean that determines long-run wealth — and it is always less than the arithmetic mean, by an amount that grows with variance. A 50% gain followed by a 50% loss does not return you to where you started: $1 \times 1.5 \times 0.5 = 0.75$. The arithmetic average of those two returns is zero, but your wealth is down 25%. The gap between what you *average* and what you *keep* is exactly the space that Kelly criterion navigates.

## Maximize the growth rate, not the jackpot

Here is the shift that unlocks everything. If you always bet the same fraction $f$ of your current bankroll, then after some wins and some losses your money is the *starting* amount multiplied by $(1 + f)$ for every win and $(1 - f)$ for every loss. Wealth compounds multiplicatively, so the natural thing to track is not the sum of your winnings but the *rate of compounding* — the exponential growth rate per bet.

John Kelly, writing at Bell Labs in 1956, was the first to make that the objective. His insight came not from gambling but from <Annotation client:load term="information theory" note="Kelly's paper reframed Shannon's channel capacity: a gambler with a noisy 'private wire' tip can grow wealth at a rate equal to the rate of useful information the wire delivers. The optimal-betting result fell out of an information-rate argument." />, in a paper his employer made him retitle so it wouldn't read as a how-to for bookies.<Sidenote n={3} href="https://archive.org/details/bstj35-4-917" source="Kelly 1956">J. L. Kelly Jr., "A New Interpretation of Information Rate," *Bell System Technical Journal* 35 (1956), 917–926 — originally drafted as "Information Theory and Gambling." Kelly was a colleague of Claude Shannon.</Sidenote> Edward Thorp — the mathematician who later beat Las Vegas blackjack and then ran a hedge fund on the same principle — turned Kelly's idea into the working formula gamblers and investors use today.

The growth-rate of a fixed-fraction bettor has one clean expression. Writing $p$ for your win probability, $q = 1 - p$ for losing, and $b$ for the payoff odds (you win $b$ units per unit staked), the long-run growth rate of wealth per bet is:

<Annotation client:load math term="g(f) = p\ln(1 + bf) + q\ln(1 - f)" note="The expected logarithm of your wealth multiplier per bet. A win multiplies your stake-adjusted wealth by (1 + b·f); a loss multiplies it by (1 − f). Maximizing the average of the log is the same as maximizing long-run compound growth — Thorp §2." /> — the expected log of your per-bet multiplier. That log is the hero of the story: it punishes the loss far more than it rewards the symmetric win, which is exactly why over-betting is fatal. A 100% loss sends $\ln(1 - f)$ to negative infinity, so the growth rate of an all-in bettor is $-\infty$ — a precise restatement of "you go broke."

Plot $g(f)$ against the fraction you bet and you get the hill from the top of this page. It starts at zero (bet nothing, grow nothing), rises to a single peak, and crosses back below zero when your bets get reckless. The peak is found the way you find any peak — set the derivative to zero — and for an even-money bet the answer is almost suspiciously simple:

The optimal fraction is <Annotation client:load math term="f^* = p - q" note="For an even-money bet. Thorp Example 2.1: with p = .53, f* = .53 − .47 = .06, i.e. bet 6% of bankroll. With unequal payoffs the formula generalizes to $f^* = (bp - q)/b$, the version every practitioner actually uses. Either way the recipe is the same: bet your edge, scaled by the odds, and not a penny more.

## Steer the bet yourself

The formula is small enough to hold in your head, but the *behavior* — how $f^*$ moves as the game changes — is easier to feel than to read. So feel it. The chart below is the same growth-rate hill, now under your control: one slider sets how often you win, the other sets the payoff odds.

A few things are worth provoking on purpose. Slide the win probability down to 50% at even money and the hill collapses to a flat line on the floor — $f^*$ snaps to zero, because Kelly never bets a game it doesn't expect to win.<Sidenote n={4} href="https://gwern.net/doc/statistics/decision/2006-thorp.pdf" source="Thorp §5">For a *fair* game the Kelly fraction is exactly zero; Thorp's blackjack treatment has f* fall continuously to 0 as the player's advantage vanishes. The formula refuses to bet a coin it doesn't expect to beat — the cleanest possible discipline.</Sidenote> Now nudge it back up a single percentage point: the optimal stake lifts off the floor immediately, but the growth rate it buys is tiny — a one-percent edge is a real edge, just a thin one. Finally, push the payoff-odds slider out to 5-to-1 while keeping a modest win rate: $f^*$ shrinks even though the bet is now lucrative, because longer odds mean you lose more often, and the formula quietly protects you from the longer dry spells. The discipline is built into the curve.

## Past the peak, the ground gives way

The most expensive misunderstanding in betting is treating the peak as a floor — assuming that if a little more than optimal is greedy, it is at least still profitable. It is not. The growth-rate hill is brutally asymmetric on its downslope.

For an even-money bet, the growth rate returns all the way to zero at exactly twice the Kelly fraction.<Sidenote n={5} href="https://gwern.net/doc/statistics/decision/2006-thorp.pdf" source="Thorp Ex. 2.1">With $p = .53$, $f^* = .06$, and solving $g(f) = 0$ numerically gives the critical fraction $f_c \approx .11973$ — just under $2f^*$. Below $f_c$ wealth grows to infinity; above it, "eventual downward fluctuations will inexorably drive the values toward zero."</Sidenote> Bet anywhere between the peak and that point and you are paying for the privilege of a wilder ride while *reducing* your long-run wealth. Bet past it and a winning game now sends you broke — the all-in disaster, arriving more slowly. The same fortune that compounds at the peak evaporates a stone's throw to its right.

This is why the shape of the hill matters more than its height. Two bettors who both *know* the edge — who agree on $p$ and $b$ to the decimal — can still end up at opposite fates, because one stood on the peak and the other took three steps to the right. Knowing the odds is not enough. Sizing the bet is the whole game.

## The drawdown tax, and why the pros bet less

There is a catch the formula's elegance hides, and the people who run real money feel it in their stomachs. Betting full Kelly is a stomach-churning ride. The optimal-growth bettor will, with mathematical certainty, watch their bankroll get cut in half at some point along the way — and there is a clean law for exactly how rough it gets.

Thorp gives a startlingly simple result: for a full-Kelly bettor, the probability that the bankroll *ever* falls to some fraction $x$ of its starting value is just $x$ itself.<Sidenote n={6} href="https://gwern.net/doc/statistics/decision/2006-thorp.pdf" source="Thorp §3.2">In the continuous approximation, "for the Kelly optimal fraction $f^*$, $P(V \text{ ever} \le x V_0) = x$." So there is a 50% chance of ever halving, a 10% chance of ever falling to a tenth. Thorp adds that "most cautious gamblers or investors who use Kelly find the frequency of substantial bankroll reduction to be uncomfortably large."</Sidenote> A coin-flip chance of ever halving. A one-in-ten chance of ever losing 90% of everything. Those are not tail risks; those are the advertised terms. And so almost nobody serious bets full Kelly. They bet a fixed fraction of it — *fractional* Kelly — and the trade they get is one of the most favorable in all of finance.

## Where the formula actually bites

None of this stayed theoretical. Thorp built the first card-counting blackjack system around it, where a counter's edge swings from negative to a point or two in the player's favor, and the right bet is to scale your stake to that thin, shifting advantage rather than to your confidence.<Sidenote n={8} href="https://gwern.net/doc/statistics/decision/2006-thorp.pdf" source="Thorp §5">In Thorp's blackjack model the optimal Kelly fraction tracks the count: $f^*$ decreases continuously to zero as the player's advantage shrinks, with a typical favorable-deck stake on the order of a couple percent of bankroll.</Sidenote> Then he pointed the same lens at the stock market, modeling a broad index as a continuous bet with an expected return, a volatility, and a risk-free rate to borrow against.

The result there is the one that should make any investor sit up. Using rough historical estimates for the S&P 500 — an 11% expected return, 15% volatility, and a 6% risk-free rate — the full-Kelly fraction comes out *greater than one*: about 2.2 times your bankroll.<Sidenote n={9} href="https://gwern.net/doc/statistics/decision/2006-thorp.pdf" source="Thorp §7.2">Thorp's S&P example: with $m = .11$, $s = .15$, $r = .06$, the continuous-time Kelly fraction is $f^* = (m - r)/s^2 \approx 2.22$, with long-run growth $g(f^*) \approx .115$. Full Kelly here means more than 2:1 leverage — which is precisely why almost no one bets it; the same drawdown law makes it a 50% chance of ever halving your stake.</Sidenote> Full Kelly on the index is not "go all in" — it is "borrow to hold *twice* your net worth in stocks." Which is exactly why prudent investors quietly run fractional Kelly without ever naming it: a half-Kelly 1.1× position keeps most of the growth and spares them the gut-wrenching drawdowns that full leverage guarantees. The formula does not tell you to be reckless. Read honestly, it tells you precisely how much risk your edge can *afford* — and almost always, it is less than your greed would like.

## From Bell Labs to the trading floor

The trajectory of the Kelly criterion from a nine-page BSTJ paper to the operating doctrine of quantitative finance is itself a study in unlikely connections. Kelly wrote the paper in 1956, framed around a gambler receiving tips over a noisy telephone wire — a deliberately abstract setup that was really about how much information an edge carries, not about gambling at all. Bell Labs, uncomfortable with the gambling metaphor, had him retitle it. Kelly died of a stroke in 1965, at 41, without ever applying his own formula to markets.

It was Thorp who made the connection real. He had read Kelly's paper by 1960 and realized immediately that card counting in blackjack was the exact discrete analog of Kelly's noisy-wire gambler: a small, shifting, calculable edge that compounds correctly only if bet correctly. His 1962 book *Beat the Dealer* introduced both card counting and the Kelly criterion to a general audience.<Sidenote n={11} href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_O._Thorp" source="Wikipedia: Edward O. Thorp">Thorp published *Beat the Dealer* in 1962; an updated edition followed in 1966. The book sold over 700,000 copies and was on the *New York Times* bestseller list. It introduced Kelly's formula to the gambling community and demonstrated, for the first time in print, that a card counter with correct bet sizing could hold a positive expected return at a casino over the long run.</Sidenote> Shannon — Thorp's colleague at MIT, and Kelly's colleague at Bell Labs — had independently arrived at related ideas about optimal portfolio rebalancing. The intellectual genealogy ran: information theory → gambling theory → portfolio theory, all at Bell Labs, all within a decade.

Then Thorp crossed over entirely. In 1969 he co-founded Princeton Newport Partners with Jay Regan, a convertible-bond arbitrage fund that ran Kelly-based position sizing on a portfolio of statistically hedged trades. The fund compounded at roughly 20% per year, after fees, without a single down year across nearly two decades of operation — while the S&P returned 10% over the same period with dramatically higher volatility.<Sidenote n={12} href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Princeton_Newport_Partners" source="Wikipedia: Princeton Newport Partners">Princeton Newport Partners compounded at approximately 20% annualized, after fees, from its founding in 1969 until it was forced to liquidate in the late 1980s when its head trader became entangled in the Drexel Burnham Lambert junk-bond investigation. The principals were eventually exonerated, but the legal burden forced the fund to close. Thorp later ran Ridgeline Partners (1994–2002) using statistical arbitrage.</Sidenote> The fund did not close because the strategy failed. It closed because its head trader became entangled in the Drexel Burnham Lambert junk-bond investigation — the principals were eventually cleared, but the legal costs forced liquidation. The Kelly-sized trades held up. The non-mathematical risks did not.

What Princeton Newport demonstrated — and what Thorp's later statistical-arbitrage work at Ridgeline Partners confirmed — is that Kelly bet sizing is not magic. It is a disciplined answer to a specific question: given that you have an edge, how do you make sure you survive long enough to collect it? The criterion maximizes the long-run growth rate, but it also, implicitly, maximizes the probability of not being ruined before that long run arrives. The two objectives are the same thing, viewed from different directions. Compound as fast as you can without betting yourself into oblivion — and the formula tells you where that line is, to the decimal.

The arithmetic of ruin, then, is really the arithmetic of restraint. The math hands you a single number that separates compounding from collapse, and the hardest part is not computing it — it is sitting a little below it, on purpose, for the rest of the game.
