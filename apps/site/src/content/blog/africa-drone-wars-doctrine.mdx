That gap — not any single strike — is the real story of Africa's drone wars.

Since 2019, cheap drones — Turkish Bayraktar TB2s and Akıncıs bought by cash-strapped juntas, commercial FPV quadcopters bought by the insurgents fighting them — have turned the Sahel, Somalia, and Sudan into the world's busiest live laboratory for what airpower can and cannot do to an insurgency. The volume of that laboratory's output is not in dispute: strikes went from 31 in 2020 to 484 in 2024, and Sudan and the Sahel alone accounted for 84 percent of that total, according to the Africa Center for Strategic Studies, which draws its figures from the Armed Conflict Location & Event Data Project (<Annotation client:load term="ACLED" note="Armed Conflict Location & Event Data Project — the primary conflict-event dataset this piece draws its incident-level claims from throughout. The 900+/3,000+-since-2011 aggregate is Africa Center's own ACLED-derived count; the corroborating figure below (Drone Wars UK, via Al Jazeera) is a wholly separate, independently compiled dataset with a narrower civilians-only scope, not a matching total." />). That specific 900-plus-strikes, 3,000-plus-fatalities aggregate traces to one dataset; a separate, unrelated tracker — the London-based Drone Wars UK, whose “Death on Delivery” report Al Jazeera covered independently — put civilian drone deaths alone at more than 943 across six of these same countries in just three of these years (November 2021–November 2024), using its own investigative methodology rather than ACLED's. The two counts are not directly comparable (different scope, different years, civilians-only versus combatants-plus-civilians) but they corroborate the same underlying reality from two unconnected sources: this is now a very large, escalating, and — by Drone Wars UK's own admission — probably undercounted body of casualties. What is in dispute — what this dispatch tries to quantify rather than assert — is whether any of it wins wars. The record so far says: it wins engagements, sometimes it wins territory, and it almost never wins the peace, because the insurgents on the other end of the strike adopt the same technology faster than the states that started the arms race can adapt their doctrine to it.

## Why now: the juntas' bet, and who is arming whom

The timing is not a coincidence. Between 2020 and 2023, a wave of military coups in Mali, Burkina Faso, and Niger led each junta to expel French, American, and UN forces that had underwritten regional counterterrorism for a decade — and each junta needed a fast, cheap substitute for the airpower and intelligence capability it had just given up. Turkish drones were the answer Foreign Affairs' own reporting on the region identifies directly: Bayraktar TB2s and, later, the larger Akıncı, offered a way to compensate for lost Western arsenals without the political strings attached to a Western basing agreement. Türkiye did not become Africa's top drone supplier by accident — it holds 32 government-to-government drone agreements across the continent, 28 of them signed since 2021 alone, according to the Africa Center for Strategic Studies (that specific count is Africa Center's own; a separate Africa Defense Forum count, reported independently, does not repeat the exact figure but does independently confirm Bayraktar drones in at least a dozen African militaries — Angola, Burkina Faso, Djibouti, Ethiopia, Kenya, Mali, Morocco, Niger, Nigeria, Somalia, Togo, and Tunisia — corroborating the scale of Turkish dominance even where the precise agreement-count isn't cross-checked). That is a supplier relationship built almost entirely inside the same five years this piece covers.

Read down that table and the asymmetry driving this whole dispatch is right there in the last row: every state actor in this piece depends on a foreign government's export decision, a maintenance contract, and (per the Somalia case) a foreign intelligence-sharing arrangement just to keep its drones flying. Every non-state actor in this piece needs a credit card and a hobbyist retailer. Nine African countries — Algeria, Egypt, Ethiopia, Kenya, Morocco, Nigeria, South Africa, Sudan, and Tunisia — now produce their own military drones and supply roughly 12 percent of the continental market, per the Africa Center, which narrows that gap for governments somewhat. It does nothing for the insurgencies fighting them, and it does not need to: a modified DJI quadcopter costs a rounding error next to a TB2, and the barrier that used to separate hobbyist drones from weapons — the ability to strip geofencing, wire a mortar-round payload, and fly a strike without emitting a signal an intelligence service could trace — has, per the Policy Center's own technical assessment, already collapsed with the help of offline, open-source AI tools that need no internet connection to plan a flight path or troubleshoot a firmware override.

## The insurgent adaptation curve

Start with the clearest, most granular piece of evidence in the whole file: a dated incident log. Niccola Milnes and Rida Lyammouri, writing for the Policy Center for the New South in July 2025, built an annex tracking every confirmed drone incident by five armed groups — JNIM, the Azawad Liberation Front (FLA) and its allied CSP-DPA faction, ISWAP, the Islamic State–Sahel (ISGS), and Boko Haram — across Mali, Burkina Faso, Niger, Togo, and Nigeria, from JNIM's first armed drone strike in September 2023 through June 22, 2025. Aggregated by quarter, it does not look like gradual adoption. It looks like an inflection point.

This is not a group slowly bolting a new tool onto an old playbook. A wholly separate count, from a different dataset entirely, points the same direction: Al Jazeera reported in February 2026 that ACLED — not the Policy Center — had logged JNIM using drones at least 89 times between 2023 and 2025, 69 of them attacks rather than surveillance. That figure is roughly three times the Policy Center's own “over two dozen confirmed incidents,” because the two trackers count differently (ACLED's broader event-coding standard versus the Policy Center's narrower, individually-verified incident log) — but a second, independently sourced dataset finding the same escalating trend, even at a different absolute count, is the corroboration that matters here: JNIM's drone campaign is not an artifact of one report's counting method. Burkina Faso's army released its own TB2 strike footage starting in late 2023, and JNIM appears to have taken the hint: since early 2025 it has recorded and distributed its own drone-strike videos in a visual style that mimics state footage almost shot for shot — surveillance angle, target lock, aftermath — an insurgent group consciously contesting the state's monopoly on the aesthetics of air superiority, not just its ordnance.

The tactical range by mid-2025 had gone well past propaganda. On May 23, JNIM — reportedly under a former FLA drone commander, Colonel Hussein Ghulam, who crossed over to JNIM in mid-2024 — used three suicide drones to seize the military base at Dioura, Mali, briefly controlling the town outright. In early June 2025, a drone-monitored JNIM assault on the military base at Boulkessi produced the single deadliest confirmed drone-linked incident in the whole annex — though how deadly depends on whose count you use. The Policy Center's own log puts it at 100-plus soldiers killed, matching JNIM's own claim of “over 100”; independent Malian security sources cited by Africanews, by contrast, put the toll at “more than 30” in the days immediately after the attack, before JNIM's own propaganda claim spread. This piece uses the wider, source-attributed range — 30-plus by independent security sources, 100-plus by the insurgent-aligned and Policy Center count — rather than collapsing it to a single number neither side has independently verified. ### Where the skill actually came from

None of that adaptation happened in a vacuum, and tracing the transfer chain matters for doctrine because it shows the state side has no monopoly on drone expertise even within its own conflict zone. The Policy Center's reporting traces JNIM's leap from bottle-bomb IEDs to coordinated multi-drone assaults to a specific personnel pipeline: the FLA was the first Sahelian armed group to build a dedicated drone team, and its spokesperson told RFI in September 2024 that the group had trained that team over a ten-month period before its July 2024 attack on Malian and Wagner positions at Tinzaouaten — the same battle West Point's Modern War Institute cites as the moment Malian drone reconnaissance failed to detect the buildup against it. In mid-2024, a former FLA commander, Colonel Hussein Ghulam, crossed over to JNIM; his name has since been linked to several of JNIM's most damaging 2025 strikes, including the Dioura and Tiby operations. Kyiv has officially denied supplying drones to Mali's rebels directly, but the tactical playbook — cheap FPV airframes, distributed ISR, kamikaze strikes on stationary armor — visibly echoes the low-cost drone war Ukraine popularized from 2022 onward, and the Policy Center paper treats that resemblance as more than coincidence even where it can't prove a direct supply line.

The technical side of the adaptation is just as telling. By the Policy Center's account, JNIM's engineers have learned to bypass commercial geofencing, integrate 3D-printed mortar-round adapters, and troubleshoot firmware using offline, open-source AI models — tools that need no internet connection and therefore leave no signal for a state intelligence service to intercept. And JNIM did not stop at the hardware: since early 2025 it has filmed and distributed its own drone-strike footage in a visual style — surveillance angle, target lock, aftermath shot — that directly mimics the videos Burkina Faso's army had been releasing of its own TB2 strikes since late 2023. An insurgency that started this five-year window with no armed drones at all is now contesting the state's monopoly on the *imagery* of air superiority, not only its ordnance — which is as clear a signal as this dataset offers that “doctrine” here is not a static asset one side owns, it is a race both sides are running, and the side with the shorter procurement cycle for a hobbyist quadcopter has, so far, been running it faster than the side with the longer procurement cycle for a foreign-built UCAV.

JNIM is the sharpest case in the log precisely because it is the only group tracked operating across three countries at once, but it is not the only insurgency running this playbook. The same Policy Center annex records the Islamic State–Sahel using a kamikaze-drone assault against a Nigerien army outpost at Eknewane in May 2025 that killed 41 soldiers — the deadliest single confirmed drone-linked incident in the dataset after Boulkessi — and it separately records ISWAP's own grenade-strapped-drone attack on a Nigerian army base at Wajiroko in December 2024 (5 soldiers injured, none killed), then a joint ISWAP-and-Boko-Haram drone attack on bases at Wajiroko and Wulgo in March 2025 that the annex specifically records as drone-initiated and followed by a ground assault (12 killed), and a further joint ISWAP-and-Boko-Haram armed-drone-and-grenade-launcher attack on an IDP camp at Mallam Fatori in June 2025 (12 killed, including three commanders) — three distinct incidents, not identical in group attribution or sequencing, but all inside the same six-month stretch. Eighteen hundred kilometers east, Islamic State–Somalia ran its own, entirely independent first: two successful drone strikes against Puntland security forces in January 2025, the first time ACLED had ever recorded that group using drones at all, alongside a surge of IED attacks the same month. None of these five groups coordinate with each other in any confirmed way. What they share is simpler and more worrying for doctrine purposes than a hidden alliance: the same commercial hardware, the same tactical insight (use the drone to find the gap in the defense, then send the ground assault through it), arrived at independently, in five different insurgencies, in under two years. That is what an idea whose technical barrier to entry has collapsed looks like when it spreads — not a single supply chain to interdict, but a tactic that reinvents itself locally everywhere the hardware is cheap enough to buy.

## Somalia: the clearest recapture-then-reversal on the record

If the Sahel shows the adaptation curve, Somalia shows the recapture-reversal cycle in its most complete, most quantified form — because unlike most African drone campaigns, this one has a hard number attached to both the gain and (in part) the loss.

What they could not do was hold it, garrison it, or pay the clan militias whose cooperation the state needed to keep it. <Sidenote>Somalia is not unique in this — it is the best-documented instance of a pattern this piece traces across three separate theaters below.</Sidenote>

## The claim that started the autonomy debate — and how much weaker it is than the headlines

Before going further into 2025's Sahel, it is worth returning to where “autonomous” African drone warfare supposedly began, because the founding claim of that whole discourse turns out to be far shakier than five years of retelling has made it sound — and this piece was specifically asked to check it carefully rather than repeat it.

In March 2021, a UN Panel of Experts on Libya, reporting under Security Council Resolution 1970, described the closing days of the 2019–2020 battle for Tripoli, in which Turkish-backed Government of National Accord forces routed Khalifa Haftar's Libyan National Army. The report's language, describing retreating Haftar-affiliated convoys, is precise and dramatic:

<Pullquote cite="UN Panel of Experts on Libya, Final Report, S/2021/229 (March 2021)" href="https://docs.un.org/S/2021/229" kind="document">
Logistics convoys and retreating [Haftar-affiliated forces] were subsequently hunted down and remotely engaged by the unmanned combat aerial vehicles or the lethal autonomous weapons systems such as the STM Kargu-2 … programmed to attack targets without requiring data connectivity between the operator and the munition: in effect, a true ‘fire, forget and find’ capability.
</Pullquote>

That paragraph launched a thousand headlines about the “first known case” of a lethal autonomous weapon killing a human being without a person in the loop. Read the actual evidentiary chain behind it, and the claim thins out fast.

Four independent readings of the same primary document — a wire service, an arms-control-focused science publication, and two university-affiliated legal/security scholars — converge on the same correction: the UN Panel of Experts never confirmed that an autonomous weapon killed anyone in Libya, and it never confirmed the Kargu-2 was in autonomous mode rather than remotely piloted during the engagement it describes. What is confirmed, directly from the primary text, is that Turkish-backed forces used armed drones — some of them technically capable of autonomous target engagement — to hunt down a retreating army in 2020, and that this hunting, whatever its exact autonomy setting, worked: the GNA held Tripoli, a ceasefire followed months later, and the capital was never retaken. That is a real and important finding about drones enabling a durable outcome. It is a much smaller and more careful finding than “the first robot kill,” and this piece treats it as exactly that — a hunting tactic that succeeded, not a confirmed autonomous homicide.

Hitoshi Nasu's analysis at the Lieber Institute makes the case that chasing the “first autonomous kill” headline is actually a distraction from the question that matters for doctrine going forward: not whether a human died without a finger on a trigger, but whether a weapon capable of engaging without real-time human confirmation was deployed in compliance with the law of armed conflict, and who is accountable if it was not. That question does not require resolving the disputed autonomy claim at all — a Kargu-2 flying a pre-programmed search pattern over a retreating convoy raises the same targeting and distinction concerns whether a human confirmed the final engagement or not. Five years on, this is the more durable legacy of the Libya episode: not a confirmed robot kill, but the first time a body of evidence — however contested — forced international lawyers to write about autonomous targeting in Africa as a live operational fact rather than a hypothetical, years before the Sahel's FPV insurgents made the same technology a monthly occurrence.

Notice what closes the loop: the offensive that ends this cycle does not just retake ground, it retakes the exact piece of infrastructure — the TB2 ground station — that made the original recapture possible. That is doctrine failure written in the plainest terms available: the state's drone advantage was never doctrine, in the sense of an institutionalized way of holding what airpower clears. It was a single imported asset, dependent on foreign operators and a fragile mercenary alliance, sitting in a region where the other side had spent 2024 and 2025 building exactly the skill the state assumed only it could have. Small Wars Journal and the Institute for the Study of War's Critical Threats project both frame the April 2026 offensive the same way Somalia's collapse gets framed: not a single battlefield defeat, but the arrival bill for two years of a state that could clear ground with borrowed airpower faster than it could build the security-force capacity to keep it cleared.

The tactical half of the story reads like a doctrine success: the Sudanese Armed Forces' Turkish TB2s and Iranian-made drones helped push the paramilitary Rapid Support Forces out of populated areas in and around Khartoum through late 2024 and into 2025, according to the Africa Center — a real, drone-enabled recapture of the capital region, distinct from Somalia's and Mali's reversals in that it has held, so far. But the cost of getting there has compounded faster than the recapture itself: drone-attributed deaths in Sudan rose sixfold in a single year, per ACLED's own count (no independent tracker has published a competing total, so this figure is presented as ACLED's, not as an established consensus). The civilian toll is corroborated more broadly, though: the UN's human rights office recorded at least 880 civilian deaths from drone strikes between January and April 2026 alone — more than 80 percent of all conflict-related civilian deaths that period — a figure reported independently by Euronews; a month later, UN rights chief Volker Türk put the running total at “more than 1,000” for the first five months of 2026, reported separately by the Associated Press. Sudan also breaks the pattern this piece has traced so far in one important way: here, the “insurgent” side is not flying commercial FPV quadcopters, it is flying its own combat-grade fleet. The RSF has acquired Chinese CH-4 MALE combat drones — a class of aircraft comparable to the TB2 itself — along with Chinese-made “Sunflower” suicide drones, according to the Africa Center. Sudan's war is therefore not a state-drone-versus-improvised-insurgent-drone contest like the Sahel or Somalia; it is two drone fleets built by two different foreign supply chains (Turkish and Iranian equipment for the SAF, Chinese equipment reaching the RSF) fighting each other over the same city, with the civilian population caught in the cross-fire of both. That reframes the “civilian toll” finding above: it is not solely a story of one side's strikes escalating, but of an arms race between two combatant drone fleets whose combined tempo the population has to absorb. A separate, independent civilian-toll estimate — Drone Wars UK's “Death on Delivery” report, an investigative dataset wholly distinct from ACLED, as reported by Al Jazeera — put drone-strike civilian deaths at more than 943 across six African conflicts (Sudan, Somalia, Nigeria, Burkina Faso, Mali, and Ethiopia) between November 2021 and November 2024, and warned its own total was likely “the tip of the iceberg” given how many countries run undisclosed drone campaigns. That is a different dataset, a different time window, and a civilians-only count rather than ACLED's combatants-plus-civilians figure — so it is not a like-for-like check on Sudan's 2,670 — but it independently confirms the same underlying finding from a source with no connection to ACLED: drone-strike deaths in Africa are large, accelerating, and undercounted by any single tracker.

## Where drones actually did produce durable control

None of this means drones never work as advertised — and a piece that only showed reversals would be cherry-picking as badly as the headlines it is correcting. Two cases in the same dataset show the opposite pattern, and the difference is instructive.

Line the five up and a variable emerges that has nothing to do with drone quality: the two campaigns that held — Libya and Ethiopia — were fights against a conventional or semi-conventional army defending fixed positions and supply lines, where a decisive strike campaign could inflict losses the other side could not replace and had no guerrilla fallback to absorb. Haftar's LNA in 2019–2020 was a maneuvering conventional force with armor and logistics columns — exactly what a Bayraktar TB2 is built to find and kill from the air, and exactly the kind of target that, once destroyed, cannot simply melt into a market town and wait. The TPLF's push toward Addis Ababa in 2021 was the same kind of target: a mechanized advance with a supply tail, not a dispersed insurgency. The three that reversed or came at an escalating cost — Somalia, Mali, Sudan's civilian toll — were fights against or alongside forces built specifically to dissolve into the population, disperse, and rearm: al-Shabaab leaned on the guerrilla tactics, dispersal, and clan-militia countermobilization that West Point's Combating Terrorism Center identifies as the reason the 2022 offensive's gains proved reversible, and JNIM and the FLA simply bought the same category of weapon the state had just used against them. Foreign Affairs — this piece's curated seed and the outlet that first framed this whole comparison — puts the underlying mechanism plainly in its own reporting: the equalizing effect of cheap, commercially available drones increasingly makes integration into a state's existing force structure — logistics, garrison capacity, intelligence culture, the boring parts of counterinsurgency — the actual determinant of outcomes, not which side owns the fancier aircraft. West Point's Modern War Institute reaches the same conclusion from the failure side: Malian and Wagner drone reconnaissance never detected the buildup behind the catastrophic July 2024 defeat at Tinzaouaten, because a sensor pointed at the wrong problem is not a doctrine, it is an expensive camera.

## So what: the doctrine that hasn't been written yet

Line up what the data actually shows, because the honest version is sharper than either “drones are revolutionizing African warfare” or “drones don't matter.” Drones are winning the opening battle almost everywhere they're used at scale — 215-plus towns in Somalia, Kidal in 2023, Khartoum's suburbs in 2024–25, Tripoli in 2020. What they are not reliably winning is the six-to-thirty-month period after that battle, when the recaptured ground needs a garrison, a payroll for the militia that helped take it, and an intelligence apparatus that can see the insurgency rebuilding rather than just the insurgency's last known position. Insurgents have figured this out faster than governments have: JNIM went from zero armed drone strikes to twenty in a single quarter in under two years; al-Shabaab and JNIM alike specifically target the seam between initial victory and durable administration, not the drones themselves. The doctrine gap here is not technological — cheap FPV drones and a UN report are not, in the end, hard to acquire or to read. It is institutional: no African state in this dataset has yet built the follow-through capacity to match the speed of its own airpower, and every insurgency in the dataset has had less trouble matching the airpower than the states had building that capacity. Until that changes, the pattern documented above will keep repeating, one recaptured town, one Bayraktar station, and one UN report at a time.

There is a narrower, more falsifiable version of that claim worth stating precisely, because it is the one an actual military planner could act on. The variable that separates the two campaigns that held (Libya, Ethiopia) from the three that didn't (Somalia, Mali, Sudan's civilian toll) is not drone quality, strike volume, or even initial territorial gain — Somalia's 215-plus towns dwarf anything Libya's TB2 campaign needed to retake. It is whether the enemy being struck had a place to disperse to and a way to re-arm cheaply once dispersed. A conventional army's tanks, once destroyed, are gone; a clan militia's loyalty, once bought, decays every month a salary goes unpaid, and a dispersed insurgent fighter with retail-priced drone parts and an open-source AI tool can rebuild an air-strike capability, per the Policy Center's own technical assessment, faster than a state can rebuild a garrison's supply chain. That asymmetry in reconstitution speed — not the asymmetry in aircraft — is the actual mechanism this dataset keeps surfacing, from Cowsweyne to Tinzaouaten to the seizure of Mali's own TB2 ground station. A serious African counter-drone doctrine, if one gets written from this evidence, would have to treat holding-capacity and payroll reliability as the primary weapons system, and the aircraft as the secondary one — which is precisely backward from how every government in this piece has spent its money so far.
