Three things happened this week that look unrelated until you line them up.

First, a research paper argued that the routing inside mixture-of-experts models
is doing something subtler than load balancing — it's reproducing a *curriculum*,
teaching the model easy tokens before hard ones. That makes these models cheaper
to run well, which matters most where compute is scarce.

Second, the marginal cost of inference on a commodity edge NPU dropped below a
threshold that a year ago looked like a 2028 problem. Cheap inference at the edge
changes where intelligence lives.

Third — and quietest — an engineering team collapsed three network hops into one
programmable edge tier and cut tail latency by a factor of five. Not an AI story
on its face. But it's the same story: the smart part of the system is moving
*outward*, toward the user, away from the center.

The throughline is economics, not novelty. When the cost curve bends, the
architecture follows.
