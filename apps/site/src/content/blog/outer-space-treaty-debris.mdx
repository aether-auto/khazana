Here is the orbit that the world's only binding rulebook for space was written to govern. In October 1967, when the Outer Space Treaty entered into force, the geostationary belt — the ring 35,786 km up where a satellite's orbital period matches Earth's rotation, so it appears to hang motionless over one spot — held eight objects. The whole of Earth orbit, every shell combined, held roughly eighteen hundred. Today one catalog run by the European Space Agency counts 45,860 tracked objects. Statistical models behind that catalog put the untracked population at something like 140 million fragments a millimeter or larger — most of them moving at speeds that turn a paint fleck into a bullet.

In 1967, low Earth orbit — everything within 2,000 km of the surface, where the International Space Station and most modern satellites fly — already carried more debris than payloads: <Annotation client:load term="1,412 fragments" note="US Space Force via Our World in Data, low-earth-orbits-objects series, 1967 value. The debris problem, in other words, is not younger than the treaty. It is exactly as old, and nobody who wrote Article IX's promise of "due regard" was thinking about it as the central design constraint. What they were thinking about is Article II, and the number that mattered to *that* article was eight — the population of the one orbital shell every state on Earth would eventually want a piece of.

Hold those four numbers. The rest of this piece is an argument about what happens when a legal architecture designed for the first number on that list — a domain with a few thousand objects, all of them state-owned, all of them trackable by eye through a telescope — is asked to govern the fourth, fifth, and sixth orders of magnitude past it, with no amendment, no binding successor treaty, and a liability standard that has been tested exactly once.

## Eight objects, four articles, one treaty

The treaty's formal name is the Treaty on Principles Governing the Activities of States in the Exploration and Use of Outer Space, Including the Moon and Other Celestial Bodies. It opened for signature on 27 January 1967 and entered into force that October, and it is still, nearly sixty years later, the foundational document of space law — every other instrument in the regime (the 1968 Rescue Agreement, the 1972 Liability Convention, the 1976 Registration Convention, the failed 1979 Moon Agreement) sits underneath it, elaborating articles it never revised. <Annotation client:load term="118 states" note="UNOOSA treaty status page, as of October 2025 — the treaty has near-universal adherence among spacefaring and non-spacefaring nations alike." /> are party to it today. Four articles carry the argument this piece makes, and each was written against a domain that, measured in objects per shell, looked nothing like the one it now governs.

### Article II — the clause that couldn't see debris coming

Article II is one sentence: outer space, including the moon and other celestial bodies, "is not subject to national appropriation by claim of sovereignty, by means of use or occupation, or by any other means." It was written to close off exactly one scenario — a state planting a flag on the moon or in orbit and claiming the ground underneath it, the way European powers had claimed continents. It succeeded completely at that. No state has ever formally claimed a piece of outer space under color of Article II, and the clause is treated as settled customary international law even by states, like the US, that have flirted with resource-extraction regimes for asteroids and lunar regolith.

What Article II's drafters could not have modeled is a form of appropriation that requires no flag and no claim: simply filling an orbital shell with junk until no one else can safely use it. <Annotation client:load term="Chelsea Muñoz-Patchen's 2018 analysis" note="Muñoz-Patchen, Regulating the Space Commons, Chicago Journal of International Law 19(1) (2018): argues space debris functions as de facto appropriation of orbital resources, restricting other states' access without any formal claim of sovereignty — a use the treaty's drafters never anticipated." /> in the Chicago Journal of International Law makes the case explicitly: a defunct satellite or a rocket body left in a congested shell for decades functions as abandoned property that nobody has title to and nobody is obligated to remove, and its practical effect — foreclosing safe use of that orbital volume by others — is indistinguishable from appropriation, even though it triggers none of Article II's language. The clause protects against a claim of ownership. It has nothing to say about a claim of *occupancy by neglect*, and occupancy by neglect is precisely what a shell holding <Annotation client:load term="27,805 tracked objects" note="US Space Force via Our World in Data: LEO Payloads (14,508) + Rocket bodies (1,576) + Debris (11,721), 2025 values." /> in low Earth orbit now amounts to.

### Article VI and VII — responsibility without a mechanism that can find fault

Article VI makes states "internationally responsible for national activities in outer space... whether such activities are carried on by governmental agencies or by non-governmental entities," and requires that private actors be authorized and continuously supervised by a state party. Article VII goes further: a state that launches, or from whose territory an object is launched, is internationally liable for damage that object causes — on Earth, in the air, or in outer space.

These two articles sound like exactly what a debris regime needs: a clear chain from harm back to a responsible state. The 1972 Liability Convention was supposed to operationalize that chain, and for one category of harm it does, cleanly. <Annotation client:load term="Article II of the Liability Convention" note="Convention on International Liability for Damage Caused by Space Objects (1972), Article II: a launching state is absolutely liable for damage its space object causes on the surface of the Earth or to aircraft in flight — no fault needs to be proven." /> makes a launching state absolutely liable for damage its object causes on the surface of the Earth or to an aircraft — no fault has to be proven, full stop. That is the article that produced the regime's one real test case.

<Pullquote cite="Convention on International Liability for Damage Caused by Space Objects, Article III (1972)" href="https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Space_Liability_Convention" kind="document">
A launching State shall be liable... only if the damage is due to its fault or the fault of persons for whom it is responsible.
</Pullquote>

That is the article that governs everything in orbit — collisions between satellites, a working spacecraft struck by an untracked fragment, one state's debris destroying another state's asset. And "fault" has never been defined anywhere in the Convention. To win a claim under Article III, an injured state does not just need to identify which piece of debris hit its satellite — an act of forensics that is often impossible once a fragment has spent years in a shared, congested shell — it then has to *prove* that whoever owned the parent object was negligent, not merely unlucky. In fifty-four years, exactly one claim has ever been filed under the Liability Convention.

Notice the last column. Altitude, not intent, decided how long each of these debris fields lasted — Kosmos 1408's fragments, released 300–400 km lower than Fengyun-1C's, mostly reentered within four years, while the 2007 test's debris is still being tracked through the 2030s. That is a genuinely useful fact and a genuinely unhelpful one at the same time: it means the newest, lowest-altitude debris clouds self-clean, but it also means every collision or test conducted at a *popular working altitude* — the 700–1,000 km band where Fengyun-1C died and where a large share of Earth-observation and communications satellites still fly — leaves a mess for a generation, no matter which article of which treaty was supposedly protecting that shell.

Not one of these three events produced a Liability Convention claim. Not the ASAT tests, not the collision. <Annotation client:load term="Kosmos 954" note="Canada v. USSR (1978): after the nuclear-powered Kosmos 954 crashed in Canada's Northwest Territories scattering radioactive debris, Canada filed a $6M USD claim; the two states settled for $3M CAD through direct diplomatic negotiation, never reaching the Convention's own Claims Commission mechanism." /> — a 1978 reactor crash on Canadian soil, a case of absolute liability on Earth, the easy article — remains the *only* claim the Liability Convention has ever processed, and even that claim skipped the Convention's own adjudication machinery entirely in favor of a negotiated settlement. Article III, the fault-based standard that governs every orbital collision, has never been tested even once.

That is the structural failure this piece keeps returning to: the treaty's liability chain was built for single, attributable events — one launch, one falling object, one identifiable owner. A cascade, by construction, destroys attribution. Every collision after the first mixes the evidence trail a fault-based claim depends on. The legal mechanism designed to assign responsibility gets *less* capable exactly as the physical risk gets *more* severe — which is the same shape as the physics problem underneath it, and that is not a coincidence worth glossing over.

### Article IX — "due regard" has never been to court

Article IX is the treaty's closest approach to an environmental clause, and it is also its vaguest. States "shall conduct all their activities in outer space... with due regard to the corresponding interests of all other States Parties," and shall pursue their activities "so as to avoid... harmful contamination" of space and adverse changes to Earth's environment.

<Definition client:visible term="due regard" def="A duty-of-care standard, not a defined threshold — the treaty never specifies what counts as sufficient regard, what conduct breaches it, or who adjudicates a dispute over whether it was met." /> sounds like exactly the clause a debris regime needs — a general duty not to foul the commons other states rely on. In practice it has generated no case law, no arbitration, no formal invocation over a debris-related dispute of any kind in nearly sixty years. Legal scholars treat it as a standard of conduct so open-textured it cannot itself be enforced; it functions, at best, as the moral vocabulary in which *other* instruments get justified, never as an instrument a state can actually sue under.

The instrument that tried to give Article IX teeth is UN COPUOS's 2007 Space Debris Mitigation Guidelines — seven principles (limit debris released in normal operations; minimize break-up potential during operations; limit the probability of accidental collision; avoid intentional destruction and other harmful activities; minimize post-mission break-ups from stored energy; limit a spacecraft's long-term presence in the protected LEO and GEO regions after its mission ends) that the UN General Assembly endorsed in <Annotation client:load term="Resolution 62/217" note="UN General Assembly Resolution 62/217 (22 December 2007) endorsed the COPUOS Space Debris Mitigation Guidelines and called on member states to implement them 'through relevant national mechanisms... to the greatest extent feasible' — explicitly voluntary language, not a binding obligation." /> on 22 December 2007. Read the operative language closely and the gap is right there in the text: states are called on to implement the guidelines "to the greatest extent feasible." That is not an obligation. It is an invitation, and the invitation has been open for nineteen years.

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="The regime's last binding instrument predates the smartphone">
The 1979 Moon Agreement is the most recent *binding* multilateral space treaty — and it has been ratified by only 17 states, none of them the United States, Russia, or China: the three nations that operate self-launched human spaceflight and generate the overwhelming majority of orbital debris. Every debris-specific instrument since 1979, including the 2007 COPUOS guidelines, is voluntary.
</Callout>

## The physics the treaty's authors had no reason to model

None of this would matter as urgently if orbital debris behaved the way debris on Earth does — sitting inertly until someone chooses to clean it up. It doesn't. In 1978, NASA scientists Donald Kessler and Burton Cour-Palais published the paper that gives this dynamic its name, applying the collision mathematics used to model the asteroid belt to low Earth orbit and finding that above a certain object density, collisions between existing objects would generate fragments faster than atmospheric drag could remove them — a <Annotation client:load term="self-sustaining cascade" note="Kessler & Cour-Palais (1978), JGR Space Physics 83: applying asteroid-belt collision-frequency mathematics to LEO, the paper modeled debris growth becoming self-sustaining above a critical object density, and predicted debris would overtake micrometeoroids as the dominant impact risk to spacecraft 'by about 2000.'" /> that continues even if humanity stops launching anything new. Their model predicted debris would overtake micrometeoroids as the dominant impact risk to spacecraft by roughly the year 2000.

The open question for decades was whether that critical density had actually been reached anywhere in LEO. In 2006, NASA scientists J.-C. Liou and Nicholas Johnson answered it in *Science*, and the answer was not reassuring.

<Pullquote cite="J.-C. Liou & N.L. Johnson, “Risks in Space from Orbiting Debris,” Science 311 (2006)" href="https://www.science.org/doi/10.1126/science.1121337" kind="document">
The current debris population in the LEO region has reached the point where the environment is unstable, and collisions will become the predominant mechanism for generating debris in the future.
</Pullquote>

That finding predates Fengyun-1C, Iridium-Cosmos, and Kosmos 1408 — all three events happened *after* NASA's own scientists concluded the densest shells had already crossed the line, and all three added mass to exactly the shells the paper was warning about. Kessler himself, interviewed decades after his original paper, did not soften the conclusion.

<Pullquote cite="Donald Kessler, interviewed by Aerospace America (AIAA)" href="https://aerospaceamerica.aiaa.org/a-conversation-with-donald-kessler/" kind="quote">
We're creating a planet like Saturn — and it's not going to stop.
</Pullquote>

Kessler was specifically skeptical that mitigation measures — including the deorbit rules this piece covers next — would be sufficient on their own. Asked about a 25-year deorbiting standard, his assessment was blunt: "that would slow down the process but will not eliminate it," and pressed on whether current efforts had made a dent, he said flatly, "I don't think it's even slowed it down."<Sidenote client:load href="https://aerospaceamerica.aiaa.org/a-conversation-with-donald-kessler/" source="Aerospace America">Kessler, in the same interview — a working assessment from the physicist whose 1978 model is the field's foundational reference, not a fringe claim.</Sidenote> That is the physics this treaty regime has to survive, and it is worth being precise about what "survive" would even mean: not preventing every collision, which is not on the table, but having a liability and mitigation architecture that can actually assign responsibility and force compliance once the collisions start compounding. On the current numbers, it does not have one.

Zoom into the tracked-object numbers decade by decade and the story splits into three distinct regimes, not one smooth climb — and the order they arrive in matters for how convincing "we have this under control" claims should sound.

## What's actually filling Article IX's silence

If the treaty's "due regard" clause and COPUOS's voluntary guidelines were the only things standing between today's launch rate and unmanaged chaos, the outcome would already be worse than it is. What has actually filled the gap is not international law at all — it is a patchwork of unilateral domestic rules and, increasingly, private operators managing traffic themselves because no one else will.

The clearest example is the FCC's <Annotation client:load term="5-year deorbit rule" note="FCC 22-74 (adopted 29 September 2022, effective 29 September 2024): satellites in low Earth orbit under FCC jurisdiction must be disposed of no later than five years after end of mission, replacing the prior 25-year international guideline. It binds only operators the FCC licenses — it has no treaty basis and creates no international obligation." />, adopted in September 2022 and effective two years later. It is not a treaty amendment, not a COPUOS guideline made binding — it is a purely domestic US regulation, applicable only to operators the FCC licenses, that unilaterally replaced the decades-old 25-year international norm with something five times stricter. It exists because the international guideline was voluntary and non-enforceable, and one national regulator decided that was no longer good enough for the satellites it licenses. <Annotation client:load term="ESA's own 2025 Environment Report" note="ESA Space Environment Report 2025 (data through end of 2024): roughly 90% of LEO rocket bodies now comply with the 25-year mitigation guideline, and roughly 80% comply with ESA's own stricter 5-year standard, adopted in 2023 — a second, separate unilateral tightening, distinct from the FCC's." /> shows the same pattern from a different regulator: ESA adopted its own 5-year standard in 2023, and reports roughly 80% compliance against it, versus roughly 90% against the older 25-year guideline it's gradually superseding.

Meanwhile the sheer volume of operational traffic-management activity has scaled in a way no 1967 or 1972 drafter could have imagined needing — increasingly handled by individual operators and downstream civil agencies as routine operational necessity, not as anything the treaty regime coordinates or requires.

Lewis was describing a single operator's numbers: according to SpaceX's own filings with the FCC, Starlink satellites carried out <Annotation client:load term="144,404 collision-avoidance maneuvers" note="Aerospace America (AIAA), sourcing SpaceX's biannual FCC filings: 144,404 Starlink collision-avoidance maneuvers between December 2024 and May 2025 alone — an average of roughly 804 a day — for a constellation operating at roughly one-fifth its eventual planned size (SpaceX has sought FCC approval to expand from roughly 12,000 to 42,000 satellites)." /> between December 2024 and May 2025 alone — an average of roughly 804 a day — for a constellation SpaceX intends to grow more than threefold. None of that activity is coordinated through the Outer Space Treaty regime. It is a private company, alone, deciding when to fire its own thrusters, informed by data pipelines the treaty never contemplated and reporting obligations a domestic regulator, not an international one, imposed.

NOAA's own Traffic Coordination System for Space program manager, Dmitry Poisik, described the scale of what his office now has to process: "On a bad day, it's a million predicted just in the next week."<Sidenote client:load href="https://aerospaceamerica.aiaa.org/features/heavy-traffic-ahead/" source="Aerospace America">Poisik, quoted in Aerospace America (AIAA) — a volume that exists only because tracking and constellation growth outran the treaty regime, not because of it.</Sidenote> That is a domestic US civil agency doing, at enormous and growing operational cost, the coordination work the treaty's Article IX gestures toward and never operationalized.
</Callout>

The national-security dimension compounds the same pattern rather than offsetting it. China's satellite fleet, per the Pentagon's own tracking, has grown from a small program to more than <Annotation client:load term="1,189 operational spacecraft" note="US Department of Defense, 2025 Military and Security Developments Involving the People's Republic of China (reported via SatNews): China's operational satellite fleet exceeds 1,189 spacecraft, over 510 of them ISR-capable, roughly a 927% increase since 2015." /> — a roughly 927% increase since 2015 — including over 510 satellites the Pentagon classifies as intelligence, surveillance, and reconnaissance-capable. Two in-progress Chinese megaconstellations, Thousand Sails and Guowang, together plan for something on the order of 27,000 satellites by the mid-2030s, a build-out on a scale that makes the Cipher Brief's framing of China "approaching twenty thousand" satellites within fifteen years directionally sound, even if the exact horizon compresses the underlying program disclosures somewhat. Article II's non-appropriation promise, Article IX's due-regard promise, and the COPUOS guidelines' voluntary-compliance promise all apply, in principle, equally to every one of those planned satellites — and none of the three mechanisms has any enforcement teeth to test that principle against.

<Detail summary="Go deeper: how many active satellites are there right now, precisely?">
The honest answer is a range, not a single number, and the range itself is instructive. ESA's DISCOSweb catalog lists 18,346 objects classified as "Payloads" as of 25 June 2026 — but that category includes every payload ever launched that hasn't yet reentered, active and defunct alike. Jonathan McDowell's independently maintained tracker, the source government agencies and newsrooms turn to for "how many Starlink satellites are actually up there," counted 16,234 *currently operational* payloads as of 8 July 2026. ESA's own 2025 Environment Report separately estimated active payloads at roughly 11,000 as of end-2024. All three figures are legitimate, dated, and differently scoped — the honest read is that the actively-controlled population sits somewhere in the 11,000–18,000 range depending on definition and vintage, and it has been climbing sharply through 2025–26 on the back of Starlink and its emerging competitors.
</Detail>

## So what — the mismatch, precisely stated

Line up what the four articles actually do against what the physics actually requires, and the mismatch is not vague. It is specific and mechanical.

Article II was built to stop a claim of sovereignty and has nothing to say about occupancy through accumulated junk — the exact failure mode a shell with 27,805 tracked LEO objects now exhibits. Articles VI and VII, operationalized through the Liability Convention, built a liability chain that works perfectly for a single, attributable event on Earth's surface — the one time it was tested, against Kosmos 954 in 1978 — and has never once been tested against the fault-based standard that governs everything in orbit, because a cascading field destroys the forensic attribution that standard requires before a claim can even be filed. Article IX's due-regard clause and its nearest operational descendant, the 2007 COPUOS guidelines, both use the word "voluntary" in their own defining documents, and neither has produced a single adjudicated dispute — in fifty-nine years and nineteen years, respectively.

<Callout kind="key-insight" title="The pattern across all four articles">
Every mechanism in the 1967–2007 treaty regime was built around the assumption that a space object has one identifiable owner, one identifiable location, and one identifiable cause of harm. Kessler-syndrome physics — corroborated as already active in the densest LEO shells by NASA's own 2006 analysis — breaks exactly that assumption, and breaks it faster than any diplomatic process has moved to replace the mechanisms that depend on it.
</Callout>

What has filled the resulting vacuum is not a stronger international instrument — none has been ratified since 1979, and none is close — but a scramble of domestic rules (the FCC's and ESA's competing deorbit standards), private operational discipline (Starlink alone logged 144,404 collision-avoidance maneuvers in a single six-month stretch, done because the company's own business depends on not losing satellites, not because any treaty compels it), and national security build-outs (China's roughly tenfold expansion of its on-orbit fleet since 2015) that all take place inside a legal frame none of them were designed to test. The treaty is not obsolete in the sense of being ignored — states still cite it, still ratify it, still invoke Article II reflexively whenever a lunar mining company makes headlines. It is obsolete in the narrower, more dangerous sense that its central enforcement mechanism, fault-based liability, cannot function once the debris field it is meant to adjudicate reaches the density Liou and Johnson identified twenty years ago. A regime built for eight objects in the shell that mattered most to its drafters is now asked to assign blame inside a shell nobody can fully see, filling at a rate no single actor controls, using a legal test that requires exactly the kind of clean attribution a cascade is defined by destroying.
